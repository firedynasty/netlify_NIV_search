Numbers - Matthew Henry Commentary Summaries
================================================

Chapter 1: The First Census
----------------------------------------
Henry frames the book of Numbers as establishing Israel as "a commonwealth, or rather a kingdom; for the Lord was their King." The census at Sinai served multiple purposes beyond mere enumeration.

The commission to number (vv. 1-16):
- Given in "the second year" after the Exodus, from the tabernacle
- Only males twenty years and older, "able to go forth to war"
- Each tribe had a prince assigned to assist
- The census organized by families and houses - "every man might know his relations"

Reasons for the numbering:
1. To prove the fulfillment of God's promise to Abraham - from 75 souls to 603,550 men in 215 years
2. To show God's care as the "Shepherd of Israel" who counts his flock
3. To distinguish Israelites from the "mixed multitude" - "The Lord knows those that are his"
4. To organize them for orderly march and administration

Key observations:
- Judah is the largest tribe (74,600), honored because "from that tribe Christ was to come"
- Ephraim and Manasseh numbered as separate tribes, fulfilling Jacob's blessing
- Dan (62,700) was nearly double Benjamin, though Dan had only one son entering Egypt
- "As one generation passeth away another generation cometh"

The Levites excepted (vv. 47-54):
- Not numbered with the other tribes
- Made "guardians of the spiritualities" - charged with the tabernacle
- "Those that minister about holy things should neither entangle themselves, nor be entangled, in secular affairs"
- Must pitch "round about the tabernacle, that there be no wrath upon the congregation"

Chapter 2: The Arrangement of the Camp
----------------------------------------
Henry describes the marshalling of Israel's camp - "a regular disposition... by a divine appointment."

General principles (vv. 1-2):
- All dwelt in tents: "a movable state... is not our life a warfare?"
- Those of a tribe pitched together - bonds of nature strengthening Christian communion
- "Every one must know his place and keep in it" - God quarters them
- Standards and ensigns for each tribe and family - "God is the God of order"
- The tabernacle at center, visible from all sides - "God in the heart of them"
- Yet they must pitch "afar off" in reverence (about 2000 cubits)

The four squadrons:
1. East (van): Judah (lead), Issachar, Zebulun - 186,400 men
2. South: Reuben, Simeon, Gad - 151,450 men
3. West (rear): Ephraim, Manasseh, Benjamin - 108,100 men
4. North: Dan, Asher, Naphtali - 157,600 men

Key observations:
- "God himself appointed them their place, to prevent strife and envy"
- Tribes grouped by kinship (Leah's sons, Rachel's sons, etc.)
- Judah in first position of honor - "from that tribe Christ was to come... the Lion of the tribe of Judah"
- Nahshon of Judah "is reckoned among the ancestors of Christ" (Matt. 1:4)
- Levites pitched closest to the tabernacle, within the other tribes
- Dan brought up the rear as second largest tribe

"The children of Israel observed the orders given them... without murmuring or disputing, and, as it was their safety, so it was their beauty; Balaam was charmed with the sight of it: How goodly are thy tents, O Jacob!"

Chapter 3: The Levites and the Firstborn
----------------------------------------
Henry presents the Levites in two roles: (1) assistants to the priests, and (2) substitutes for the firstborn.

Aaron's family (vv. 1-10):
- The four sons "all anointed to minister... though two were wise and two were foolish"
- Nadab and Abihu died for offering "strange fire" - "let them know that God is a jealous God"
- They "had no children" - Providence preventing their line from continuing
- Eleazar and Ithamar "ministered in the sight of Aaron" - "good for young people to act under direction"
- "The stranger that cometh nigh shall be put to death"

The Levites given to Aaron (vv. 5-9):
- "To minister to the priests in their ministration to the Lord"
- "As the deacons to the bishops in the evangelical constitution"
- Killed sacrifices, prepared incense, so priests could focus on sacred acts
- A "great trust... not only for the glory of Christ, but for the good of his church"

The exchange for the firstborn (vv. 11-13):
- At the Exodus, God claimed all firstborn as his own
- Levites substituted for firstborn - "God's institutions put no hardships upon men"
- "For us he spared not his own Son"

The three Levite families:
- Gershonites (7,500): westward, charged with curtains and hangings
- Kohathites (8,600): southward, charged with ark, table, candlestick, altars
- Merarites (6,200): northward, charged with boards, bars, pillars, sockets
- Moses and Aaron with priests: eastward

Redemption of excess firstborn (vv. 40-51):
- 22,273 firstborn versus 22,000 Levites
- The 273 excess redeemed at 5 shekels each
- "The church of the first-born... is ransomed with the precious blood of the Son of God"

"The tribe of Levi was by much the least of all the tribes... God's part in the world is too often the smallest part. His chosen are comparatively a little flock."

Chapter 4: The Levites' Service
----------------------------------------
Henry details the duties of serviceable Levites aged 30-50, "in the prime of their time for service."

The age requirements:
- Must be 30 years old to serve, 50 to retire from heavy labor
- "The service of God requires the best of our strength, and the prime of our time"
- Five years as probationers from age 25 (Num. 8:24)
- Ministers "must not be novices" and "must learn before they teach"
- Discharged at 50: "In favour to them" and "in honour to the work"
- "Those do not consider this who put off their repentance to old age"

The Kohathites' charge (vv. 4-20):
- Responsible for "the most holy things"
- Aaron and sons must first cover all holy things before Kohathites could approach
- The ark: covered with veil, badgers' skins, then blue cloth
- Table, candlestick, golden altar, brazen altar: all covered with colored cloths and badgers' skins

Purposes of the coverings:
1. Safety - protected from elements
2. Decency and ornament - blue, purple, scarlet
3. Concealment - "signified the darkness of that dispensation"

"They shall not touch any holy thing, lest they die" - Uzza's later death proved this. "That was a dispensation of terror... but now through Christ we are encouraged to come boldly to the throne of grace."

Eleazar oversaw the Kohathites - "more honour... but then he had more care."

The Gershonites' charge (vv. 21-28):
- All drapery: curtains, hangings, coverings
- Each man assigned specific items - "that every one may know his work"
- Under Ithamar's oversight

The Merarites' charge (vv. 29-33):
- Heavy carriage: boards, bars, pillars, sockets, pins, cords
- Inventory given "by name" - "to teach us with the greatest exactness to preserve pure and entire all divine institutions"

The census of serviceable Levites:
- Kohathites: 2,750 (of 8,600 total - less than a third)
- Gershonites: 2,630 (of 7,500 - about a third)
- Merarites: 3,200 (of 6,200 - more than half)
- Total: 8,580 serviceable men

Henry notes: "The greatest burden lay upon that family" (Merarites), "and God so ordered it that... they should have the most able men among them; for whatever service God calls men to he will furnish them for it."

""The death of the saints is represented as the taking down of the tabernacle... The immortal soul, like the most holy things, is first covered and taken away, carried by angels."

Chapter 5: Camp Purity, Restitution, and Jealousy
----------------------------------------
Henry presents three distinct regulations for maintaining Israel's holiness as God's dwelling place.

Removing the unclean (vv. 1-4):
- All lepers, those with issues, and defiled by the dead put outside the camp
- "The purity of the church must be as carefully consulted and preserved as the peace and order of it"
- God's tabernacle in their midst required cleanliness - "if it be defiled, he will be affronted... and provoked to withdraw"
- Signified both church discipline ("purge out scandalous persons") and final judgment ("into the new Jerusalem no unclean thing shall enter")

The law of restitution (vv. 5-10):
- Wrong against neighbor is "a trespass against the Lord, who is the protector of right"
- Three requirements: confession, sacrifice (ram of atonement), and full restitution plus one-fifth
- "While that which is got by injustice is knowingly retained... the guilt of the injustice remains upon the conscience"
- If wronged party deceased with no kinsman, restitution goes to the priest - "what is not our property will never be our profit"

The trial of jealousy (vv. 11-31):
- When husband suspects wife of adultery but lacks proof
- Adultery described as "going aside from God and virtue... committing a trespass against the husband"
- The bitter water ceremony: holy water, dust from tabernacle floor, written curses dissolved in water
- Woman must say "Amen" to the oath and drink the water
- If guilty: "her belly shall swell, and her thigh shall rot"
- If innocent: "she shall be free, and shall conceive seed"

"Secret sins are known to God... Whoremongers and adulterers God will judge. To the pure all things are pure, but to the defiled nothing is so."

Chapter 6: The Nazarite Vow and Priestly Blessing
----------------------------------------
Henry presents the Nazarite as a picture of voluntary separation unto God, followed by the divinely prescribed blessing.

The Nazarite vow (vv. 1-21):
- "A person separated unto the Lord" - either lifelong (Samson, John Baptist, Samuel) or for a set time
- Open to both men and women
- "Every true Christian is a spiritual Nazarite, separated by vow unto the Lord"

Three obligations of the Nazarite:
1. Nothing from the vine - "not so much as eat a raisin"
   - "Examples of temperance and mortification"
   - "Drinking much wine for the palate's sake... does by no means become those who profess to walk not after the flesh"
2. No cutting of hair - "a badge of subjection" and visible testimony of purity
   - "A noble neglect of the body and the ease and ornament of it"
3. No contact with dead bodies, even parents
   - "The greater profession of religion we make... the greater care we must take to avoid all sin"

If defiled accidentally (vv. 9-12):
- Must be purified, offer sin and burnt offerings
- Must start the entire vow period over - "all that were past... were lost"
- "If a righteous man turn away from his righteousness... all his righteousness that he has done shall be lost to him"

Discharge from the vow (vv. 13-21):
- Bring burnt offering, sin offering, peace offering, meat and drink offerings
- Hair burned in the fire of the peace offerings
- "Even the Nazarite... durst not appear before the holy God without a sin-offering"

The priestly blessing (vv. 22-27):
- "The Lord bless thee, and keep thee"
- "The Lord make his face shine upon thee, and be gracious unto thee"
- "The Lord lift up his countenance upon thee, and give thee peace"

Henry notes the threefold repetition of Jehovah: "The New Testament having explained it... directs us to expect the blessing from the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, the love of the Father, and the communion of the Holy Ghost."

The blessing conveys: protection ("the Lord keep thee"), pardon ("be gracious"), and peace. "They shall put my name upon the children of Israel; and I will bless them."

Chapter 7: The Princes' Offerings at Dedication
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this "the history of the additional services which Israel performed to God" - the princes bringing offerings for the tabernacle and altar.

Offerings for transport (vv. 1-9):
- Six covered wagons and twelve oxen brought by the princes
- Distributed according to need: 2 wagons to Gershonites (lighter carriage), 4 to Merarites (heavier)
- Kohathites received none - "the service of the sanctuary belonging unto them was that they should bear upon their shoulders"
- "Whatever burden God in his providence lays upon us, he will by his sufficient grace proportion the strength to it"

Offerings for the altar (vv. 10-88):
- Each of twelve princes brought identical offerings over twelve days
- Each brought: silver charger (130 shekels), silver bowl (70 shekels), golden spoon with incense
- Sacrifices: burnt offerings, sin offerings, peace offerings
- "Each prince on his day" - solemnity prolonged, equal honor to each tribe

Key observations:
- "Those that are above others in power and dignity ought to go before others... in every thing that is good"
- Judah offered first through Nahshon - "of which tribe Christ came"
- Identical offerings showed "all the tribes of Israel had an equal share in the altar"
- Even amid peace offerings, "still in the midst of their sacrifices we find a sin-offering"

Why recorded twelve times in detail:
- Encouragement to each tribe - "rich and poor meet together before God"
- "What is so given is lent to the Lord, and he carefully records it, with every one's name prefixed"
- "Even a cup of cold water shall have its reward"

Total: 12 silver chargers, 12 silver bowls (2,400 shekels silver), 12 golden spoons (120 shekels gold), plus 12 bullocks, 12 rams, 12 lambs for burnt offerings; 12 kids for sin offerings; 24 bullocks, 60 rams, 60 he-goats, 60 lambs for peace offerings.

God's acceptance (v. 89):
Moses heard God's voice "from off the mercy seat... from between the two cherubims." Henry sees this as "an earnest of the incarnation of the Son of God... when the Word should be made flesh."

Chapter 8: The Lamps and the Levites' Consecration
----------------------------------------
Henry treats both the golden lampstand and the Levites as "lights of the sanctuary."

The lamps (vv. 1-4):
- Aaron to light them so they "give light over against the candlestick"
- "The commandment is a lamp... the scripture is a light shining in a dark place"
- "The work of ministers is to light these lamps, by expounding and applying the word of God"
- The middle lamp lit from altar fire, others from it - "the fountain of all light and knowledge is in Christ"
- "They were not lighted... to burn to themselves, but to give light"

Consecration of the Levites (vv. 5-22):
1. Cleansing: sprinkled with water of purifying, shaved all flesh, washed clothes
   - "The great pains they were to take with themselves... teaches all Christians... to cleanse themselves from all filthiness of flesh and spirit"
   - "Those must be clean that bear the vessels of the Lord"

2. Presentation: brought before the Lord, Israel laid hands on them
   - Not ordination by the people, but "signified the people's parting with that tribe out of their militia"
   - "Whatever God calls for from us to serve his own glory by, we must cheerfully resign it"

3. Sacrifice: sin offering and burnt offering for atonement
   - "We are all utterly unworthy and unfit to be admitted into... the service of God, till atonement be made for sin"

4. Offering to God: presented as a "wave-offering" - "they must continually lift up themselves towards God"

5. Given to Aaron and sons: "Whatever we give up to God, he will give back to us unspeakably to our advantage"

Their purpose: "To make an atonement for the children of Israel, that there be no plague among them"

Age of service (vv. 23-26):
- Begin at 25 years old (lighter duties) or 30 (heavier carrying work)
- "Return from the warfare" at 50 - not dismissed but honored
- After 50: "minister with their brethren" as advisors and guards, "but they shall not be put upon any service which may be a fatigue to them"
- "If God's grace provide that men shall have ability according to their work, man's prudence should take care that men have work only according to their ability"

Chapter 9: The Second Passover and the Pillar of Cloud
----------------------------------------
Henry treats this chapter as containing both a vital ordinance and a vital guidance system.

The Passover at Sinai (vv. 1-14):
- Kept in the second year, on the fourteenth day of the first month
- Though passover was originally appointed for when they reached Canaan, God ordered this one "that their children might more distinctly understand the solemnity"
- "Extraordinary performances must not supersede or jostle out stated services"

The case of the defiled men (vv. 6-8):
- Some were ceremonially unclean from touching dead bodies and couldn't keep the passover
- They complained: "Wherefore are we kept back, that we may not offer an offering of the Lord?"
- "It is a blessed thing to see people hungering and thirsting after God's ordinances"
- Moses consulted God rather than deciding rashly: "I will hear what the Lord will command"

God's provision (vv. 10-14):
- The unclean or those on a journey may keep passover on the fourteenth of the second month
- All ceremonies must still be observed - "when we cannot do as we would we must do the utmost we can"
- But willful neglect brings judgment: "that man shall bear his sin"
- Strangers may also keep it - "an intimation of the favour designed for the poor Gentiles by Christ"

The pillar of cloud and fire (vv. 15-23):
- Cloud by day, fire by night - "sensible demonstrations of the constancy of his presence with them"
- Rested on the tabernacle - "God manifests his presence with his people in and by his ordinances"
- Directed all movements: "At the commandment of the Lord they rested... at the commandment of the Lord they journeyed"

Lessons from the cloud:
- "He that believeth doth not make haste. There is no time lost while we are waiting God's time"
- "It is uncomfortable staying when God has departed, but very safe and pleasant going when we see God go before us"
- The guidance signifies "the guidance of the blessed Spirit" - "all the children of God shall be led by the Spirit of God"

"Father, thy will be done; dispose of me and mine as thou pleasest; here I am, desirous to be found waiting on my God continually."

Chapter 10: The Silver Trumpets and the March from Sinai
----------------------------------------
Henry covers the last commandment from Sinai and the beginning of Israel's wilderness journey.

The silver trumpets (vv. 1-10):
- Two trumpets of beaten silver, blown only by priests
- "The Lord's ministers should lift up their voice like a trumpet, to show people their sins"

Four uses of the trumpets:
1. Calling assemblies - "public notice ought to be given of the time and place of religious assemblies"
2. Signaling camp movements - different sounds for different squadrons
3. Going to war - "signifying thereby your appeal to heaven... and your prayer to God to give you victory"
4. Solemnizing feasts - "Holy work should be done with holy joy"

The departure from Sinai (vv. 11-28):
- "On the twentieth day of the second month" the cloud was taken up
- "Mount Sinai genders to bondage... but we must not rest in it, but advance towards the joys and liberties of the children of God"
- "All our removals in this world are but from one wilderness to another"

The march order:
1. Judah's squadron (van) - "an earnest of the sceptre which in David's time should be committed to it"
2. Gershonites and Merarites with tabernacle
3. Reuben's squadron
4. Kohathites with sacred furniture - "in the midst of the camp, the safest and most honourable place"
5. Ephraim's squadron
6. Dan's squadron (rear guard) - "gathering host... gathered up all that were left behind"

Moses and Hobab (vv. 29-32):
- Moses invites his brother-in-law: "Come thou with us, and we will do thee good"
- "Those that are bound for the heavenly Canaan should invite and encourage all their friends to go along"
- Hobab initially refused, drawn by "the magnetic virtue of this earth"
- "Thou mayest be to us instead of eyes" - human help not incompatible with divine guidance

Moses's prayers at the ark (vv. 35-36):
- When ark moved: "Rise up, Lord, and let thine enemies be scattered"
- When ark rested: "Return, O Lord, unto the many thousands of Israel"
- "The welfare and happiness of the Israel of God consist in the continual presence of God among them"

Chapter 11: Murmuring, Quails, and Judgment
----------------------------------------
Henry marks this as the beginning of "a melancholy scene" - "the measures are all broken, God has turned to be their enemy."

The fire at Taberah (vv. 1-3):
- The people "complained" - "there were some secret grudgings and discontents"
- "The Lord heard it... and the fire of the Lord burnt among them"
- "God is acquainted with the secret frettings and murmurings of the heart"
- Moses prayed and "the fire was quenched" - "God delights not in punishing"
- Named Taberah ("burning") as a memorial warning

The people's lust for flesh (vv. 4-9):
- The "mixed multitude" (non-Israelites) began it - "the scabbed sheep that infected the flock"
- They romanticized Egypt: "We remember the fish... the cucumbers, and the melons, and the leeks"
- "But they do not remember the brick-kilns and the task-masters"
- They despised manna: "Our soul is dried away: there is nothing at all, beside this manna"
- "Those that might be very happy often make themselves very miserable by their discontents"

Moses's complaint (vv. 10-15):
- "I am not able to bear all this people alone, because it is too heavy for me"
- "Kill me, I pray thee, out of hand"
- Henry admits the provocation was great, but notes Moses's failures:
- Undervalued God's honor in making him leader
- Magnified his own performances
- Took too much upon himself: "Whence should I have flesh?"
- "Is this Moses? Is this the meekest of all men on the earth? The best have their infirmities"

God's double provision (vv. 16-25):
1. Seventy elders to assist Moses - "they prophesied" upon receiving the Spirit
2. Flesh for the people - but as judgment: "until it come out at your nostrils, and it be loathsome unto you"

Moses's doubt and God's rebuke (vv. 21-23):
- Moses: "Shall the flocks and the herds be slain for them, to suffice them?"
- God: "Has the Lord's hand waxed short?"
- "It is certain that God's hand is not short... with him nothing is impossible"

Eldad and Medad (vv. 26-29):
- Two elders prophesied in the camp, not at the tabernacle
- Joshua: "My lord Moses, forbid them"
- Moses: "Enviest thou for my sake? Would God that all the Lord's people were prophets"
- "We must not be forward to condemn and silence those that differ from us"

The quails and plague (vv. 31-35):
- Wind brought quails, covering the ground "two cubits high"
- The people gathered greedily - "ten homers" each
- "While the flesh was yet between their teeth... the Lord smote the people with a very great plague"
- Named Kibroth-hattaavah ("graves of lusters")
- "What we inordinately desire, if we obtain it, will be some way or other a grief and cross to us"

Chapter 12: Miriam and Aaron Against Moses
----------------------------------------
Henry shows "the best persons and families have both their follies and their crosses."

The accusation (vv. 1-2):
- Miriam and Aaron "spake against Moses" - his own siblings
- Two complaints: his Ethiopian wife and his exclusive leadership
- "Hath the Lord indeed spoken only by Moses? Hath he not spoken also by us?"
- Miriam likely instigated; Aaron was drawn in
- "Striving to be greatest is a sin which easily besets disciples themselves"

Moses's meekness (v. 3):
- "Now the man Moses was very meek, above all the men which were upon the face of the earth"
- "When God's honour was concerned... no man more zealous than Moses; but, when his own honour was touched, no man more meek"
- "He, as a deaf man, heard not" - but "the Lord heard it"
- "The more silent we are in our own cause the more is God engaged to plead it"

God's summons and rebuke (vv. 4-9):
- God called all three to the tabernacle: "Come out ye three"
- He distinguished Moses: "My servant Moses... is faithful in all mine house"
- "With him will I speak mouth to mouth... and the similitude of the Lord shall he behold"
- "Were ye not afraid to speak against my servant Moses?"
- "It is a dangerous thing to offend Christ's little ones"

Miriam's leprosy (v. 10):
- "The cloud departed... and, behold, Miriam became leprous, white as snow"
- "Her foul tongue is justly punished with a foul face"
- Aaron not struck because "God would put a difference between those that mislead and those that are misled"
- His priestly office also protected him - he must remain qualified to serve

Aaron's submission (vv. 11-12):
- "Alas, my lord, I beseech thee, lay not the sin upon us"
- "We have sinned, we have done foolishly"
- "Repentance is the unsaying of that which we have said amiss"

Moses's intercession (v. 13):
- "Heal her now, O God, I beseech thee"
- "By this he made it to appear that he did heartily forgive her"
- "We are taught to pray for those that despitefully use us"
- "This pattern of Moses, and that of our Saviour, Father, forgive them, we must study to conform to"

The seven days' exclusion (vv. 14-15):
- "If her father had but spit in her face, should she not be ashamed seven days?"
- Miriam shut out from the camp; the people did not journey until she was restored
- "Those that are under censure and rebuke for sin ought to be treated with a great deal of tenderness"
- "Sinners must be cast out with grief, and penitents taken in with joy""

Chapter 13: The Twelve Spies
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this "a memorable and very melancholy story" - Israel's turning back at the border of Canaan, referred to in Hebrews 3 as warning to Christians.

The commission to spy (vv. 1-16):
- God permitted what the people requested out of unbelief (Deut. 1:22)
- "They would not take God's word that it was a good land"
- "How absurd was it for them to send to spy out a land which God himself had spied out for them"
- "Thus we ruin ourselves by giving more credit to the reports and representations of sense than to divine revelation"
- One ruler from each tribe sent - "persons of figure" whose evil report carried more weight

Moses renames Oshea to Joshua:
- Oshea means "Save thou" (a prayer)
- Joshua means "He will save" (a promise)
- "So near is the relation between prayers and promises"
- "Jesus is the same name with Joshua" - he was "a type of Christ as successor to Moses, Israel's captain, and conqueror of Canaan"

The instructions (vv. 17-20):
- Assess the land: good or bad, fat or lean
- Assess the inhabitants: strong or weak, few or many, fortified or open
- "Be ye of good courage" - bring back fruit as proof
- Moses "was well satisfied that Canaan was a very good land, but he sent these spies to bring an account of it for the satisfaction of the people"

The survey (vv. 21-25):
- They traveled the whole land, from south to north
- Visited Hebron, where the patriarchs were buried
- Found the sons of Anak (giants) - "Where they expected the greatest encouragements they met with the greatest discouragements"
- The famous cluster of grapes from Eshcol - "foretastes of the fulness of joy we expect in the heavenly Canaan. We may see by them what heaven is"

The report (vv. 26-33):
- Ten spies: "The land floweth with milk and honey... Nevertheless the people be strong"
- They contradicted themselves: first calling it fruitful, then "a land that eateth up the inhabitants"
- "We were in our own sight as grasshoppers, and so we were in their sight"
- They concluded: "We be not able to go up against the people"

Henry's rebuke of their cowardice:
- 600,000 men was "as formidable an army as perhaps was ever brought into the field"
- The Canaanites were dispersed and divided
- They had God's presence, past deliverance from Egypt, and specific promises
- "To say, We are not able to go up against them, was in effect to say, God himself is not able to make his words good"
- "They despised the land, they believed not his word"

Caleb's faith (v. 30):
- "Caleb stilled the people... Caleb signifies all heart"
- "Let us go up at once, and possess it; for we are well able to overcome it"
- "He does not say, Let us go up and conquer it; he looks upon that to be as good as done already"
- "The righteous are bold as a lion. Difficulties that lie in the way of salvation dwindle and vanish before a lively active faith"
- "All things are possible, if they be but promised, to him that believes"

Chapter 14: Israel's Rebellion and the Forty-Year Sentence
----------------------------------------
Henry describes this as "that fatal quarrel between God and Israel upon which, for their murmuring and unbelief, he swore in his wrath that they should not enter into his rest."

The people's despair (vv. 1-4):
- "They lifted up their voices and cried... and wept that night"
- "Unbelief, or distrust of God, is a sin that is its own punishment. Those that do not trust God are continually vexing themselves"
- "Would God that we had died in Egypt! or in this wilderness!"
- "See the prodigious madness of unbridled passions, which make men prodigal even of that which nature accounts most dear, life itself"
- They blasphemed God as intending their ruin: "They, in effect, charge that God who is love itself with the worst of malice"
- "Let us make a captain, and return to Egypt" - choosing bondage over conquest

Henry's analysis of their folly:
- "Better be as they were than go back to Egypt"
- "Had they so soon forgotten the sore bondage they were in there?"
- "This transcribes the folly of Israel, who, when they were within a step of Canaan, would make a captain, and return to Egypt"

Moses, Aaron, Caleb, and Joshua plead (vv. 5-10):
- Moses and Aaron fell on their faces
- Joshua and Caleb rent their clothes: "The land is an exceeding good land"
- "If the Lord delight in us... Only rebel not ye against the Lord, neither fear ye the people"
- "They are bread for us: their defence is departed from them, and the Lord is with us"
- The people responded: "All the congregation bade stone them with stones"
- "The glory of the Lord appeared" to save them

God's threatened sentence (vv. 11-12):
- "How long will this people provoke me? and how long will it be ere they believe me?"
- "I will smite them with the pestilence, and disinherit them"
- "I will make of thee a greater nation" - a test of Moses's character
- "Distrust of God, of his power and promise, is itself a very great provocation"

Moses's intercession (vv. 13-19):
- Pleaded God's glory: "Then the Egyptians shall hear it... Because the Lord was not able to bring this people into the land"
- "The best pleas in prayer are those that are taken from God's honour"
- Quoted God's proclamation of his name: "The Lord is longsuffering, and of great mercy"
- "Pardon, I beseech thee, the iniquity of this people according unto the greatness of thy mercy"
- "Here he was a type of Christ, who interceded for his persecutors"

God's answer (vv. 20-35):
- "I have pardoned according to thy word" - but with consequences
- "All the earth shall be filled with the glory of the Lord"
- They had "tempted me now these ten times" - God counts our provocations
- Sentence: "Your carcases shall fall in this wilderness... forty years"
- A day for a year: "After the number of the days in which ye searched the land, even forty days, each day for a year"
- "You shall know my breach of promise" - they broke covenant first

Caleb's reward (v. 24):
- "My servant Caleb, because he had another spirit... and hath followed me fully, him will I bring into the land"
- "Another spirit" - "different from the rest of the spies"
- To follow God fully means "universally, without dividing—uprightly, without dissembling—cheerfully, without disputing—and constantly, without declining"
- "The heavenly Canaan shall be the everlasting inheritance of those that follow the Lord fully"

The children's exception (v. 31):
- "Your little ones... them will I bring in"
- "They had invidiously charged God with a design to ruin their children"
- "God can put a difference between the guilty and the innocent"

The ten spies' death (vv. 36-38):
- "Died by the plague before the Lord"
- Joshua and Caleb "lived still" - preserved though surrounded by the dying
- "Those greatly provoke God who misrepresent religion... and raise prejudices in men's minds against it"

The presumptuous attempt to invade (vv. 39-45):
- After hearing the sentence, they "mourned greatly" and then "rose up early" to invade
- "We have sinned... Lo, we be here, and will go up"
- Moses warned: "Go not up, for the Lord is not among you"
- "They presumed to go up unto the hill top" without Moses or the ark
- Defeated by the Amalekites and Canaanites
- "That affair can never end well that begins with sin"
- "God bade them go, and they would not; he forbade them, and they would. Thus is the carnal mind enmity to God"

Chapter 15: Offerings, Presumptuous Sins, and Fringes
----------------------------------------
Henry notes this chapter "comes in between the story of two rebellions" - showing that God continued giving laws despite their sin, "typical of the gifts which Christ was to receive even for the rebellious."

Laws for offerings in Canaan (vv. 1-16):
- The encouraging premise: "When ye be come into the land... which I give unto you"
- "This was a plain intimation... that God was reconciled to them notwithstanding the sentence"
- Meat offerings and drink offerings to accompany sacrifices
- Proportions: larger offerings required larger accompaniments
- "Our religious services should be governed... by the rule of proportion"

One law for native and stranger (vv. 14-16):
- "One ordinance shall be both for you... and for the stranger"
- "An invitation to the Gentiles to become proselytes"
- "A mortification to the pride of the Jews"
- "A happy presage of the calling of the Gentiles"
- "If the law made so little difference between Jew and Gentile, much less would the gospel"

The heave offering of dough (vv. 17-21):
- First of their dough given to the Lord
- "They must own their dependence upon God for their daily bread"
- "Give us this day our daily bread" - not yearly harvest but daily provision
- "When God has had his dues out of our estates, we may expect the comfort of what falls to our share"

Sins of ignorance (vv. 22-29):
- Provision for national sins "committed by ignorance" - ceremonial neglect over time
- "The ceremonial observances were so numerous... that, it might easily be supposed, some of them by degrees would be forgotten"
- Forgiveness available through prescribed sacrifice
- "Sins committed ignorantly need to have atonement made for them; for, though ignorance will in a degree excuse, it will not justify"
- "David prayed to be cleansed from his secret faults"
- Christ's prayer: "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do"

Presumptuous sins (vv. 30-31):
- "The soul that doeth ought presumptuously... reproacheth the Lord"
- "That soul shall utterly be cut off"
- Sins "with a high hand" - those who "avowedly confront God's authority"
- "It is not only to sin against knowledge, but to sin designedly against God's will and glory"
- "He despises the word of the Lord" - thinking himself "too great, too good, and too wise, to be ruled by it"
- "There remains no sacrifice for those sins" under the law

The sabbath-breaker executed (vv. 32-36):
- A man "found gathering sticks upon the sabbath day"
- "This seemed but a small offence, but it was a violation of the law of the sabbath"
- God's verdict: "The man shall be surely put to death... stoned with stones"
- "God is jealous for the honour of his sabbaths"
- "One would think there could be no great harm in gathering a few sticks, on what day soever it was"
- But this was presumptuous: "a standing warning to us all, to make conscience of keeping holy the sabbath"

The law of fringes (vv. 37-41):
- Blue ribbon on the borders of garments
- "To stir up their pure minds by way of remembrance"
- "If they were tempted to sin, the fringe would be a monitor"
- "Our Saviour, being made under the law, wore these fringes"
- Purpose: "That ye seek not after your own heart and your own eyes, after which ye use to go a whoring"
- The chapter closes with the fundamental law: "Be holy unto your God"
- "I am the Lord your God, which brought you out of the land of Egypt"

Chapter 16: Korah's Rebellion
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this as perhaps the most dramatic rebellion - "a daring and dangerous rebellion raised against Moses and Aaron."

The conspirators (vv. 1-3):
- Korah (Levite, cousin to Moses) - "the ring-leader"
- Dathan and Abiram (Reubenites) - likely envious that Judah led the march
- On - mentioned once, then disappears (perhaps repented)
- 250 princes, "men of renown" - "famous men... became infamous"
- "The pride, ambition, and emulation, of great men, have always been the occasion of a great deal of mischief"

Their complaint:
- "Ye take too much upon you... all the congregation are holy, every one of them"
- "Small reason they had to boast of the people's purity" given recent rebellions
- "They would either have no priests at all... or they would not acquiesce in that constitution of the government which God had appointed"
- "See here what spirit levellers are of"

Moses's response (vv. 4-11):
- "He fell on his face" - humbling himself, seeking God's counsel
- Proposed a test: "Even to morrow the Lord will show who are his"
- Confronted Korah: "Seemeth it but a small thing unto you, that the God of Israel hath separated you... to do the service of the tabernacle?"
- "It will help to keep us from envying those that are above us duly to consider how many there are below us"
- "Seek ye the priesthood also?" - he saw their true aim
- "You are gathered together against the Lord... What is Aaron, that ye murmur against him?"

Dathan and Abiram's defiance (vv. 12-15):
- "We will not come up" - refused Moses's summons
- Called Egypt "a land that floweth with milk and honey" - the very description of Canaan
- Accused Moses of wanting "to make thyself altogether a prince over us"
- "Thou hast not brought us into a land that floweth with milk and honey" - blaming Moses for their own unbelief
- Moses "was very wroth" and prayed: "Respect not thou their offering: I have not taken one ass from them"

The assembly and God's appearance (vv. 16-22):
- Korah and the 250 princes appeared with censers
- "Korah gathered all the congregation against them" - confident of victory
- "The glory of the Lord appeared unto all the congregation"
- God commanded: "Separate yourselves from among this congregation, that I may consume them in a moment"
- Moses and Aaron interceded: "O God, the God of the spirits of all flesh, shall one man sin, and wilt thou be wroth with all the congregation?"

The warning and the judgment (vv. 23-34):
- Moses warned: "Depart, I pray you, from the tents of these wicked men, and touch nothing of theirs"
- "Those that would not perish with sinners must come out from among them"
- The congregation withdrew, but Dathan and Abiram "stood in the door of their tents" with their families
- Moses's solemn appeal: "If these men die the common death of all men... then the Lord hath not sent me"
- "But if the Lord make a new thing, and the earth open her mouth, and swallow them up"
- "The ground clave asunder... and the earth opened her mouth, and swallowed them up"
- "They went down alive into the pit, and the earth closed upon them"
- "God created a new thing" - unprecedented judgment

The nature of this judgment:
- "Unparalleled" - God's arsenal is not limited
- "Very terrible" - to "go down alive into their own graves"
- "Altogether miraculous" - "cleaving of the earth was as wonderful as the cleaving of the sea"
- "Typical of the eternal ruin of sinners who die impenitent"
- Israel fled "at the cry of them... Lest the earth swallow us up also"

Fire upon the 250 (vv. 35-40):
- "There came out a fire from the Lord, and consumed the two hundred and fifty men that offered incense"
- Aaron preserved alive among them
- "Our God is a consuming fire"
- "It is at our peril if we meddle with that which does not belong to us"
- Their censers beaten into plates covering the altar - "a memorial unto the children of Israel, that no stranger... come near to offer incense"

The next day's rebellion (vv. 41-50):
- "On the morrow all the congregation... murmured against Moses and Aaron, saying, Ye have killed the people of the Lord"
- "Could any thing have been said more unjustly and maliciously?"
- "The continued obstinacy of this people... shows how necessary the grace of God is to the effectual change of men's hearts"
- A plague began; God again threatened to "consume them in a moment"
- Moses directed Aaron: "Take a censer... and make an atonement for them"
- "Aaron ran into the midst of the congregation... and he stood between the dead and the living; and the plague was stayed"
- 14,700 died - in addition to those swallowed and burned

Aaron as intercessor:
- "A very good man" who "had a true love for the children of his people, though they hated and envied him"
- "A very bold man" - venturing among an enraged mob and deadly plague
- "A man of God... his call to the priesthood was hereby abundantly confirmed"
- "A type of Christ, who came into the world to make an atonement for sin... and who, by his mediation and intercession, stands between the living and the dead"

""Those that quarrel with less judgments prepare greater for themselves; for when God judges he will overcome."

Chapter 17: Aaron's Rod That Budded
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this chapter as God's gracious confirmation of Aaron's priesthood - "not a miracle of wrath, as before, but of grace."

The test proposed (vv. 1-7):
- After Korah's rebellion, the other tribes began to murmur: "If the head of a tribe must be a priest, why not the head of some other tribe?"
- God "searches the heart" and knew this thought; he anticipated the rebellion to prevent bloodshed
- "It seems then the priesthood was a preferment worth seeking and striving for, even by the princes of the tribes"
- Twelve rods to be placed before the ark: one for each tribe, with Aaron's representing Levi
- "Writing is often a good preservative against strife, for what is written may be appealed to"
- "I will make to cease from me the murmurings of the children of Israel"
- This sign was "to them, as Christ said the sign of the prophet Jonas... should be to the men of that generation"

The miracle (vv. 8-9):
- Aaron's rod "budded, and brought forth buds, and bloomed blossoms, and yielded almonds"
- All three stages visible at once - "this was miraculous, and took away all suspicion of a fraud"
- "Fruitfulness is the best evidence of a divine call"
- "The plants of God's setting, and the boughs cut off from them, will flourish"

Typology of the budding rod:
1. The priesthood would be "fruitful and serviceable to the church of God" - Christ ordained ministers "that they should go and bring forth fruit"
2. "There should be a succession of priests" - "buds and blossoms promising more hereafter"
3. "This priesthood should not be perpetual" - the almond tree's early flowering a sign of old age; "the Mosaic priesthood... soon became old and ready to vanish away"
4. A type of Christ: "He is the man, the branch, that is to be a priest upon his throne"

The rod preserved (vv. 10-11):
- Kept "before the testimony, in perpetuam rei memoriam"
- "It is probable that the buds, and blossoms, and fruit, continued fresh"
- Preserved with the tables of the law and the pot of manna - "to show to after-ages how the ancient church was taught, and fed, and ruled"
- "Such is the difference between the sacraments which Christ has appointed for edification and the relics which men have devised for superstition"

The people's response (vv. 12-13):
- "Behold, we die, we perish, we all perish. Shall we be consumed with dying?"
- Henry offers two interpretations:
  1. Repining: "As if God was a hard Master, that sought advantage against them"
  2. Repenting: "We submit to the divine will... we will not contend any more"
- "When God judges he will overcome, and... will oblige the most obstinate gainsayers to confess their folly"

Chapter 18: Duties and Provisions of Priests and Levites
----------------------------------------
Henry explains this as God's response to the people's fear (17:12-13) and as balance to Aaron's newly confirmed honors.

The burden of the priesthood (vv. 1-7):
- Aaron and sons "shall bear the iniquity of the sanctuary" - responsible if profaned
- "The greater the trust... the greater is our danger of contracting guilt"
- "This is a good reason why we should neither be envious at others' honours nor ambitious ourselves of high places"
- "Who would covet the care of souls who considers the account that must be given of that care?"

The duty of service:
- "You shall serve" - not "You shall rule"
- "The priesthood is a service. If any desire the office of a bishop he desires a good work"
- "Ministers must remember that they are ministers, that is, servants"
- Levites "joined" to Aaron - an allusion to the name Levi which means "joined"
- "We are to value it as a great gift of the divine bounty to have those joined to us that will be helpful and serviceable to us in the service of God"

Priestly provision (vv. 8-19):
- Share of sacrifices: "God's work is its own wages, and his service carries its recompence along with it"
- "Even in keeping God's commandments there is great reward. The present pleasures of religion are part of its pay"
- Redemption money for firstborn; firstfruits of oil, wine, wheat
- "The best of the oil, and the best of the wine" - "We must always serve and honour God with the best we have"
- Given "by reason of the anointing" - not personal merit but office
- "A covenant of salt for ever" - permanent provision for permanent ministry
- "Lo, I am with you always, even to the end of the world"

No inheritance in the land (vv. 20-24):
- "I am thy part, and thy inheritance"
- "Those that have God for their inheritance... ought to look with a holy contempt and indifference upon the inheritances of this world"
- Yet they lived "better than any other tribe" - "Providence has various ways of supporting those that live in a dependence upon it"
- Each order kept in its sphere: "If ministers expect that people should keep in their sphere... let them keep in theirs"

The Levites' tithe and the priests' tithe (vv. 25-32):
- Levites received the tithe of all Israel
- From their tithe, they must give a tenth to the priests - "a heave-offering to the Lord"
- "Those that are employed to assist the devotions of others must be sure to pay their own"
- After giving God's portion: "You shall eat the rest... with the same freedom that the other Israelites eat"
- "The way to have the comfort of all our worldly possessions" - first earn honestly, then give God his dues

Chapter 19: The Red Heifer and Water of Purification
----------------------------------------
Henry notes this chapter's connection to 17:13 - providing purification so the people could safely approach the tabernacle.

The preparation of the ashes (vv. 1-10):
- A red heifer "without spot, wherein is no blemish, and upon which never came yoke"
- "Typical of the death and sufferings of Christ, by which he intended, not only to satisfy God's justice, but to purify and pacify our consciences"
- Red color: "Christ, as man, was the Son of Adam, red earth, and we find him red in his apparel"
- No yoke: "The voluntary offer of the Lord Jesus... He was bound and held with no other cords than those of his own love"

The burning ceremony:
- Eleazar (not Aaron) performed it - the high priest must not be defiled
- Slain outside the camp - "our Lord Jesus, being made sin and a curse for us, suffered without the gate"
- Blood sprinkled seven times toward the sanctuary - "signified the satisfaction that was made to God by the death of Christ"
- Cedar wood, hyssop, and scarlet cast into the fire - used also for cleansing lepers
- Ashes gathered and stored "for the congregation of the children of Israel"

The significance of the ashes:
- "One heifer were sufficient to season as many vessels of water as the people of Israel would need for many ages"
- "There is virtue enough in the blood of Christ for all that repent and believe... for the sins of the whole world"
- "No bodily substance is so incorruptible as ashes" - "a very fit emblem of the everlasting efficacy of the sacrifice of Christ"
- "The blood of Christ is laid up for us in the word and sacraments, as an inexhaustible fountain of merit"

Uncleanness from death (vv. 11-16):
- Touching a dead body, a bone, a grave, or entering a tent with a dead body caused defilement for seven days
- "This was one of the greatest burdens of the ceremonial law"
- "Death is the wages of sin, entered into the world by it, and reigns by the power of it"
- "The law could not conquer death... as the gospel does by bringing life and immortality to light"
- Now through Christ: "O grave! where is thy victory? Where is thy pollution?"

Application of the purification water (vv. 17-22):
- Ashes mixed with running water: "The ashes signified the merit of Christ, so the running water signified the power and grace of the blessed Spirit"
- "Those that promise themselves benefit by the righteousness of Christ, while they submit not to the grace and influence of the Spirit, do but deceive themselves"
- Sprinkled with hyssop: "Faith is the bunch of hyssop wherewith the conscience is sprinkled and the heart purified"
- Applied on the third and seventh days: "We need to do that often which is so necessary to be well done"
- Neglect brought severe judgment: "It is a dangerous thing to contemn divine institutions, though they may seem minute"
- "It is not the wound that is fatal, so much as the contempt of the remedy"

Chapter 20: Meribah, Edom, and Aaron's Death
----------------------------------------
Henry marks this as the beginning of "the fortieth year (which was the last year) of the Israelites' wandering."

The death of Miriam (v. 1):
- "A prophetess, and had been an instrument of much good to Israel"
- "Yet she had once been a murmurer, and must not enter Canaan"

The second Meribah (vv. 2-13):
- Water shortage at Kadesh; the people "chid with Moses" again
- "Would God that we had died when our brethren died before the Lord!"
- "They need not wish that they had died with their brethren, they are here taking the ready way to die like their brethren"
- Aggravations of their sin:
  1. They had suffered for their fathers' discontent for nearly forty years
  2. They had constant experience of God's goodness
  3. Miriam had just died - they should have been more respectful to remaining leaders

Moses and Aaron's retreat to the tabernacle:
- "There they lay waiting for orders: Speak, Lord, for thy servants hear"
- God appeared "on his throne of glory, to silence their unjust murmuring" and "on his throne of grace, to satisfy their just desires"
- Commanded: "Speak ye unto the rock... and it shall give forth his water"

Moses and Aaron's sin (vv. 10-12):
- "Hear now, ye rebels; must we fetch you water out of this rock?"
- Moses "smote the rock twice" instead of speaking to it
- God's rebuke: "Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel"
- Their fault was "complicated":
  1. Did not follow orders - smote instead of spoke
  2. Assumed too much glory: "Must we fetch water?" - "They did not sanctify God"
  3. Unbelief: "You believed me not" - doubted the command would work
  4. Passion: "They provoked his spirit, so that he spoke unadvisedly with his lips"
  5. Public example: "Before the eyes of the children of Israel, to whom they should have been examples of faith"

Lessons from Moses's sin:
- "The best of men have their failings, even in those graces that they are most eminent for"
- "The man Moses was very meek, and yet here he sinned in passion"
- "God judges not as man judges concerning sins"
- "The nearer any are to him the more offensive are their sins"
- "When our heart is hot within us, we are concerned to take heed that we offend not with our tongue"
- Moses's impartiality: "He himself left this upon record concerning himself"

The Edomites' refusal (vv. 14-21):
- Moses sent ambassadors: "Thus saith thy brother Israel"
- Reviewed their history: Egyptian oppression, God's deliverance
- Requested passage: "We will go by the king's high way, we will not turn to the right hand nor to the left"
- "Though God himself, in the pillar of cloud and fire, was Israel's guide... yet God would have this respect paid to the Edomites"
- "Dominion is founded in providence, not in grace"
- Edom refused: "The old enmity which Esau bore to Israel"
- "Israel turned away from him" - "We must not think it strange if the most reasonable requests be denied by unreasonable men"

The death of Aaron (vv. 22-29):
- "God bids Aaron die" - gathered to his people, not cut off
- Something of displeasure: "Aaron must not enter Canaan, because he had failed in his duty"
- Much of mercy: "Dies with ease and in honour... in the arms of divine grace"
- "Aaron must not enter Canaan, to show that the Levitical priesthood could make nothing perfect"

The transfer of the priesthood:
- Moses stripped Aaron's garments and placed them on Eleazar
- "Death will strip us; naked we came into the world, and naked we must go out"
- "A great comfort to Moses... to see that it should be kept up in a succession"
- "A great satisfaction to Aaron to see his son, who was dear to him, thus preferred, and his office... thus preserved"
- A type of Christ: "His everlasting priesthood, in which alone his would be perpetuated"

Aaron's death and mourning:
- "Quickly after he was stripped of his priestly garments, he laid himself down and died contentedly"
- "A good man would desire, if it were the will of God, not to outlive his usefulness"
- All Israel mourned thirty days: "While he lived, they were murmuring at him... but now that he was dead they mourned for him"
- "Many good men have had more honour done to their memories than ever they had to their persons""

Chapter 21: Victory, Serpents, and Conquests
----------------------------------------
Henry marks this as Israel's emergence from the wilderness, entering "a land inhabited" and beginning their campaign of conquest.

The defeat of Arad (vv. 1-3):
- Arad attacked first, taking some Israelites prisoner
- "He meddled to his own hurt; had he sat still, his people might have been last destroyed"
- Israel vowed to devote the spoils to God if granted victory
- God gave victory; the place named Hormah (destruction)
- "When we are desiring and expecting mercy from God we should bind our souls with a bond that we will faithfully do our duty to him"

The fiery serpents (vv. 4-9):
- The people "discouraged because of the way" around Edom
- Complained against God and Moses; loathed "this light bread" (manna)
- "Those that are disposed to quarrel will find fault where there is no fault to be found"
- God sent fiery serpents: "Justly are those made to feel God's judgments that are not thankful for his mercies"
- The serpents called "fiery" from their color, rage, or the burning fever they caused
- "They had unjustly complained for want of water; to chastise them for which God sends upon them this thirst, which no water would quench"

The people's repentance:
- "We have sinned, for we have spoken against the Lord, and against thee"
- Moses prayed for them - "a type of Christ, who interceded for his persecutors"
- "Afflictions often change men's sentiments concerning God's people"

The brazen serpent - Christ typified (vv. 8-9):
- God ordered Moses to make a brass serpent on a pole
- "Our Saviour has told us so (John iii. 14, 15), that as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness so the Son of man must be lifted up"

The parallels with Christ:
1. The disease: "The devil is the old serpent, a fiery serpent... Sin is the biting of this fiery serpent"
2. The remedy: "God himself has found the ransom... a very unlikely method of cure"
3. The likeness: "Christ, though perfectly free from sin himself, yet was made in the likeness of sinful flesh"
4. The lifting up: "He was lifted up upon the cross... lifted up by the preaching of the gospel"
5. The application: "They looked and lived, and we, if we believe, shall not perish; it is by faith that we look unto Jesus"

Key application:
- "If sinners either despise Christ's righteousness or despair of benefit by it their wound will, without doubt, be fatal"
- "Whoever looked up to this healing sign, though from the outmost part of the camp, though with a weak and weeping eye, was certainly healed"
- "There are weak brethren for whom Christ died"

The well at Beer (vv. 16-18):
- God provided water "before they prayed" - "anticipated them with the blessings of his goodness"
- Israel sang: "Spring up, O well!" - "With joy must we draw water out of the wells of salvation"
- "As the brazen serpent was a figure of Christ... so is this well a figure of the Spirit"
- The princes dug with their staves: "God's favours must be expected in the use of such means as lie within our power"

Victory over Sihon and Og (vv. 21-35):
- Israel sent a peaceable message; Sihon refused and attacked
- "God hardened his spirit and made his heart obstinate, that he might deliver him into the hand of Israel"
- "The enemies of God's church are often infatuated in those very counsels which they think most wisely taken"
- God's providence in territorial arrangements: "He set the bounds of the people according to the number of the children of Israel"
- Og of Bashan also attacked: "Fear him not: for I have delivered him into thy hand"
- "Giants are but worms before God's power"

Chapter 22: Balak and Balaam
----------------------------------------
Henry introduces "the famous story of Balak and Balaam" - God's people told to "remember what Balak the king of Moab consulted, and what Balaam the son of Beor answered him, that they might know the righteousness of the Lord" (Micah 6:5).

Balak's fear and scheme (vv. 1-7):
- Moab "sore afraid" though Israel had no intention of attacking them
- "It is common for those that design mischief to pretend that mischief is designed against them"
- They imagined Israel would "lick up all that are round about us, as the ox licketh up the grass"
- "Thus were the wicked in great fear where no fear was"
- Balak trusted "more to his arts than to his arms" - sought to engage God against Israel
- Sent for Balaam from Mesopotamia: "He whom thou blessest is blessed, and he whom thou cursest is cursed"
- "The rewards of divination in their hand" - "the wages of unrighteousness"

Balaam's character:
- "Had been a great prophet... but, growing proud and covetous, God departed from him"
- Then "betook himself to diabolical arts" to maintain his reputation
- "The love of money is the root of all evil"

God's first prohibition (vv. 8-14):
- Balaam lodged the messengers to consult God - "When we enter into a parley with temptations we are in great danger of being overcome"
- God's clear command: "Thou shalt not go with them; thou shalt not curse the people: for they are blessed"
- Balaam's unfaithful report: "The Lord refuseth to give me leave to go with you" - not mentioning Israel was blessed
- "Those are a fair mark for Satan's temptation that speak diminishingly of divine prohibitions"

The second embassy (vv. 15-21):
- "More, and more honourable" messengers sent
- Promises of "very great honour" and blank check: "I will do whatsoever thou sayest"
- Balaam's noble words but corrupt heart: "If Balak would give me his house full of silver and gold, I cannot go beyond the word of the Lord my God"
- "It is an easy thing for bad men to speak very good words... God knows the heart"
- He hoped God might "alter his mind" - "a vile reflection upon God Almighty"
- "It is a very great affront to God... to beg leave to sin"

God's permissive will:
- "Since thou hast such a mind to go, even go" - "God gave him up to his own heart's lust"
- "God has wicked men in a chain; hitherto they shall come by his permission, but no further"
- "As God sometimes denies the prayers of his people in love, so sometimes he grants the desires of the wicked in wrath"

The angel and the donkey (vv. 22-35):
- "God's anger was kindled because he went"
- "The sin of sinners is not to be thought the less provoking to God because he permits it"
- The angel stood as adversary: "The holy angels are adversaries to sin"
- "Those are really our best friends... that stop our progress in a sinful way"

The donkey's wisdom:
- The donkey saw the angel when Balaam could not: "How vainly did Balaam boast that he was a man whose eyes were open"
- "The ass knows his owner, sees his danger, but Balaam does not know, does not consider"
- Three times the donkey saved Balaam; three times he beat her
- "Those who by wilful sin are running headlong into perdition are angry at those that would prevent their ruin"

The donkey speaks:
- "This was a great miracle, quite above the power of nature"
- "The devil, when he tempted our first parents to sin, employed a subtle serpent, but God, when he would convince Balaam, employed a silly ass"
- "Christ has chosen the foolish things of the world to confound the wise"
- The donkey's arguments: her faithful service, her unusual behavior
- "The rare occurrence of an offence should moderate our displeasure against an offender"

Balaam confronted:
- "Thy way is perverse before me"
- "When our eyes are opened we shall see what danger we are in in a sinful way"
- Balaam's confession: "I have sinned" - but "no sign that his heart is turned"
- "Many leave their sins only because their sins have left them"

Meeting Balak (vv. 36-41):
- Balak: "Am not I able indeed to promote thee to honour?"
- "Promotion to honour is a very tempting bait to many people"
- Balaam: "Have I now any power at all to say any thing?"
- Taken to "the high places of Baal" to view Israel
- "Let us not... be led into temptation"

Chapter 23: The First Two Blessings
----------------------------------------
Henry observes that while Balak and Balaam schemed, "neither Moses nor the elders of Israel know any thing of the matter... but God, who keeps Israel, and neither slumbers nor sleeps, baffles the attempt."

The elaborate preparations (vv. 1-6):
- Seven altars, seven bullocks, seven rams
- "Thus they liked not to retain God in their knowledge, but became vain in their imaginations"
- Balaam retired to seek enchantments: "Solitude gives a good opportunity for communion with God"
- But he went with only "a peradventure" - "conscious to himself of guilt"
- He boasted of his sacrifices: "The acts of devotion which are done in hypocrisy are commonly reflected upon with pride"

The first blessing (vv. 7-10):
- "How shall I curse, whom God hath not cursed?"
- His confession of impotence: "The weakness and folly" of divination against God's people
- "The dominion of the divine power... the inviolable security of the people of God"

Israel's distinctiveness:
- "The people shall dwell alone, and shall not be reckoned among the nations"
- "It is the duty and honour of those that are dedicated to God to be separated from the world"
- "Those who make conscience of peculiar duties may take the comfort of peculiar privileges"

Israel's multitude:
- "Who can count the dust of Jacob?" - fulfilling the promise to Abraham
- Balak wanted Balaam exasperated by their numbers; instead he admired them
- "The better acquainted we are with God's people the better opinion we have of them"

The death of the righteous:
- "Let me die the death of the righteous, and let my last end be like his!"
- Evidence of belief in the soul's immortality and "a different state on the other side death"
- "Many desire to die the death of the righteous, but do not endeavour to live the life of the righteous"
- "They would be saints in heaven, but not saints on earth"
- "This is the desire of the slothful, which kills him, because his hands refuse to labour"

Balak's frustration (vv. 11-12):
- "What hast thou done unto me!"
- "Sometimes God makes the enemies of his church a vexation one to another"
- Balaam: "Must I not take heed to speak that which the Lord has put in my mouth?"

The second attempt (vv. 13-26):
- Change of location: Balak hoped a partial view might enable partial cursing
- "See how restless and unwearied the church's enemies are in their malicious attempts"
- Same sacrifices repeated
- Balak asked: "What hath the Lord spoken?" - "This should be our enquiry when we come to hear the word of God"

The second blessing - God's unchangeableness:
- "Rise up, Balak, and hear" - even a king must attend God's message with reverence
- "God is not a man, that he should lie; neither the son of man, that he should repent"
- "He never changes his mind, and therefore never recalls his promise"
- "It is impossible for God to lie... with God there is no variableness nor shadow of turning"
- "He hath blessed and I cannot reverse it"

Israel unblamable:
- "He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob, neither hath he seen perverseness in Israel"
- Not that there was no sin, but no idolatry or such sin as would provoke abandonment
- "While we keep from sin we keep from harm"
- Alternative reading: God "will not see any grievance done to Israel" - he protects them

Israel's irresistible power:
- "The Lord his God is with him... the shout of a king is among them"
- "God brought them out of Egypt; he hath as it were the strength of an unicorn"
- "Such is the strength which the God of Israel gives unto his people"

No enchantment against Jacob:
- "Surely there is no enchantment against Jacob, neither is there any divination against Israel"
- "The curses of hell can never take place against the blessings of heaven"
- "According to this time it shall be said... What hath God wrought!"
- "The defeating of the designs of the church's enemies ought to be had in everlasting remembrance"

The lion imagery:
- "The people shall rise up as a great lion... shall not lie down until he eat of the prey"
- Points to Israel's coming victories over Canaan

Balak's desperation:
- "Neither curse them at all, nor bless them at all" - would settle for silence
- "God can make those that depart from him weary of the multitude of their counsels"
- Yet they persist: a third attempt from Mount Peor, Baal's high place
- "Because sinners are borne with... their hearts are the more fully set in them to do evil"

Chapter 24: The Star Out of Jacob
----------------------------------------
Henry presents the conclusion of "the defeat of the counsels of Balak and Balaam against Israel, not by might, nor by power, but by the Spirit of the Lord."

The changed method (vv. 1-4):
- Balaam "went not, as at other times, to seek for enchantments"
- "Sooner or later God will convince men of their folly in seeking after lying vanities"
- "The Spirit of God came upon him" - as upon Saul to prevent harm to David
- His boast: he "heard the words of God" and "saw the vision of the Almighty"
- Yet "many have their eyes open that have not their hearts open, are enlightened, but not sanctified"
- "That knowledge which puffs men up with pride will but serve to light them to hell"

The third blessing - Israel's beauty (vv. 5-9):
- "How goodly are thy tents, O Jacob!"
- Their beauty was in "their admirable order, according to their tribes"
- "Nothing recommends religion more to the good opinion of those that look upon it at a distance than the unity and harmony of its professors"
- Compared to fruitful valleys, fine gardens, fragrant spices
- "The righteous, doubtless, is more excellent than his neighbour"

Israel's fruitfulness:
- "He shall pour the water out of his buckets, and his seed shall be in many waters"
- Speaks to their wonderful increase as a nation

Israel's honor:
- "His king shall be higher than Agag" - the most potent monarch then known
- Fulfilled in Joshua, then Saul's triumph over Agag

Israel's power:
- "God brought him forth out of Egypt; he hath as it were the strength of an unicorn"
- "He shall eat up the nations his enemies"
- Looking back: their deliverance from Egypt
- Looking forward: their coming conquests

Israel's security:
- "He lay down as a lion, as a great lion: who shall stir him up?"
- Lions "do not retire into places of shelter to sleep, but lie down any where, knowing that none dares meddle with them"
- "The righteous bold as a lion... God maketh them to dwell in safety"

The blessing and curse:
- "Blessed is he that blesseth thee, and cursed is he that curseth thee"
- Confirms the blessing of Abraham (Genesis 12:3)
- Balaam himself blessed Israel because he "desired to share in the blessing of Israel's friends and dreaded the curse on Israel's enemies"

Balak's fury (vv. 10-14):
- "Smote his hands together" in indignation
- "The Lord hath kept thee back from honour" - blamed God for Balaam's "failure"
- "Those who are any way losers by their duty are commonly upbraided with it, as fools"
- Balaam reminded him of his warnings: "I cannot go beyond the commandment of the Lord"

Balaam's evil counsel:
- "I will advertise thee what this people shall do to thy people"
- He could not curse them openly but counseled Balak "to entice the Israelites to idolatry"
- "Since he could not have leave from God to curse them, he puts him in a way of getting help from the devil to tempt them"

The prophecy of the Star (vv. 15-19):
- "I shall see him, but not now: I shall behold him, but not nigh"
- "There shall come a Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre shall rise out of Israel"

The dual fulfillment:
1. David in the type: "should smite the corners of Moab" and conquer Edom (2 Samuel 8)
2. Christ in the antitype: "the promised Messiah... chiefly pointed at"

The Star prophecy and the Magi:
- "Perhaps this prophecy of Balaam (one of the children of the east)... gave occasion to the wise men... upon the sight of an unusual star over the land of Judea, to enquire for him that was born king of the Jews"

Christ's universal dominion:
- "Shall destroy, or, as some read it, shall rule over, all the children of Seth"
- "All the children of men, who descend from Seth"
- "Christ shall be king, not only of Jacob and Israel, but of all the world"
- "He shall set up a universal rule, authority, and power"

Israel's spiritual warfare:
- "His Israel shall do valiantly"
- "The people that do know their God shall be strong, and do exploits"

Prophecy concerning Amalek (v. 20):
- "Amalek was the first of the nations; but his latter end shall be that he perish for ever"
- Confirms God's perpetual war against Amalek
- "Those whom God is at war with will certainly perish for ever"

Prophecy concerning the Kenites (vv. 21-22):
- "Strong is thy dwellingplace, and thou puttest thy nest in a rock"
- "Yet the Kenites shall be wasted"
- "A nest in a rock will be no perpetual security"

Prophecy concerning distant nations (vv. 23-24):
- "Alas, who shall live when God doeth this!"
- "He acknowledges all the revolutions of states and kingdoms to be the Lord's doing"
- Ships from Chittim (Greece/Rome) shall afflict Asshur and Eber
- Fulfilled in Alexander's conquests, and Rome's destruction of Jerusalem
- "But Chittim... shall itself perish for ever, when the stone cut out of the mountain without hands shall consume all these kingdoms"

Conclusion:
- "Balaam, instead of cursing the church, curses Amalek the first, and Rome the last, enemy of the church"
- "And so let all thy enemies perish, O Lord!"

Chapter 25: The Sin at Baal-Peor
----------------------------------------
Henry shows how Balaam's counsel succeeded where his curses failed - "None are more fatally bewitched than those that are bewitched by their own lusts."

Balaam's effective strategy:
- Since God would not curse Israel, Balaam advised Balak to tempt them to ruin themselves
- "Those are our worst enemies that draw us to sin, for that is the greatest mischief any man can do us"
- "If Balak had drawn out his armed men against them... Israel had bravely resisted"
- "But now that he sends his beautiful women among them... the Israelites basely yield"
- "We are more endangered by the charms of a smiling world than by the terrors of a frowning world"

The sin of Israel (vv. 1-3):
- Whoredom with the daughters of Moab led to idolatry
- "Ever since Eve was first in the transgression the fairer sex... has been a snare to many"
- "Those that have broken the fences of modesty will never be held by the bonds of piety"
- They ate the sacrifices offered to Baal-peor - "eating the sacrifices of the dead" (Psalm 106:28)
- Aggravation: they did this when "ready to enter and take possession" of Canaan
- "The highest degree of treachery and ingratitude to be false to their God"

God's judgment:
- "Israel's whoredoms did that which all Balaam's enchantments could not do"
- A plague broke out; 24,000 died
- "Epidemical diseases are the fruits of God's anger, and the just punishments of epidemical sins"
- "If any man defile the temple of God, him shall God destroy"
- Ringleaders ordered hanged "before the sun" - "Ringleaders in sin ought to be made examples of justice"

Zimri's brazen sin (vv. 6-8):
- A prince of Simeon brought a Midianite princess publicly into camp
- "In the sight of Moses, and in the sight of all the congregation... who were weeping before the door of the tabernacle"
- "He declared his sin as Sodom... so far from blushing for it that he rather prided himself in it"
- Double affront: to the nation's justice and to its religion
- Came while the congregation was "weeping between the porch and the altar, to turn away the wrath of God"

Phinehas's zeal (vv. 7-13):
- "Never was virtue more daring than it was in Phinehas"
- He rose from prayer, took a javelin, and executed both sinners
- "It is not at all difficult to justify Phinehas... he was one of those judges of Israel whom Moses had ordered... to slay all those whom they knew to have joined themselves to Baal-peor"
- "This gives no countenance at all to private persons, under pretence of zeal against sin, to put offenders to death"
- "The civil magistrate is the avenger"

God's commendation of Phinehas:
- The plague was stayed: "If magistrates do not take care to punish sin, God will"
- "He has turned away my wrath from the children of Israel"
- "There is nothing lost by venturing for God"
- God gave him "my covenant of peace" - the priesthood confirmed to his line
- "It is requisite that ministers should be not only for God, but zealous for God"

War against Midian commanded (vv. 16-18):
- "Vex the Midianites, and smite them: For they vex you with their wiles"
- "We must set ourselves against that, whatever it is, which is an occasion of sin to us"
- "Whatever draws us to sin should be a vexation to us, as a thorn in the flesh"
- "God will certainly reckon with those that do the devil's work in tempting men to sin"

Chapter 26: The Second Census
----------------------------------------
Henry notes this chapter gives the book its name - "Numbers" - as it records the second numbering of Israel.

Purpose of the census (vv. 1-4):
- Taken "presently after the plague" - showing "though God had in justice contended with them... yet he had not made a full end"
- "God's Israel shall not be ruined, though it be severely rebuked"
- Eleazar now joined with Moses (Aaron having died)
- David later numbered without command "and paid dearly for it"
- This census done by divine order, for entering Canaan

Observations on the tribes:
- Total: 601,730 (1,820 fewer than at Sinai)
- Seven tribes increased; five decreased
- Judah, Issachar, Zebulun all increased - "all three tribes under the standard of Judah, who was the ancestor of Christ"
- "His church shall be edified and multiplied"

Notable changes:
- Manasseh increased most (from smallest to considerable) - "when the Lord saw that Manasseh was despised, he thus multiplied him... for it is his glory to help the weakest"
- Simeon decreased most dramatically: from 59,300 to 22,200 - "little more than a third"
- Likely many of the 24,000 plague victims were Simeonites, since their prince Zimri led the sin
- Hence "Simeon is not mentioned in Moses's blessing" (Deuteronomy 33)

The memorial of Korah's rebellion (vv. 9-11):
- Dathan and Abiram mentioned: they "might have been advanced in due time under God and Moses"
- But "their ambitious spirits put them upon striving against God and Moses"
- "Those that might have been famous were made infamous: they became a sign"
- "God, in their ruin, showed himself glorious in holiness"
- Yet "the children of Korah died not" - they refused to join their father's rebellion
- "If we partake not of the sins of sinners, we shall not partake of their plagues"
- Their posterity became Levitical singers: "perhaps they were made to bear his name... for warning to themselves"

The land allocation (vv. 52-56):
- "To many thou shalt give more, and to few thou shalt give less"
- Yet "the matter must be finally reserved to the providence of their God" - by lot
- "The God of nations... reserves it to himself to appoint the bounds of our habitation"
- Type of Christ: "It is not mine to give. But it shall be given to those for whom it is prepared of my Father"

The fulfillment of judgment (vv. 63-65):
- "Not one of those who were numbered... should enter Canaan, except Caleb and Joshua"
- "Better all those carcasses... should fall to the ground, than the word of God"
- God's goodness: "Though that murmuring race was cut off, yet God raised up another generation"
- God's truth: "The arrows of death... are directed to the mark intended, and no other"
- "Thousands may fall on their right hand, and ten thousands on their left, but they shall escape"

Chapter 27: Zelophehad's Daughters and Joshua's Commission
----------------------------------------
Henry presents two important decisions: one about inheritance, one about leadership.

Zelophehad's daughters (vv. 1-11):
- Their father died "in his own sin" (not in Korah's rebellion)
- They petition: "Why should the name of our father be done away... Give unto us therefore a possession"
- Their virtues displayed:
  1. "A strong faith in the power and promise of God" - Canaan was "yet unconquered, untouched"
  2. "An earnest desire of a place and name in the land of promise, which was a type of heaven"
  3. "A true respect and honour for their father, whose name was dear and precious to them"
- "It is the duty of magistrates to defend the fatherless"

The divine ruling:
- Moses "brought their cause before the Lord" - not presuming to decide alone
- God: "The daughters of Zelophehad speak right"
- "Those that seek an inheritance in the land of promise shall have what they seek"
- A general law established: daughters inherit if no sons
- In the heavenly inheritance "no difference is made between male and female"

Moses's death announced (vv. 12-14):
- "Get thee up into this mount Abarim, and see the land... then thou also shalt be gathered unto thy people"
- His sin remembered: "Ye rebelled against my commandment... to sanctify me at the water"
- "God will show his displeasure against sin, even in those that are nearest and dearest to him"
- "Those that are in reputation for wisdom and honour have need to be constantly careful"

The sweetening of the sentence:
- He shall see Canaan first - "his sight wonderfully strengthened"
- "This sight of Canaan signified his believing prospect of the better country, that is, the heavenly"
- Death only "gathers him to his people" - Abraham, Isaac, Jacob
- "As Aaron died before him" - Moses had seen Aaron "put off the priesthood first and then the body" cheerfully

Moses's prayer for a successor (vv. 15-17):
- "Envious spirits do not love their successors, but Moses was not one of these"
- "We should concern ourselves, both in our prayers and in our endeavours, for the rising generation"
- "That the congregation of the Lord be not as sheep which have no shepherd"
- He prays to "the God of the spirits of all flesh" - "He is both the former and the searcher of spirits"
- "Before God gave this blessing to Israel, he stirred up Moses to pray for it"

Joshua appointed (vv. 18-23):
- "A man in whom is the Spirit" - grace, government, conduct, courage, prophecy
- Moses to lay hands on him: "transferring the government... also in token of God's conferring the blessing"
- "This rite of imposing hands we find used in the New Testament in the setting apart of gospel ministers"
- Moses must "put some of his honour upon him" - admit him as assistant while still living
- Joshua must "stand before Eleazar" and consult the Urim
- "Though he was full of the Spirit... yet he must do nothing without asking counsel of God"

Moses's selflessness:
- He "cheerfully ordained Joshua" though "a present lessening to himself"
- "His own children had no preferment at all, but were left in the rank of common Levites"
- "Such an instance of self-denial and submission to the will of God"
- "This shows him to have had a principle which raised him above all other lawgivers"
- "Hereby it appeared that Moses acted not from himself, because he acted not for himself"

Chapter 28: The Laws of Sacrifice Renewed
----------------------------------------
Henry notes the surprising placement: with war imminent, God restates worship laws.

Why repeat these laws? (vv. 1-2):
- "Now, as they were on the point of entering Canaan, they should be sure to take their religion along with them"
- "Inter arma silent leges - law is little regarded amidst the clash of arms. No, says God, my bread for my sacrifices even now shall you observe"
- "They were peculiarly concerned to keep their peace with God when they were at war with their enemies"
- A new generation needed instruction
- "Now... they would have plenty of all good things. 'Now' (says God), 'When you are feasting yourselves, forget not to offer the bread of your God'"

The daily sacrifice (vv. 3-8):
- A lamb morning and evening - "a continual burnt-offering"
- "When we are bidden to pray always, and to pray without ceasing, it is intended that at least every morning and every evening we offer up our solemn prayers"
- The drink-offering to be "strong wine" - "the richest and most generous"
- "Though it was to be poured out upon the altar... yet God requires the strongest, to teach us to serve God with the best we have"
- "A figure of the blood of Christ... and of the blood of the martyrs"

The sabbath sacrifice (vv. 9-10):
- The daily offering doubled on the sabbath
- "This teaches us to double our devotions on sabbath days, for so the duty of the day requires"
- "The sabbath rest is to be observed, in order to a more close application to the sabbath work"
- Ezekiel's vision shows even greater sabbath offerings - "the advancement of sabbath sanctification in the days of the Messiah"
- "Every sabbath day's work in its day, studying to redeem every minute of sabbath time"

The new moon sacrifice (vv. 11-15):
- Substantial offerings: two bullocks, a ram, seven lambs, plus a sin-offering
- "When we give glory to God by confessing his mercies, we must give glory to him likewise by confessing our own sins"
- "When we rejoice in the gifts of common providence, we must make the sacrifice of Christ... the fountain and spring-head of our joy"

The Passover offerings (vv. 16-25):
- Seven days of unleavened bread following the paschal lamb
- First and last days as sabbaths with holy convocations
- "A gospel conversation, in gratitude for Christ our passover who was sacrificed, is called the keeping of this feast"
- "It is not enough that we purge out the leavened bread of malice and wickedness, but we must offer the bread of our God"

The Feast of Firstfruits/Pentecost (vv. 26-31):
- "The day of the firstfruits" - thanksgiving at harvest's end
- "It was at this feast that the Spirit was poured out (Acts 2:1), and thousands were converted... to be a kind of first-fruits of his creatures"
- "When God sows plentifully upon us he expects to reap accordingly from us"
- Note on the offerings:
  - No peace-offerings specified here (those were for the offerers' benefit)
  - Burnt-offerings "purely for the honour of God... typified evangelical piety"
  - Sin-offerings "typical of Christ's sacrifice of himself, by which we and our services are perfected and sanctified"

Chapter 29: The Solemnities of the Seventh Month
----------------------------------------
Henry notes the seventh month had more sacred solemnities than any other - partly because it was the original first month, and partly because it was "the time of vacation between harvest and seedtime, when they had most leisure to attend the sanctuary."

The Feast of Trumpets (vv. 1-6):
- First day of the seventh month - a day of blowing trumpets
- "A preparative for the two great solemnities of holy mourning on the day of atonement and of holy joy in the feast of tabernacles"
- "The intention of divine institutions is well answered when one religious service helps to fit us for another and all for heaven"
- The sacrifices were in addition to both the daily and new moon offerings
- "We must not seek occasions to abate our zeal in God's service"
- "If we perform family-worship, we must not think that this will excuse us from our secret devotions"
- "We should always abound in the work of the Lord"

The Day of Atonement (vv. 7-11):
- Besides the special services of Leviticus 16, additional burnt-offerings
- "In our faith and repentance... we must have an eye to the glory and honour of God"
- A sin-offering "besides the great sin-offering of atonement"
- "There are so many defects and faults, even in the exercises and expressions of our repentance, that we have need of an interest in a sacrifice to expiate the guilt even of that part of our holy things"
- "Though we must not repent that we have repented, yet we must repent that we have not repented better"
- "What the law could not do, in that it was weak, that Christ has done"

The Feast of Tabernacles (vv. 12-38):
- "Soon after the day of atonement... followed the feast of tabernacles"
- "Those that sow in tears shall soon reap in joy"
- Seven days of dwelling in booths

Eight key observations:
1. "Their days of rejoicing were to be days of sacrifices" - cheerfulness that fits us for duty
2. "While we are here in a tabernacle-state, it is our interest as well as duty constantly to keep up communion with God"
3. Detailed repetition for each day "that they might be very exact in those observances"
4. "The repetition of the same services, if performed with an upright heart... is no weariness to God"

The decreasing bullocks:
- Thirteen on day one, twelve on day two, down to seven on day seven
- "God herein considered the infirmity of the flesh, which is apt to grudge the charge and expense of religion"
- Or: "The legal dispensation should wax old, and vanish away at last; and the multitude of their sacrifices should end in one great sacrifice"
- "It was on the last day of the feast... that our Lord Jesus stood and cried to those who still thirsted after righteousness... to come unto him and drink" (John 7:37)

5. "The meat-offerings and drink-offerings attended all the sacrifices... We must not think that doing much in religion will be accepted if we do not do it well, and after the manner that God has appointed"
6. "Every day there must be a sin-offering... Our burnt-offerings of praise cannot be accepted of God unless we have an interest in the great sacrifice of propitiation"
7. "The continual burnt-offering must not be omitted either morning or evening... No extraordinary services should jostle out our stated devotions"
8. "Besides these, particular persons were to glorify God with their vows and their free-will offerings"
- "Our ministers' praying with us and for us will not excuse us from praying for ourselves"

Chapter 30: The Law of Vows
----------------------------------------
Henry expounds the laws governing vows, especially concerning women under authority.

General principle (vv. 1-2):
- "If a man vow a vow unto the Lord... he shall not break his word"
- "He shall not profane his word. Vowing is an ordinance of God; if we vow in hypocrisy we profane that ordinance"
- "Better not vow than vow and not pay"
- "Be not deceived, God is not mocked"
- "His promises to us are yea and amen, let not ours to him be yea and nay"

Nature of vows:
- Matter must be lawful and not already required by divine precept
- Made in "extraordinary heat of holy zeal, in humiliation for some sin committed... in pursuit of some mercy desired or in gratitude for some mercy received"
- "Vows are the hedge of separation, that is, a fence to religion"
- "A promise to man is a bond upon the estate, but a promise to God is a bond upon the soul"
- "Our sacramental vows... are bonds upon the soul, and by them we must feel ourselves bound out from all sin and bound up to the whole will of God"

Daughters in their father's house (vv. 3-5):
- Her vow "is nugatory or in suspense till her father knows it"
- Father's silence ratifies: "Silence gives consent"
- Father's disallowance nullifies: "The Lord shall forgive her"
- "This shows how great a deference children owe to their parents"
- "It is for the interest of the public that the paternal authority be supported"
- Application to marriage without consent: "It proves the sinfulness of it, and obliges the children that have thus done foolishly to repent"

Wives under their husbands (vv. 6-16):
- Widows and divorced women: "whatever vows she binds her soul with, they shall stand against her"
- Married women: cannot vow "without that [husband's allowance]"
- Husband's silence confirms; husband's disallowance cancels
- "Her obligation to her husband shall take place of it, for to him she ought to be in subjection as unto the Lord"
- "It would be her sin to disobey her husband, whose consent perhaps she ought to have asked before she made the vow"

If husband disallows wrongly:
- "He shall bear her iniquity" - "if the thing she had vowed was really good... and the husband disallowed it out of covetousness, or humour, or to show his authority"
- "Though she be discharged from the obligation of her vow, yet he will have a great deal to answer for"

The divine wisdom in this law:
- "How carefully the divine law consults the good order of families"
- "It is fit that every man should bear rule in his own house"
- "God himself would quit his right, and release the obligations even of a solemn vow"
- "So much does religion strengthen the ties of all relations, and secure the welfare of all societies"

Chapter 31: The Holy War Against Midian
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this chapter part of "the book of the wars of the Lord."

The divine command (vv. 1-2):
- "Avenge the children of Israel of the Midianites: afterward shalt thou be gathered unto thy people"
- Why Midian and not Moab? "The Midianites' wiles were Balaam's projects" - they were the tempters
- "God will have us to reckon those our worst enemies that draw us to sin"
- "On them we should avenge ourselves, not only make no league with them, but make war upon them by living a life of mortification"
- "The deceived and the deceiver are his" (Job 12:16)
- Judgment on Israel's sin already executed; now judgment on the tempters
- "Israel's quarrel with Amalek, that fought against them, was not avenged till long after: but their quarrel with Midian, that debauched them, was speedily avenged"

Moses's involvement:
- "God would have it done by Moses, in his life-time, that he who had so deeply resented that injury might have the satisfaction of seeing it avenged"
- "God sometimes removes useful men when we think they can be ill spared; but... they are never removed till they have done the work which was appointed them"

The army (vv. 3-6):
- Only 12,000 men - "God would teach them that it is all one to him to save by many or by few"
- Moses says "avenge the Lord of Midian" - "God said, Avenge Israel; Moses says, Avenge the Lord"
- "The interests of God and Israel are united, and the cause of both is one"
- Phinehas sent with the holy instruments and trumpets
- He had "signalized himself for his zeal against the Midianites" in slaying Cozbi
- "Thou hast been faithful over a few things, I will make thee ruler over many things"

The victory (vv. 7-12):
- All males slain; five kings of Midian killed
- Balaam slain: "God's overruling providence brought him thither, and there his just vengeance found him"
- "Had he himself believed what he said of the happy state of Israel, he would not have herded thus with the enemies of Israel"
- "Justly does he die the death of the wicked (though he pretended to desire that of the righteous)"
- "Now was his folly made manifest to all men, who foretold the fate of others, but foresaw not his own"

Moses's rebuke (vv. 13-18):
- "Have ye saved all the women alive?" - the very ones who caused Israel's trespass
- "These caused the children of Israel... to commit trespass against the Lord in the matter of Peor"
- The women must die: "It is dangerous to let them live; they will be still tempting the Israelites"
- Only young girls spared

Purification required (vv. 19-24):
- Seven days outside the camp for those who had killed or touched dead bodies
- "God would preserve in their minds a dread and detestation of murder"
- "David must not build the temple because he had been a man of war"
- Spoils purified: "What would bear the fire must pass through the fire"
- "To us now every thing is sanctified by the word and prayer... To the pure all things are pure"

Distribution of spoil (vv. 25-47):
- Half to soldiers, half to congregation
- "Those that tarried at home should divide the spoil" - they helped by prayers
- But soldiers got equal share for 12,000 as congregation got for 600,000+
- "The greater pains we take, and the greater hazards we run, in the service of God... the greater will our recompence be"

God's tribute:
- From soldiers' share: 1 in 500 to priests
- From people's share: 1 in 50 to Levites
- "Whatever we have, God must have his dues out of it"
- "The less opportunity we have of honouring God with our personal services the more it is expected we should honour him with our substance"

The officers' freewill offering (vv. 48-54):
- "There lacketh not one man of us" - not a single casualty
- "This was very extraordinary, and perhaps cannot be paralleled in any history"
- "Of all that were given them they had lost none; so precious also is the blood of Christ's subjects and soldiers to him"
- They brought an oblation "to make an atonement for our souls"
- "Instead of coming to Moses to demand a recompence... or to set up trophies of their victory... they bring an oblation"
- "Being conscious to themselves, as the best men must be even in their best services, that they had been defective in their duty"
- "There is not a just man upon the earth that doeth good and sinneth not"

Chapter 32: The Petition of Reuben and Gad
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this as a case study in negotiation, potential misunderstanding, and wise resolution.

The request (vv. 1-5):
- Reuben and Gad had "a very great multitude of cattle"
- The conquered land "was a place for cattle"
- "Let this land be given unto thy servants for a possession, and bring us not over Jordan"

Analysis of their motives:
- "The lust of the eye and the pride of life" (1 John 2:16)
- "Many seek their own things more than the things of Jesus Christ"
- "Many are influenced by their secular interest and advantage to take up short of the heavenly Canaan"
- "Their spirits agree too well with this world... and they say, 'It is good to be here'"
- "Lot thus chose by the sight of the eye, and smarted for his choice"
- "Would we choose our portion aright we must look above the things that are seen"
- Reuben's "pride of life": lost birthright, but "catches at the first lot, though it was out of Canaan"

Moses's misunderstanding and rebuke (vv. 6-15):
- "Shall your brethren go to war, and shall ye sit here?"
- "It ill becomes any of God's Israel to sit down unconcerned in the difficult and perilous concernments of their brethren"
- He reminds them of the spies' disastrous precedent
- "You have risen up in your fathers' stead... an increase of sinful men"
- "It is sad to see the rising generation... not only no better, but worse than that which went before it"
- "If men did but consider as they ought, what would be the end of sin, they would be afraid of the beginnings of it"

Their clarification (vv. 16-19):
- They propose: build cities for families, then go armed before Israel
- "We will not return unto our houses, until the children of Israel have inherited every man his inheritance"
- "It is an instance of the good effect of plain dealing; Moses, by showing them their sin... brought them to their duty without murmuring"

Moses's conditions (vv. 20-27):
- "You shall go armed before the Lord" - not just before brethren
- "It is God's cause more than your brethren's"
- If they keep their word: "You shall be guiltless before the Lord and before Israel"
- "Whatever possessions we have, it is desirable thus to come guiltless to them"

The solemn warning:
- "If ye will not do so, behold, ye have sinned against the Lord: and be sure your sin will find you out"
- "Sin will, without doubt, find out the sinner sooner or later"
- "It concerns us therefore to find our sins out, that we may repent of them and forsake them, lest our sins find us out to our ruin"

The agreement (vv. 28-42):
- Moses charges Eleazar and Joshua to enforce the terms
- Half-tribe of Manasseh joins: children of Machir "had taken Gilead and dispossessed the Amorites"
- "Let them win it and wear it, get it and take it"
- They rebuilt cities and changed idolatrous names (Nebo, Baal-meon)
- "God promises to take away the names of Baalim out of the mouths of his people" (Hosea 2:17)

Providence's balance:
- "As these tribes were now first placed before the other tribes, so, long afterwards, they were displaced before the other tribes"
- They were "carried captive into Assyria some years before the other tribes" (2 Kings 15:29)
- "Such a proportion does Providence sometimes observe in balancing prosperity and adversity; he sets the one over-against the other"

Chapter 33: The Journeys of Israel Reviewed
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this chapter as looking back on the wilderness march and forward to Canaan's settlement.

The journal kept (vv. 1-2):
- "Moses wrote their goings out according to their journeys by the commandment of the Lord"
- God ordered him to keep "a journal or diary, and to insert in it all the remarkable occurrences of their way"
- "It may be of good use to private Christians, but especially to those in public stations, to preserve in writing an account of the providences of God concerning them"
- "Our memories are deceitful and need this help, that we may remember all the way which the Lord our God has led us"

The departure from Egypt (vv. 3-4):
- They went forth "with their armies, rank and file, as an army with banners"
- "Under the hand of Moses and Aaron, their guides, overseers, and rulers, under God"
- "With a high hand, because God's hand was high that wrought for them"
- "They did not steal away clandestinely... but in defiance of their enemies"
- While Egyptians buried their firstborn: "They would have God's first-born buried alive, but God set them a burying their own first-born"
- "On their gods also the Lord executed judgments" - idols broken

Observations on the wilderness journey:
1. "They were continually upon the remove... Such is our state in this world; we have here no continuing city"
2. "Most of their way lay through a wilderness" - yet God sustained them forty years
3. "By less difficulties God prepares his people for greater"
4. "Our removals in this world are but from one wilderness to another"
5. "They were led to and fro, forward and backward, as in a maze or labyrinth"
6. "He led them about... and yet led them the right way"
7. "The way which God takes in bringing his people to himself is always the best way, though it does not always seem to us the nearest way"

Notable places:
- "Kibroth-hattaavah - the graves of lusts"
- "Abel-shittim" - from "Abel" meaning mourning, named for Israel's grief over the Peor sin

The command concerning Canaan (vv. 50-56):
- "Utterly to destroy all remnants of idolatry"
- "Destroy all their pictures and images, and quite pluck down all their high places"
- Not preserved "as monuments of antiquity... nor as ornaments of their houses, nor toys for their children"
- "In token of their abhorrence and detestation of idolatry and to prevent their being tempted"

The promise and warning:
- If obedient: "God would by degrees put them in full possession"
- "Learn not their way, and then fear not their power"
- If disobedient: "They would foster snakes in their own bosoms"
- The remnant would be "pricks in their eyes and thorns in their sides"
- "We must expect trouble and affliction from that, whatever it is, which we sinfully indulge"
- Ultimate judgment: "I shall do to you as I thought to do unto them"
- "If we do not drive sin out, sin will drive us out; if we be not the death of our lusts, our lusts will be the death of our souls"

Chapter 34: The Boundaries of Canaan
----------------------------------------
Henry notes God settles boundaries "not as a geographer in his map, merely to please the curious, but as a prince in his grant."

The limited territory:
- God "appoints the bounds of our habitation" (Acts 17:26)
- Two purposes: to know whom to dispossess, and to know their own portion
- "God would not have his people to enlarge their desire of worldly possessions"
- "God sets bounds to our lot; let us then set bounds to our desires, and bring our mind to our condition"

The small compass:
- "About 160 miles in length and about fifty in breadth"
- "Perhaps did not contain more than half as much ground as England"
- "This was that little spot of ground in which only, for many ages, God was known"
- "The narrowness of the extent was abundantly compensated by the extraordinary fruitfulness of the soil"

Spiritual observations:
1. "How small a part of the world God has for himself... few have the knowledge of him"
2. "How small a share of the world God often gives to his own people"
3. "Those that have their portion in heaven have reason to be content with a small pittance of this earth"
4. "A little that a righteous man has... is far better and more comfortable than the riches of many wicked"

Significance of the borders:
- Canaan was pleasant "yet it bordered upon wilderness and seas"
- "The vineyard of the church is compassed on all hands with the desert of this world"
- Many borders were "natural fortifications... the God of nature was their protector"
- Border reached Egypt: reminder of "their bondage there, and their wonderful deliverance"
- The Salt Sea (Dead Sea): "a constant warning to them to take heed of those sins which had been the ruin of Sodom"
- The Great Sea (Mediterranean): some think they had dominion of it by this grant

The commissioners (vv. 16-29):
- Eleazar and Joshua to preside - "typifying Christ, who, as priest and king, divides the heavenly Canaan"
- One prince from each tribe: "Public affairs should be so managed as not only to give their right to all, but... to give satisfaction to all"
- The tribes listed in order of their future geography: "an evidence that Moses was guided by a divine Spirit"
- "Known unto God are all his works beforehand"

Chapter 35: Cities for Levites and Refuge
----------------------------------------
Henry finds "a great deal of good law, and pure gospel" in this chapter.

The Levitical cities (vv. 1-8):
- Forty-eight cities with suburbs allotted to Levites
- "They needed not to sow, nor reap, nor gather into barns, for their heavenly Father fed them with the tithe"
- Purpose: "That they might the more closely attend to the study of the law"
- "They were not fed thus easily that they might live in idleness, but that they might give themselves wholly to the business of their profession"

Why cities:
- "That they might live near together, and converse with one another about the law"
- "In doubtful cases they might consult one another"
- Suburbs for cattle: "That they should not only live, but live plentifully"

Each tribe gives cities:
1. "That each tribe might thus make a grateful acknowledgment to God out of their real as well as out of their personal estates"
2. "That each tribe might have the benefit of the Levites' dwelling among them, to teach them the good knowledge of the Lord"
- Jacob's curse on Levi "was turned into a blessing"
- "It is a great mercy to a country to be replenished in all parts with faithful ministers"

The law of murder (vv. 9-34):
- "Wilful murder should be punished with death, and in that case no sanctuary should be allowed"
- Based on Genesis 9:6: "Whoso sheds man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed"
- "Blood defiles not only the conscience of the murderer... but also the land in which it is shed"
- "The land cannot be cleansed from the blood of the murdered, but by the blood of the murderer"
- "No satisfaction should be taken for the life of a murderer" - "The redemption of the life is so precious"
- Two witnesses required for capital punishment
- The avenger of blood (next of kin) could execute evident murderers

Manslaughter and cities of refuge:
- "If the homicide was not voluntary, nor done designedly... without enmity, or lying in wait"
- "Our law calls chance-medley, or homicide per infortunium - through misfortune"
- The manslayer safe in city of refuge till trial
- If accidental: remain banished till high priest's death
- If he leaves the city: "he put himself out of the protection of the law"

Purposes of this law:
1. "Men ought not to suffer for that which is rather their unhappiness than their crime"
2. Yet banishment teaches "a dread and horror of the guilt of blood"
3. Release at high priest's death: "an honour was put upon that sacred office"
4. "How can we expect to be saved if we neglect the salvation!"

Gospel typology of cities of refuge:
- Alluded to in Hebrews 6:18: "fleeing for refuge to the hope set before us"
- "Though there is but one Christ appointed for our refuge, yet, wherever we are, he is a refuge at hand"
- "Believers that flee to him, and rest in him, are protected from the wrath of God and the curse of the law"
- "There is no condemnation to those that are in Christ Jesus"
- Cities were Levites' cities: "It is the work of gospel ministers to bid poor sinners welcome to Christ"
- "Even strangers and sojourners... might take the benefit" - "In Christ Jesus no difference is made between Greek and Jew"
- "Even the suburbs or borders of the city were a sufficient security" - "virtue even in the hem of Christ's garment"
- "The protection... was not owing to the strength of its walls... but purely to the divine appointment"
- "Those that are in Christ must abide in Christ... Drawing back is to perdition"

Chapter 36: The Inheritance of Heiresses
----------------------------------------
Henry concludes Numbers with this case determining marriage and inheritance.

The problem raised (vv. 1-4):
- Heads of Manasseh's family raise concern about the daughters of Zelophehad
- "They speak of the land of their possession... with as great assurance as if they had it already"
- If they marry into other tribes: "Their inheritance be taken from the inheritance of our fathers"
- Likely: "great court was made to them by some young gentlemen of other tribes, because they were heiresses"
- Henry's observation: "This truly is often aimed at more than it should be in making marriages, not the meetness of the person, but the convenience of the estate"
- "Wisdom indeed is good with an inheritance; but what is an inheritance good for in that relation without wisdom?"

Their wise approach:
- They acquiesced in the former order: not trying to overturn it
- Foresaw inconvenience "and brought the case to Moses, that he might consult the oracle of God"
- "God did not direct in it till themselves that were concerned wisely foresaw the inconvenience"
- "We should not covet to be judges in our own case, for it is difficult to be so without being partial"
- "It is easier in many cases to take good advice than to give it"

Two aims:
1. "To preserve the divine appointment of inheritances" - "one tribe might not encroach upon another"
2. "To prevent contests and quarrels among posterity"
- "It is the wisdom and duty of those that have estates in the world to settle them... that no strife and contention may arise"

The divine ruling (vv. 5-9):
- "The tribe of the sons of Joseph hath said well"
- "Let them marry to whom they think best; only to the family of the tribe of their father"
- "As children must preserve the authority of their parents... so parents must consult the affections of their children"
- "Forced marriages are not likely to prove blessings"
- "The inheritances must not remove from tribe to tribe"
- "God would not have one tribe to be enriched by the straitening and impoverishing of another"

The daughters' obedience (vv. 10-12):
- "They married their father's brothers' sons"
- "How could they but marry well, and to their satisfaction, when God himself directed them?"
- On cousin marriage: "Not in itself unlawful, nor within the degrees prohibited"
- But "ordinarily it is not advisable... The world is wide"

The conclusion (v. 13):
- "These are the judgments which the Lord commanded in the plains of Moab"
- Most laws since chapter 26 "related to their settlement in Canaan, into which they were now entering"
- "Whatever new condition God is by his providence bringing us into, we must beg of him to teach us the duty of it"
- "That we may do the work of the day in its day, of the place in its place"
