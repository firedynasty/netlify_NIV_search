================================================================================
BOOK: Exodus
================================================================================

Introduction
----------------------------------------
Matthew Henry introduces Exodus as the account of Israel's transformation from private families into a great nation. The book's Greek title means "departure" or "going out," referring to Israel's exodus from Egypt. Henry draws a spiritual parallel: Genesis (beginning) is immediately followed by Exodus (departure), reminding us that from birth we must think of death.

The book divides into two major sections: (1) the accomplishment of promises made to Abraham (chapters 1-19), and (2) the establishment of ordinances Israel would observe (chapters 20-40). Henry emphasizes that Exodus contains more types of Christ than perhaps any other Old Testament book, illustrating reconciliation with God through a Mediator. Moses, who wrote of Christ (John 5:46), now becomes both the author and hero of his own narrative.


Chapter 1
----------------------------------------
This chapter presents the contrast between God's blessing on Israel and Egypt's wickedness against them. Henry notes that Jacob's seventy descendants matched the number of nations in Genesis 10, showing God's providential design. After Joseph's death, Israel multiplied extraordinarily—from 70 to 600,000 fighting men in 215 years—fulfilling God's promise to Abraham.

The new Pharaoh "who knew not Joseph" represents how quickly human gratitude fades. Henry observes: "The best and most useful services done to men are seldom remembered." Pharaoh's "wise dealing" was actually foolish wickedness, attempting to suppress Israel through slavery and infanticide.

Key theological themes: (1) Affliction often produces church growth—"the more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied"; (2) The midwives' fear of God led them to disobey Pharaoh's murderous command, teaching that "if men's commands be any way contrary to God's commands, we must obey God and not man"; (3) God rewarded the midwives with households of their own. Henry notes that Satan's enmity against the seed of the woman "divests men of humanity itself."


Chapter 2
----------------------------------------
Henry calls Moses "that man of renown" and "the most remarkable type of Christ as a prophet, saviour, lawgiver, and mediator in all the Old Testament." Moses was born into the tribe of Levi (under Jacob's disgrace) yet became distinguished—typifying Christ who "came in the likeness of sinful flesh."

The parents' faith in hiding Moses (Hebrews 11:23) demonstrates that "faith in God's promise excites and quickens to the use of lawful means." Providence orchestrated Moses's rescue: Pharaoh's daughter found him, his own mother became his nurse, and he received Egyptian education preparing him for future leadership.

Moses's choice at age 40 to identify with his suffering brethren rather than enjoy Pharaoh's court illustrates holy contempt for worldly honors. His killing of the Egyptian taskmaster was "by special warrant from Heaven" but his rejection by a quarrelsome Hebrew ("Who made thee a prince?") delayed Israel's deliverance forty years. Henry notes: "Men know not what enemies they are to their own interest when they resist and despise faithful reproofs."

Moses's flight to Midian served divine purposes: shelter from danger and preparation through the shepherd's life for communion with God. The chapter concludes with Israel's groaning reaching God, who "heard," "remembered his covenant," "looked upon" them, and "had respect unto them"—four emphatic expressions signaling imminent deliverance.


Chapter 3
----------------------------------------
Moses's life divides into three forties: prince in Egypt, shepherd in Midian, king in Jeshurun. At eighty, keeping sheep near Mount Horeb, God appears in the burning bush. Henry draws lessons: "God will encourage industry" and "retirement is a good friend to communion with God."

The bush burning unconsumed symbolizes Israel in Egyptian bondage—"perplexed but not in despair, cast down but not destroyed." The angel in the flame is likely "the second person, the angel of the covenant, who is himself Jehovah."

God's self-revelation includes: (1) The command to remove shoes, signifying reverence for holy ground; (2) Identification as "the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob"—which Christ used to prove the resurrection (Matthew 22:31-32); (3) The declaration "I AM THAT I AM," denoting God's self-existence, eternality, unchangeableness, and faithfulness.

God announces He has seen Israel's affliction, heard their cry, known their sorrows, and come down to deliver them to "a land flowing with milk and honey." Moses's commission makes him ambassador to Pharaoh and prince to Israel. To Moses's objection "Who am I?", God answers: "Certainly I will be with thee." Henry notes: "God's presence puts an honour upon the worthless, wisdom and strength into the weak and foolish."

The chapter concludes with instructions: demand Pharaoh let Israel go, expect his refusal, and know that plagues will compel compliance while Egyptians will give Israel wealth at departure.


Chapter 4
----------------------------------------
Moses continues objecting to his commission, now crossing from humble diffidence into "cowardice, slothfulness, and unbelief." God answers each objection with patience, though "the anger of the Lord was kindled against him."

Three miraculous signs are given: (1) The rod becoming a serpent and back—signifying Pharaoh's oppressive rule being reversed; (2) Moses's hand becoming leprous then healed—representing both plagues on Egypt and Israel's cleansing; (3) Water turned to blood—first a sign, later a plague. Henry notes: "True miracles are the most convincing external proofs of a divine mission."

To Moses's plea of slow speech, God responds: "Who hath made man's mouth? Have not I the Lord?" This reminds us that both our faculties' perfections and imperfections serve God's purposes. Aaron is appointed as spokesman—"the tongue of Aaron with the head and heart of Moses would make one completely fit."

The mysterious incident at the inn, where God "sought to kill" Moses for failing to circumcise his son, shows that "omissions are sins" and neglecting covenant seals displeases God. Zipporah's urgent circumcision removed the threat.

Aaron meets Moses at "the mount of God" with joy. Together they present their commission to Israel's elders. The chapter ends triumphantly: "The people believed... they bowed their heads and worshipped"—grateful that God had visited them and seen their affliction.


Chapter 5
----------------------------------------
Moses and Aaron confront Pharaoh with "Thus saith the Lord God of Israel, Let my people go." Pharaoh's defiant response—"Who is the Lord that I should obey his voice?"—reveals the core conflict: "God must rule, but man will not be ruled." Henry observes that "ignorance and contempt of God are at the bottom of all the wickedness in the world."

When humble petition fails, Pharaoh increases oppression: straw is denied for brick-making while production quotas remain unchanged. Henry notes the irony that serving God is called "idle" while backbreaking labor under tyranny is demanded. "Holy diligence in the best business is censured by many as culpable carelessness in the business of the world."

The Israelite officers, beaten for failing impossible quotas, turn against Moses and Aaron: "The Lord look upon you and judge, because ye have made our savour to be abhorred." This unjust complaint shows how "the instruments of deliverance, when they aim to help, are found to hinder."

Moses takes his distress to God: "Wherefore hast thou so evil entreated this people? Why is it thou hast sent me?" Henry defends this as proper—"when we find ourselves perplexed in the way of duty, we ought to have recourse to God." The chapter illustrates God's paradoxical method: "He often brings them to the utmost straits when he is just ready to appear for them. The lowest ebbs go before the highest tides."


Chapter 6
----------------------------------------
God responds to Moses's complaints with renewed assurance: "Now shalt thou see what I will do to Pharaoh." Henry notes: "Man's extremity is God's opportunity." God reveals himself by the name "Jehovah"—the promise-keeping, perfecting God—whereas the patriarchs knew him as "El-shaddai" (God Almighty).

God's gracious intentions unfold in emphatic repetition: "I will bring you out... I will rid you... I will redeem you... I will take you to me for a people... I will bring you in." Henry observes: "Let man take the shame of his unbelief, which needs such repetitions; and let God have the glory of his condescending grace."

Yet Israel "hearkened not unto Moses for anguish of spirit and cruel bondage." Henry comments: "Disconsolate spirits often put from themselves the comforts they are entitled to."

Moses objects again—"I am of uncircumcised lips"—but God silences further argument with a direct charge. The chapter includes a genealogy tracing Moses and Aaron through Levi, Kohath, and Amram. Henry notes that Moses honestly records his father Amram's marriage to his aunt Jochebed (later forbidden), showing he "sought not his own praise, but wrote with sincere regard to truth."


Chapter 7
----------------------------------------
God declares Moses "a god to Pharaoh" and Aaron "thy prophet"—Moses acting with divine authority, Aaron speaking as his mouthpiece. At ages 80 and 83, they finally obey without objection: "They rebelled not against his word" (Psalm 105:28).

The contest begins when Aaron's rod becomes a serpent before Pharaoh. The magicians replicate this through enchantments, but Aaron's rod swallows theirs—proving "Great is the truth, and will prevail." Yet Pharaoh, having any appearance of opposition to justify his infidelity, hardens his heart.

The first plague transforms the Nile into blood. Henry sees this as righteous judgment: Egypt had idolized the Nile, and had stained it with Hebrew infants' blood. "Thus he gave them blood to drink, for they were worthy" (Revelation 16:6). The plague contrasts with Christ's first miracle: "Moses turned water into blood... but Jesus turned water into wine; for the law was a dispensation of death and terror, but grace and truth... came by Jesus Christ."

The magicians again imitate the plague—but note their folly: they could only make more blood, not restore water. "The design of the devil is only to delude his devotees... not to do them any real kindness." The plague lasts seven days, yet Pharaoh never requests Moses's intercession.


Chapter 8
----------------------------------------
Three plagues unfold: frogs, lice, and flies. God uses "little despicable inconsiderable animals" rather than lions or bears to magnify his power and humble Pharaoh's pride. "What a mortification must it be to this haughty monarch to see himself brought to his knees by such despicable means!"

**Frogs:** They invade houses, bedrooms, ovens, and kneading troughs. Pharaoh, who once asked "Who is the Lord?", now begs Moses to entreat God. When Moses lets Pharaoh name the time for removal ("Glory over me"), Pharaoh says "Tomorrow"—perhaps hoping they'd leave naturally. Moses prays, the frogs die, "and the land stank." But "when Pharaoh saw there was respite, he hardened his heart." Henry notes: "Till the heart is renewed by grace, impressions made by affliction do not abide."

**Lice:** This plague comes without warning. The magicians try to imitate it but fail, confessing "This is the finger of God." Yet Pharaoh remains obstinate. "Those that are not made better by God's word and providences are commonly made worse."

**Flies:** God now distinguishes Goshen from Egypt—"I will put a division between my people and thy people." Pharaoh offers compromises: sacrifice in Egypt, or not go far. Moses refuses partial obedience. Henry warns: "Let not Pharaoh deal deceitfully any more"—yet when the plague lifts, Pharaoh hardens his heart again.


Chapter 9
----------------------------------------
Three more devastating plagues: murrain on livestock, boils on bodies, and hail from heaven.

**Murrain:** A pestilence kills Egyptian cattle while not one Israelite animal dies. The Egyptians had worshipped cattle; God strikes their idols. "What we make an idol of, it is just with God to remove from us." Pharaoh investigates the distinction, confirms it—"and yet his heart was hardened."

**Boils:** Ashes from furnaces sprinkled heavenward produce painful inflammations. Henry sees poetic justice: "They had oppressed Israel in the furnaces, and now the ashes of the furnace are made as much a terror to them." The magicians, struck with boils themselves, cannot even stand before Moses—"their folly was made manifest unto all men" (2 Timothy 3:9). Now "the Lord hardened Pharaoh's heart"—wilful hardness becoming judicial hardness.

**Hail:** God's warning includes opportunity: those fearing God's word shelter their servants and cattle; others leave them exposed. "Those that will take warning may take shelter." The unprecedented storm of hail, fire, and thunder devastates Egypt while Goshen remains untouched. Pharaoh confesses dramatically: "I have sinned... the Lord is righteous, and I and my people are wicked." Henry cautions: "The terror of the rod often extorts penitent acknowledgments from those who have no penitent affections." Moses intercedes; the storm ceases; Pharaoh "sinned yet more, and hardened his heart."


Chapter 10
----------------------------------------
The eighth and ninth plagues—locusts and darkness—bring Egypt to the brink of ruin.

**Locusts:** God explains his purpose: that Moses may "tell in the ears of thy son, and of thy son's son, what things I have wrought in Egypt." Pharaoh is reproved: "How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me?" His own servants urge capitulation: "Knowest thou not yet that Egypt is destroyed?" Pharaoh offers to let the men go but retain the children as hostages. Henry sees Satan's strategy: "He is a sworn enemy to early piety." Moses refuses compromise, and locusts devour everything the hail had spared. Pharaoh confesses sin and begs forgiveness—but only deprecates "this death," not "this sin." When God removes the locusts by a mighty west wind, Pharaoh's heart hardens again.

**Darkness:** For three days, "darkness which might be felt" imprisons Egypt. No one sees another or rises from their place. Henry calls this "six nights in one" and connects it to hell's "outer darkness." Yet Israel has light in their dwellings—illustrating God's particular favor: "Wherever there is an Israelite indeed, there is light."

Pharaoh offers another compromise: take children but leave livestock. Moses insists: "There shall not an hoof be left behind." Enraged, Pharaoh threatens Moses with death: "In that day thou seest my face thou shalt die." Moses answers: "Thou hast spoken well, I will see thy face again no more."


Chapter 11
----------------------------------------
God reveals the final plague: "Yet will I bring one plague more upon Pharaoh... afterwards he will let you go." The death of the firstborn, first threatened in 4:23, is now imminent. Henry observes: "See how slow God is to wrath... if the death of their cattle had humbled them, their children would have been spared."

Israel finds favor with the Egyptians; "the man Moses was very great in the land of Egypt." Before leaving, they will collect silver and gold—just wages for their years of unpaid labor.

Moses delivers God's ultimatum: at midnight, every firstborn in Egypt shall die, from Pharaoh's heir to the slave's child. Yet "against any of the children of Israel shall not a dog move his tongue"—demonstrating God's distinction between his people and his enemies. Henry draws an eternal application: "Did men know what a difference God puts, and will put to eternity, between those that serve him and those that serve him not, religion would not seem such an indifferent thing."

Moses leaves "in a great anger"—righteous indignation at Pharaoh's stubborn refusal despite all warnings. "To be angry at nothing but sin is the way not to sin in anger." The chapter concludes: "Moses and Aaron did all these wonders before Pharaoh... and the Lord hardened Pharaoh's heart."


Chapter 12
----------------------------------------
This pivotal chapter establishes the Passover—"one of the most memorable ordinances" in Scripture. God commands a new calendar beginning with Abib (spring): "It is good to begin the day, and the year, and especially to begin our lives, with God."

**The Passover Institution:** Each household selects a lamb without blemish on the 10th day, kills it on the 14th at twilight, and applies blood to doorposts with hyssop. Henry develops extensive typology:
- The lamb typifies Christ, "the Lamb of God" (John 1:29)
- Without blemish: Christ's sinless perfection
- Set apart four days: Christ entered Jerusalem four days before crucifixion
- Roasted with fire: "the wrath of God... Christ was made a curse for us"
- No bone broken: fulfilled at the cross (John 19:33, 36)
- Blood sprinkled: "the application of the merits of Christ's death to our souls"
- Eaten with bitter herbs: "We must feed upon Christ with sorrow and brokenness of heart"
- Eaten in departing posture: "we must absolutely forsake the rule and dominion of sin"

**The Tenth Plague:** "At midnight the Lord smote all the firstborn in the land of Egypt... there was not a house where there was not one dead." Pharaoh capitulates completely: "Rise up, and get you forth... and bless me also."

**The Exodus:** About 600,000 men plus women and children depart—exactly 430 years from God's promise to Abraham. "It is a night to be much observed unto the Lord."


Chapter 13
----------------------------------------
Two perpetual remembrances are instituted: the consecration of the firstborn and the feast of unleavened bread.

**Sanctification of Firstborn:** Because God spared Israel's firstborn, they belong to him. "That which is by special distinguishing mercy spared to us should be in a peculiar manner dedicated to God's honour." Clean firstlings were sacrificed; unclean ones (like donkeys) redeemed with a lamb or destroyed. Human firstborn were redeemed with silver. Henry sees gospel typology: "By the blood of Christ we are redeemed, that we may be joined to the church of the firstborn."

**Teaching Children:** Parents must explain these rites when children ask: "What mean ye by this service?" Henry commends children's curiosity about spiritual things and parents' preparedness to answer. "It is a debt we owe to the honour of God, and to the benefit of our children's souls."

**Divine Guidance:** God leads Israel not the short way through Philistine territory (lest they face war prematurely and return to Egypt), but the longer wilderness route. "God proportions his people's trials to their strength, and will not suffer them to be tempted above what they are able."

**The Pillar of Cloud and Fire:** God's visible presence guides by day (cloud) and illuminates by night (fire). "Those whom God brings into a wilderness he will not leave nor lose there." Henry sees Christ in the pillar: "The cloud of his human nature was a veil to the light and fire of his divine nature."

Moses carries Joseph's bones—fulfilling the oath of Genesis 50:25 and expressing confidence in reaching Canaan.


Chapter 14
----------------------------------------
The Red Sea crossing—"the terror of the Canaanites, the praise and triumph of the Israelites"—typifies baptism (1 Corinthians 10:1-2) and the final ruin of impenitent sinners.

**Israel Trapped:** God deliberately leads Israel to camp by the sea, appearing "entangled in the land." Pharaoh, regretting his release ("Why have we done this?"), pursues with 600 chosen chariots. Israel, seeing the army, is "sore afraid"—hemmed in by mountains, sea, and enemy. "There was no way open for them but upwards, and thence their deliverance came."

**Israel's Murmuring:** They blame Moses: "Because there were no graves in Egypt, hast thou taken us away to die in the wilderness?" Henry rebukes their "base ingratitude" and "sordid contempt of liberty."

**Moses's Encouragement:** "Fear ye not, stand still, and see the salvation of the Lord... The Lord shall fight for you, and ye shall hold your peace." Note: "It is our duty and interest, when we cannot get out of our troubles, yet to get above our fears."

**Divine Intervention:** God commands: "Speak unto the children of Israel, that they go forward." The pillar moves from front to rear—light to Israel, darkness to Egypt. Moses stretches out his hand; a strong east wind divides the sea; "the waters were a wall unto them on their right hand, and on their left."

**Egypt's Destruction:** The Egyptians pursue recklessly. At the morning watch, God "troubled" them—wheels came off, chariots drove heavily. Too late they cry: "Let us flee from the face of Israel; for the Lord fighteth for them." The waters return; "there remained not so much as one of them."

**The Effect:** "Israel saw the Egyptians dead upon the sea shore... and the people feared the Lord, and believed the Lord, and his servant Moses."


Chapter 15
----------------------------------------
**The Song of Moses (vv. 1-21):** Israel's triumph becomes worship. This "most ancient" and "admirable composition" is "typical"—combining with "the song of the Lamb" in Revelation 15:2-3.

The song glorifies God alone: "I will sing unto the Lord, for he hath triumphed gloriously." Key themes include:
- **God as Strength and Salvation** (v. 2): "Happy the people whose God is the Lord"
- **God as Warrior** (v. 3): "The Lord is a man of war"
- **God's Incomparability** (v. 11): "Who is like unto thee, O Lord, among the gods? who is like thee, glorious in holiness, fearful in praises, doing wonders?"
- **God's Eternal Reign** (v. 18): "The Lord shall reign for ever and ever"

The song recounts Egypt's destruction—sinking "like a stone," "like lead"—and anticipates future conquest: "Fear and dread shall fall upon them... till thy people pass over." Miriam leads the women with timbrels and dancing.

**The Waters of Marah (vv. 22-27):** Joy quickly turns to trial. Three days without water, then only bitter water. The people murmur; Moses prays; God shows him a tree that sweetens the water. Henry sees typology: "The cross of Christ sweetens the bitter waters of affliction."

Here God establishes terms: "If thou wilt diligently hearken to the voice of the Lord... I will put none of these diseases upon thee." Obedience brings blessing; "I am the Lord that healeth thee."

**Elim (v. 27):** Twelve wells and seventy palm trees provide refreshment. "God can find places of refreshment for his people even in the wilderness of this world."


Chapter 16
----------------------------------------
One month after leaving Egypt, Israel murmurs for food in the wilderness of Sin. Henry notes the irony: "Such a multitude would soon be hungry... since nothing would serve but the flesh-pots of Egypt." Their complaint—"Would to God we had died by the hand of the Lord"—shows ungrateful hearts preferring slavery with bread to liberty with faith.

**Manna from Heaven:** God promises bread from heaven, testing whether Israel will follow his instructions. Manna appears "like coriander seed, white; and the taste... was like wafers made with honey." Each morning they gather exactly what they need—an omer per person. Those gathering much have nothing over; those gathering little have no lack. "He that had gathered much had nothing over"—Henry sees here the principle of Christian contentment.

**Sabbath Established:** On the sixth day they gather double, as manna doesn't appear on the seventh. Henry emphasizes: "It is fit that God should be both the director and master of our time." Some disobediently seek manna on the Sabbath and find none. The manna teaches daily dependence, provides occasion for observing the Sabbath, and typifies Christ: "our Lord Jesus Christ is the true manna, the bread of life, of which that was a type" (John 6:31-35).

A pot of manna is preserved in the ark as memorial "to be kept for your generations." Henry notes it was kept "before the Testimony," anticipating the ark's construction. Manna sustained Israel for forty years until they entered Canaan.


Chapter 17
----------------------------------------
Two trials test Israel: thirst at Rephidim and war with Amalek.

**Water from the Rock (vv. 1-7):** Following God's guidance, Israel arrives where there's no water. Again they murmur—"Give us water!"—and accuse Moses of murderous intent. "They tempted the Lord, saying, Is the Lord among us, or not?" Moses cries to God; the people nearly stone him. God commands Moses to strike the rock at Horeb, and abundant water flows. Henry draws rich typology: "That rock was Christ" (1 Corinthians 10:4). "The graces and comforts of the Spirit are compared to rivers of living water... these flow from Christ, who is the rock smitten by the law of Moses." The place is named Massah (temptation) and Meribah (strife)—memorializing Israel's sin rather than God's mercy.

**Victory over Amalek (vv. 8-16):** Amalek attacks Israel's rear—"smote those that were faint and feeble." Joshua commands in battle while Moses intercedes on the hilltop with uplifted rod. "When Moses held up his hand, Israel prevailed; when he let down his hand, Amalek prevailed." Aaron and Hur support Moses's weary arms until sunset, and Joshua wins decisively.

Henry sees two types of Christ: "Joshua, the captain of our salvation who fights our battles, and our Moses, who, in the upper world, ever lives making intercession." Moses builds an altar called "Jehovah-nissi"—"The Lord is my banner." God commands this victory recorded in writing and promises perpetual war against Amalek until his remembrance is blotted out.


Chapter 18
----------------------------------------
Jethro, priest of Midian and Moses's father-in-law, visits with Zipporah and their sons Gershom ("stranger") and Eliezer ("God is my help"). Moses recounts God's mighty acts; Jethro "rejoiced for all the goodness which the Lord had done." While Israel murmured despite God's favors, this Gentile celebrates them—"the faith of the Gentiles shamed the unbelief of the Jews."

Jethro's confession is notable: "Now I know that the Lord is greater than all gods; for wherein they dealt proudly, he was above them." He offers sacrifices to God and shares a covenant meal with Moses and the elders—"they did eat bread before God."

**Jethro's Counsel on Delegation (vv. 13-27):** Observing Moses judging the people from morning to evening, Jethro warns: "The thing that thou doest is not good. Thou wilt surely wear away." He advises appointing subordinate judges—rulers of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens—to handle routine matters while Moses focuses on teaching God's statutes and judging hard cases.

The qualifications for judges are exemplary: (1) able men of good sense, (2) God-fearing men of conscience, (3) men of truth and integrity, (4) men "hating covetousness"—despising bribes. Henry observes: "He that despiseth the gain of oppressions, and shaketh his hands from the holding of bribes, is fit to be a magistrate."

Moses wisely accepts this counsel, demonstrating that "a wise man will hear, and will increase learning, and not slight good counsel, though given by an inferior."


Chapter 19
----------------------------------------
Israel arrives at Sinai in the third month—"about fifty days after their coming out of Egypt," prefiguring Pentecost. Here God establishes his covenant with Israel.

**The Covenant Proposal (vv. 3-8):** God reminds Israel: "Ye have seen what I did unto the Egyptians, and how I bare you on eagles' wings, and brought you unto myself." Henry notes the eagle imagery denotes speed, strength, and tender protection—the eagle carries young upon its wings so enemies must shoot through the parent first.

The covenant terms: "If ye will obey my voice indeed... then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people... a kingdom of priests, and an holy nation." Henry emphasizes God's gracious initiative: "In all our dealings with God, free grace anticipates us." Israel unanimously accepts: "All that the Lord hath spoken we will do."

**Preparations for Sinai (vv. 9-15):** God announces he will descend "in a thick cloud." The people must wash their clothes, sanctify themselves, and observe bounds around the mountain—death penalty for anyone touching it. These preparations teach "humble awful reverence" before God.

**The Theophany (vv. 16-25):** On the third day: thunders, lightnings, thick cloud, trumpet "exceeding loud," and smoke "as the smoke of a furnace." The mountain quakes greatly. "Never was so great a congregation called together, and preached to, at once." Moses alone approaches "the thick darkness where God was"—typifying Christ our Mediator who draws near to God on our behalf.


Chapter 20
----------------------------------------
The Ten Commandments—"as remarkable a portion of scripture as any in the Old Testament."

**The Preface (vv. 1-2):** "God spoke all these words... I am the Lord thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt." Three obligations bind Israel: (1) God is Jehovah—self-existent and sovereign; (2) he is their covenant God; (3) he is their Redeemer from bondage. Christ similarly, "having rescued us out of the bondage of sin, is entitled to the best service we can do him."

**First Table—Duties to God:**
- **First Commandment** (v. 3): No other gods. "Pride makes a god of self, covetousness makes a god of money, sensuality makes a god of the belly."
- **Second Commandment** (vv. 4-6): No images. God is jealous; idolaters are "haters of him." Henry condemns Rome: "They leave out this commandment... joining the reason of it to the first."
- **Third Commandment** (v. 7): No vain use of God's name—including hypocrisy, broken vows, rash swearing, false swearing, and light speech about sacred things.
- **Fourth Commandment** (vv. 8-11): Remember the Sabbath. "God has blessed the sabbath day and sanctified it."

**Second Table—Duties to Neighbor (vv. 12-17):**
- **Fifth**: Honor parents—with promise of long life.
- **Sixth**: No murder—including hatred and malice.
- **Seventh**: No adultery—including lustful looks.
- **Eighth**: No stealing—including all fraud.
- **Ninth**: No false witness—including slander.
- **Tenth**: No coveting—striking at the root of all sin.

**The People's Terror (vv. 18-21):** Israel begs: "Let not God speak with us, lest we die." Moses assures them: the terror is "to prove you, and that his fear may be before your faces, that ye sin not." Moses draws near the thick darkness; the people stand afar off. Henry sees the contrast between law and gospel: "The law was given by Moses in such a way as might startle, affright, and humble men, that the grace and truth which came by Jesus Christ might be the more welcome."


Chapter 21
----------------------------------------
These "judgments" expand the fifth and sixth commandments, governing relationships and preserving life. Former slaves themselves, Israelites must treat servants mercifully.

**Laws Concerning Servants (vv. 1-11):** Hebrew servants serve six years then go free. One who chooses permanent service for love of master and family has his ear bored to the doorpost—a mark of voluntary bondage. Henry sees Christ typified: "He loved his Father, and his captive spouse, and the children that were given him, and would not go out free from his undertaking." Female servants sold with expectation of marriage must be treated honorably; if not married, they gain freedom.

**Capital Crimes (vv. 12-17):** Murder demands death; manslaughter allows refuge in cities of asylum. Striking or cursing parents warrants death—"those are perfectly lost to all virtue... that have broken through the bonds of filial reverence." Man-stealing (kidnapping for slavery) is capital: "men-stealers are reckoned among those wicked ones against whom laws must be made."

**Personal Injuries (vv. 18-27):** Assault requires compensation for lost time and healing costs. Masters who kill servants in correction face punishment; maiming a servant grants freedom. The law of retaliation ("eye for eye") establishes proportional justice—not private vengeance but magistrates' guidance.

**Liability for Animals and Property (vv. 28-36):** Owners bear responsibility for dangerous oxen; negligently kept animals or uncovered pits require compensation. Henry notes: "We must take heed not only of doing that which will be hurtful, but of doing that which may be so."


Chapter 22
----------------------------------------
Laws concerning theft, property, and social ethics continue the exposition of God's commandments.

**Theft and Property (vv. 1-15):** A stolen ox requires five-fold restitution, a sheep four-fold—more for the ox because of lost labor. A thief killed breaking in at night incurs no blood-guilt ("A man's house is his castle"), but killing by daylight requires accountability. Trespass by cattle, damage by fire, mishandled trusts, and borrowed goods all have prescribed remedies. Henry observes: "Fraud and injustice, so far from enriching men, will impoverish them."

**Moral Crimes (vv. 16-20):** Seduction requires marriage or compensation. Witchcraft earns death because it "gives that honour to the devil which is due to God alone." Bestiality and idolatry are capital offenses.

**Protection of the Vulnerable (vv. 21-24):** Strangers must not be oppressed: "You were strangers in Egypt." Widows and orphans receive God's special advocacy: "If thou afflict them in any wise, and they cry at all unto me, I will surely hear their cry." Henry warns oppressors: "Their wives shall become widows, and their children fatherless."

**Economic Ethics (vv. 25-31):** Lending to the poor must not involve usury. Pledged garments must be returned by nightfall. Rulers must not be reviled. First-fruits belong to God: "God is the first and best, and therefore must have the first and best." Israel must be "holy men unto me," distinguished by dietary purity.


Chapter 23
----------------------------------------
This chapter concludes the "first session" of laws from Sinai with commands about justice, mercy, sacred times, and promises for Canaan.

**Justice in Courts (vv. 1-9):** False witness and slanderous reports are forbidden—"the receiver, in this case, is as bad as the thief." Judges must resist crowd pressure: "Thou shalt not follow a multitude to do evil." Neither poverty nor wealth should bias judgment. "Keep thee far from a false matter"—distance yourself from injustice. Bribes blind wisdom. Strangers deserve justice because "ye know the heart of a stranger."

**Kindness to Enemies (vv. 4-5):** Even an enemy's stray ox must be returned; his fallen donkey must be helped. Henry notes this "old commandment" anticipates Christ's teaching to love enemies.

**Sacred Times (vv. 10-19):** Every seventh year the land rests—teaching dependence on Providence and trust that "the sixth year's increase should serve for two years' subsistence." The weekly Sabbath remains inviolable. Three annual feasts (Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles) require pilgrimage: "O what a good Master do we serve, who has made it our duty to rejoice before him!"

**Promises for Canaan (vv. 20-33):** God sends his Angel—likely "the angel of the covenant," Christ himself—to guide and protect. "Obey his voice... for my name is in him." Blessings attend obedience: blessed food and water, removed sickness, fruitfulness, long life. Conquest will be gradual: "By little and little I will drive them out... until thou be increased." Israel must make no covenant with Canaanites or their gods: "If thou serve their gods, it will surely be a snare unto thee."


Chapter 24
----------------------------------------
The covenant is ratified with blood—"a very solemn transaction... typifying the covenant of grace between God and believers through Christ."

**Covenant Ratification (vv. 1-8):** Moses presents "all the words of the Lord" to the people; they unanimously consent: "All that the Lord hath said will we do, and be obedient." Moses writes the covenant, builds an altar, erects twelve pillars representing the tribes, and offers burnt and peace offerings. Half the blood is sprinkled on the altar (Israel's dedication to God); half on the people (God's gracious favor confirmed). "Behold the blood of the covenant"—Henry connects this to Christ's words at the Last Supper and to Hebrews 9:19-20.

**The Elders Behold God (vv. 9-11):** Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders ascend the mountain. They "saw the God of Israel"—not his form, but glorious manifestation. Under his feet appeared sapphire pavement "as it were the body of heaven in his clearness." Yet "upon the nobles of Israel he laid not his hand"—they survived, even feasted in God's presence. Henry: "It is of the Lord's mercies that we are not consumed."

**Moses's Forty Days (vv. 12-18):** Moses receives the stone tablets written by God. Joshua accompanies him partway; Aaron and Hur govern below. The cloud covers Sinai six days; on the seventh, God calls Moses into the cloud. The glory appears "like devouring fire" to watching Israel. Moses enters the cloud and remains forty days and forty nights—"we are hereby taught to spend much time in communion with God."


Chapter 25
----------------------------------------
Instructions for the tabernacle begin—God's "royal palace" among his people. Henry notes Scripture describes this sanctuary more minutely than creation itself: "God's church and instituted religion are more precious to him... than all the rest of the world."

**The Offering (vv. 1-9):** Materials must be given "willingly with the heart"—gold, silver, brass, colored yarns, skins, wood, oil, spices, precious stones. God enriched Israel through Egypt's spoils; they should return thanks through generous giving. "Let them make me a sanctuary; that I may dwell among them." The tabernacle is both ceremonial (God's earthly palace) and typical (prefiguring Christ's body and the gospel church). Everything must follow "the pattern... showed thee in the mount."

**The Ark (vv. 10-22):** The ark—a gold-overlaid chest holding the testimony (law tablets)—symbolizes God's presence. Poles allow transport, teaching us to "take our religion along with us" wherever we go. The mercy seat (gold covering) typifies Christ "the great propitiation, whose satisfaction fully answers the demands of the law, covers our transgressions." Cherubim of beaten gold, wings outstretched, gaze toward the mercy seat—representing angels attending the Redeemer, desiring "to look into the mysteries of the gospel" (1 Peter 1:12). From between the cherubim God promises: "There I will meet with thee, and I will commune with thee."

**The Table of Showbread (vv. 23-30):** Twelve loaves represent the tribes before God. The bread acknowledges God's provision, symbolizes communion (God and Israel eating together), and typifies gospel provision—"In our Father's house there is bread enough."

**The Candlestick (vv. 31-40):** Seven-branched, all of beaten gold. The tabernacle had no windows; this candle-light "intimates the comparative darkness of that dispensation." Yet "the word of God is the candlestick, a light shining in a dark place."


Chapter 26
----------------------------------------
Detailed instructions for the tabernacle's construction—"a tent, such as soldiers now use in the camp."

**The Tent Structure:** God suits his presence to Israel's condition: a movable tabernacle for a wandering people. Henry observes: "The church in this world is in a tabernacle-state... we shall never be fixed till we come to heaven." Church privileges are "movable goods, from one place to another."

**Inner Curtains (vv. 1-6):** Ten curtains of fine linen in blue, purple, and scarlet, embroidered with cherubim (signifying angelic protection), joined by gold clasps. "Thus the churches of Christ... though they are many, are yet one."

**Outer Coverings (vv. 7-14):** Goat-hair curtains over the inner, then rams' skins dyed red and badgers' skins. The outside appears coarse; the beauty lies within. "Those in whom God dwells must labour to be better than they seem to be. Hypocrites put the best side outwards... but the king's daughter is all glorious within."

**Boards and Bars (vv. 15-30):** Gold-covered boards in silver sockets provide structure. "Curtains without boards would have been shaken by every wind; but it is a good thing to have the heart established with grace."

**The Two Veils (vv. 31-37):** The inner veil partitions the Most Holy Place—"the ceremonial law could not make the comers thereunto perfect." Christ's death rent this veil (Matthew 27:51). The outer veil covers the door; "A curtain shall be (if God please to make it so) as strong a defence to his house as gates of brass."


Chapter 27
----------------------------------------
Instructions for the altar, court, and lamp oil.

**The Brazen Altar (vv. 1-8):** Five cubits square, three cubits high, overlaid with brass, with horns at the corners. Sacrifices were bound here; refugees grasped these horns. "This brazen altar was a type of Christ dying to make atonement for our sins." The brass protected the wood from consuming fire—"nor could the human nature of Christ have borne the wrath of God if it had not been supported by a divine power." Christ sanctifies himself as our altar (John 17:19); "to the horns of this altar poor sinners fly for refuge when justice pursues them."

**The Court (vv. 9-19):** Fine linen hangings on bronze pillars with silver hooks enclose the tabernacle court—100 cubits long, 50 wide, 5 high. "This court was a type of the church, enclosed and distinguished from the rest of the world." The linen represents "the righteousness of saints" (Revelation 19:8). Yet under the gospel, "the enclosure is taken down. God's will is that men pray every where."

**Oil for the Lamps (vv. 20-21):** Pure beaten olive oil keeps the lamps burning perpetually. "The pure oil signified the gifts and graces of the Spirit... without which our light cannot shine before men." Priests light and tend the lamps daily—"thus it is the work of ministers, by the preaching and expounding of the scriptures (which are as a lamp), to enlighten the church."


Chapter 28
----------------------------------------
The priestly garments for Aaron and his sons—"for glory and for beauty."

**The Priests Appointed (vv. 1-5):** Aaron and his sons become priests. Moses humbly delegates the hereditary priesthood to his brother rather than his own family. "It is an instance of the humility of that great man." Skilled artisans, "filled with the spirit of wisdom," craft the garments.

**The Ephod (vv. 6-14):** A gold-woven outer garment with two onyx stones on the shoulders, engraved with the twelve tribes' names. "Christ our high priest... presents to himself and to his Father a glorious church." He bears Israel's names "as the representative of all Israel and an advocate for them."

**The Breastplate (vv. 15-30):** A square pouch with twelve precious stones representing the tribes, worn upon Aaron's heart. "Aaron shall bear the names of the children of Israel... upon his heart." Each tribe shines as a precious stone: "How small and poor soever the tribe was, it was a precious stone." The Urim and Thummim ("light and integrity") enable divine consultation—typifying Christ "the true light, the faithful witness."

**The Robe (vv. 31-35):** Blue robe with golden bells and pomegranate embroidery around the hem. The bells announce the priest's movements, allowing the people to join in prayer. "Some make the bells of the holy robe to typify the sound of the gospel of Christ in the world."

**The Golden Plate (vv. 36-39):** Engraved "HOLINESS TO THE LORD" and worn on Aaron's forehead. "Aaron must bear the iniquity of the holy things"—typifying Christ through whom "what is amiss in our services is pardoned" and "what is good is accepted."

**Inferior Priests' Garments (vv. 40-43):** Similar but simpler attire for Aaron's sons—coats, girdles, bonnets, and linen breeches. These garments typify "the righteousness of Christ" and "the armour of God."


Chapter 29
----------------------------------------
The elaborate consecration ceremony for priests.

**The Ceremony (vv. 1-37):** The Hebrew phrase for consecration is "filling the hand"—"ministers have their hands full; they have no time to trifle." Moses performs the ceremony at the tabernacle door—"the fittest place for those... to mediate between God and man."

The rites proceed in order:
1. **Washing** (v. 4): "Those must be clean who bear the vessels of the Lord."
2. **Clothing** (vv. 5-9): "They must put on the graces of the Spirit."
3. **Anointing** (v. 7): "In token of the pouring out of the Spirit."
4. **Sacrifices**: A bullock for sin-offering (confessing they "deserved to die for their own sin"); a ram for burnt-offering (total dedication); a ram of consecration with blood applied to ear, thumb, and toe—"the benefit of the expiation... applied and assured to them."

Blood and oil sprinkle both persons and garments. The priests feast on part of the sacrifice—"God and they might feast together, in token of friendship and fellowship." This continues seven days, a bullock daily, until priests and altar are fully consecrated.

Henry draws typology: "Our Lord Jesus is the great high-priest... anointed with the Spirit above his fellows... sanctified by his own blood... made perfect, or consecrated, through sufferings."

**The Daily Sacrifice (vv. 38-46):** Two lambs daily, morning and evening, as "continual burnt-offering throughout their generations." Henry applies: "This typified the continual intercession which Christ ever lives to make" and "teaches us to offer up to God the spiritual sacrifices of prayer and praise every day."

God's promises conclude gloriously: "I will dwell among the children of Israel, and will be their God."


Chapter 30
----------------------------------------
Additional tabernacle instructions: incense altar, ransom money, laver, anointing oil, and incense.

**Altar of Incense (vv. 1-10):** Gold-covered, placed before the veil "though he that ministered at the altar could not see the mercy-seat... yet he must look towards it." Aaron burns incense every morning and evening. "The brazen altar in the court was a type of Christ dying on earth; the golden altar in the sanctuary was a type of Christ interceding in heaven." Prayer is "the true incense"—it "must be offered daily... for we must pray always."

**The Ransom Money (vv. 11-16):** At each census, every Israelite twenty and older pays half a shekel—"the rich shall not give more, and the poor shall not give less." This equality signifies that "the souls of the rich and poor are alike precious" and "the rich have as much need of Christ as the poor." The money supports tabernacle service: "Those that have the benefit of God's tabernacle among them must be willing to defray the expenses of it."

**The Bronze Laver (vv. 17-21):** Placed between altar and tabernacle for priests to wash hands and feet before ministering. "He only shall stand in God's holy place that has clean hands and a pure heart." This teaches "daily to renew our repentance for sin and our believing application of the blood of Christ."

**The Anointing Oil (vv. 22-33):** Compounded from myrrh, cinnamon, calamus, cassia, and olive oil—used exclusively for tabernacle and priests. Christ is typified: "Christ's name is said to be as ointment poured forth." Making this for common use meant being "cut off."

**The Incense (vv. 34-38):** Sweet spices beaten very fine—"thus it pleased the Lord to bruise the Redeemer when he offered himself for a sacrifice of a sweet-smelling savour." Like the oil, duplicating it for personal use was forbidden. "It is a great affront to God to jest with sacred things."


Chapter 31
----------------------------------------
God concludes his instructions to Moses with appointments of workmen and the law of the Sabbath.

**Bezaleel and Aholiab (vv. 1-11):** God names the craftsmen for tabernacle construction. Bezaleel of Judah is "filled with the Spirit of God, in wisdom, and in understanding, and in knowledge, and in all manner of workmanship." Aholiab of Dan assists him. Henry observes: "Skill in common arts and employments is the gift of God... he teaches the husbandman discretion, and the tradesman too." The pairing of Judah (honored tribe) with Dan (less honored) shows that "God gives honour to that part which lacked... the head cannot say to the foot, I have no need of thee."

**The Sabbath Command (vv. 12-17):** Strictly renewed: "Verily my sabbaths ye shall keep." Even urgent tabernacle work must yield to sabbath rest. "The observance of the sabbath is indeed the hem and hedge of the whole law; where no conscience is made of that, farewell both godliness and honesty." The Sabbath is a perpetual covenant sign: "If we sanctify God's day, it is a sign between him and us that he has sanctified our hearts." Death is the penalty for profaning it.

**The Two Tables (v. 18):** God delivers to Moses "tables of testimony, tables of stone, written with the finger of God." Henry: "It is God only that can write his law in the heart; he gives a heart of flesh, and then, by his Spirit, which is the finger of God, he writes his will in the fleshly tables of the heart."


Chapter 32
----------------------------------------
The golden calf apostasy—"a very lamentable interruption" of the covenant's establishment.

**The Sin (vv. 1-6):** Moses delays on the mount; the people demand Aaron: "Up, make us gods." Henry analyzes: they were "weary of waiting for the promised land," "weary of waiting for the return of Moses," and "weary of waiting for a divine institution of religious worship." Aaron capitulates shamefully—he who "knew that there was no manner of similitude seen" on the mount fashions a molten calf. They proclaim "a feast to the Lord" yet worship the image. Henry rebukes: "Is this Aaron, the saint of the Lord... this he that had been with Moses in the mount?"

**God's Anger and Moses's Intercession (vv. 7-14):** God informs Moses: "Thy people... have corrupted themselves." They are "stiff-necked"—God threatens consumption. Yet "Let me alone" actually invites intercession. Moses pleads God's redemption, God's glory before Egypt, and God's covenant promises. "The Lord repented of the evil"—Henry: "See the power of prayer; God suffers himself to be prevailed with."

**Moses's Actions (vv. 15-29):** Descending, Moses breaks the tablets—"The greatest sign of God's displeasure against any person or people is his taking his law from them." He burns and grinds the calf, making Israel drink its powder. Aaron's excuse is pathetic: "I cast it into the fire, and there came out this calf." Moses calls: "Who is on the Lord's side?" The Levites execute 3,000 idolaters—"Consecrate yourselves to day to the Lord."

**Further Intercession (vv. 30-35):** Moses returns to God: "Oh, this people have sinned a great sin." Remarkably: "If not, blot me, I pray thee, out of thy book"—typifying Christ who was "cut off from the land of the living for the transgression of my people." God promises mercy yet reserves future discipline.


Chapter 33
----------------------------------------
The consequences of sin and the restoration of communion through Moses's mediation.

**Divine Displeasure (vv. 1-6):** God orders departure for Canaan but withdraws his special presence: "I will not go up in the midst of thee... lest I consume thee." Only a guardian angel will accompany them. This is "evil tidings"—Israel mourns and strips off ornaments. Henry: "Of all the bitter fruits and consequences of sin, that which true penitents most lament... is God's departure from them."

**The Tabernacle Outside Camp (vv. 7-11):** Moses pitches the meeting tent outside camp—"to signify to them that they had rendered themselves unworthy of it." Yet seekers of God may still approach. When Moses enters, the cloudy pillar descends; all the people worship at their tent doors. "The Lord spake unto Moses face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend"—intimate communion typifying Christ's access to the Father. Joshua remains at the tabernacle as attendant.

**Moses's Petition (vv. 12-17):** Moses prays for God's presence: "If thy presence go not with me, carry us not up hence." Better to die in the wilderness than advance without God. He argues God's glory—how will it be known Israel has found grace "is it not in that thou goest with us?" God grants: "I will do this thing also that thou hast spoken; for thou hast found grace in my sight."

**Request to See God's Glory (vv. 18-23):** "Show me thy glory." God answers: "I will make all my goodness pass before thee." Yet "thou canst not see my face: for there shall no man see me, and live." Moses will stand in "a clift of the rock" and see God's "back parts"—typifying that we shelter in Christ and see God's glory only in passing glimpses in this life.


Chapter 34
----------------------------------------
Covenant renewal and the proclamation of God's name—"four instances of the return of his favour."

**New Tablets (vv. 1-4):** Moses hews two stone tablets; God will rewrite the law. "Backsliders must repent, and do their first works." Henry sees gospel truth: "The best evidence of the pardon of sin and peace with God is the writing of the law in the heart."

**God's Proclamation (vv. 5-9):** God descends in cloud and proclaims his name—the fullest Old Testament declaration of divine character: "The Lord, The Lord God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth, keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty." Henry expounds each attribute:
- **Merciful**: "This is put first, because it is the first wheel in all the instances of God's good-will to fallen man."
- **Gracious**: "Freeness and kindness... of his own good-will, and not for the sake of any thing in them."
- **Long-suffering**: "Slow to anger... waits to be gracious."
- **Forgiving**: "Pardoning mercy is specified, because in this divine grace is most magnified."

Moses responds with worship and prayer: "Pardon our iniquity and our sin, and take us for thine inheritance."

**Covenant Renewed (vv. 10-28):** God promises to drive out the Canaanites—"marvels, such as have not been done in all the earth." Israel must destroy idolatrous altars and avoid intermarriage: "The Lord, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God." The three annual feasts are reinstated. Moses remains forty days, fasting; God rewrites the ten commandments.

**Moses's Shining Face (vv. 29-35):** Descending, "the skin of his face shone." This glory, unseen by Moses himself, terrifies the people. He wears a veil except when speaking with God. Henry applies: "Communion with God makes the face to shine in true honour... we should let our light shine before men."


Chapter 35
----------------------------------------
The tabernacle construction begins with willing offerings and Spirit-gifted workmen.

**Sabbath Reiterated (vv. 1-3):** Before work begins, sabbath rest is commanded—"even in earing time, and in harvest." Tabernacle urgency cannot override sabbath observance: "The honour of the sabbath was above that of the sanctuary, more ancient and more lasting."

**Call for Contributions (vv. 4-19):** "Whosoever is of a willing heart let him bring"—not compulsory taxation but voluntary offering. "God loves a cheerful giver, and is best pleased with the free-will offering." Every skilled person ("wise-hearted") should work. Moses lists everything needed: tabernacle structure, ark, table, lampstand, incense altar, burnt offering altar, laver, courtyard, and priestly garments.

**The People's Response (vv. 20-29):** They bring offerings immediately—"O that every congregation would thus depart from the hearing of the word of God, with a full resolution to be doers of the same!" Both men and women contribute: "bracelets, and earrings, and rings, and tablets, all jewels of gold." The women spin yarn of blue, purple, scarlet, and fine linen. Even goats' hair spinning is done "in wisdom." Henry: "It is not only rich gifts... it is not only fine work that God accepts." The rulers bring precious stones and spices. All gave from Egypt's spoil—"It was by a special providence... that the Israelites got all that spoil, and therefore it was highly fit that they should devote a part of it to the service of that God to whom they owed it all."

**The Workmen (vv. 30-35):** Moses announces Bezaleel and Aholiab, filled with God's Spirit "to devise curious works... and to teach." "Those that rule should teach; and those to whom God had given knowledge should be willing to communicate it."


Chapter 36
----------------------------------------
Tabernacle construction begins with superabundant offerings and faithful workmen.

**The Work Begins (vv. 1-4):** Bezaleel, Aholiab, and every "wise hearted man" receive the materials and begin. Moses calls those "in whose heart God had put wisdom" and "whose heart stirred him up to come unto the work." Henry: "Ability and willingness (with resolution) are the two things to be regarded in the call of ministers." The materials represent "precious souls" committed to builders "to be framed and wrought upon by their edification."

**Contributions Restrained (vv. 5-7):** A remarkable problem—the people bring too much! The workmen report: "The people bring much more than enough." Moses must proclaim a halt. Henry marvels: "The people were restrained from bringing... such was the zeal of those people." The workmen's honesty is notable: "Those are the greatest cheats that cheat the public"—but these "were men that knew when they had enough."

**The Tabernacle Structure (vv. 8-34):** The curtains are made—ten fine linen curtains with cherubim, coupled together. Henry draws spiritual meaning: the tabernacle typifies "the state of the church in this world":
- "Its foundation is not in the earth... Christ's kingdom is not of this world"
- "It is mean and mutable, and in a militant state"
- "Yet there is a beauty in holiness; the curtains were embroidered"
- "The several societies of believers are united in one... all become one tabernacle"

The outer coverings of goats' hair, rams' skins, and badgers' skins signify divine protection: "God has provided for his people a shadow from the heat, and a covert from storm and rain." The boards and bars show "the strength and stability of the church... the union of the church, and the hearty agreement of those that are its stays and supporters."

**The Veils (vv. 35-38):** The inner veil signifies "the darkness and distance of that dispensation" and "our present state, in comparison with heaven." The door hanging teaches: "While we are in this present state, we must get as near to God as we can."


Chapter 37
----------------------------------------
The tabernacle's holy furniture is made—ark, table, candlestick, and golden altar.

**Why the Repetition? (vv. 1-9):** Henry defends the detailed account of what was already prescribed: (1) Israel needed to "read and hear often of these divine and sacred treasures"; (2) Moses shows "the great care... to make every thing exactly according to the pattern"; (3) "God takes delight in the sincere obedience of his people, and keeps an exact account of it"; (4) "The spiritual riches and beauties of the gospel tabernacle are hereby recommended to our frequent and serious consideration."

**The Ark, Mercy Seat, and Cherubim:** These three together "represent the glory of a holy God, the sincerity of a holy heart, and the communion that is between them, in and by a Mediator":
- "It is the glory of a holy God that he dwells between the cherubim"
- "It is the character of an upright heart that, like the ark of the testimony, it has the law of God hid and kept in it"
- "By Jesus Christ, the great propitiation, there is reconciliation made... From the mercy-seat he will teach us, there he will accept us"

**The Table (vv. 10-16):** "God is a good householder, that always keeps a plentiful table." But note the gospel's superiority: the law offered only "show-bread, bread to be looked upon, not to be fed upon"—but Christ's table invites all believers: "Eat, O friends, come eat of my bread."

**The Candlestick (vv. 17-24):** All beaten work of pure gold, signifying "that light of divine revelation with which God's church upon earth... has always been enlightened." Henry: "The Bible is a golden candlestick; it is of pure gold." Its knops and flowers remind us that Scripture has beauties "we can no more give a reason for than for these knops and flowers, and yet we are sure that they were added for a good purpose."

**The Golden Altar and Incense (vv. 25-29):** The incense altar signifies "both the prayers of saints and the intercession of Christ, to which are owing the acceptableness and success of those prayers." Bezaleel, though untrained, made the anointing oil and incense "according to the work of the apothecary"—for "where God gives wisdom and grace, it will make the man of God perfect, thoroughly furnished to every good work."


Chapter 38
----------------------------------------
The brazen altar, laver, court, and an accounting of all materials used.

**The Brazen Altar (vv. 1-7):** Made of shittim wood overlaid with brass. "Christ was himself the altar to his own sacrifice of atonement, and so he is to all our sacrifices of acknowledgment. We must have an eye to him in offering them, as God has in accepting them."

**The Laver from Women's Mirrors (v. 8):** A remarkable detail—made from "the looking-glasses of the women assembling, which assembled at the door of the tabernacle." Henry honors these devoted women: "They assembled by troops... a blessed sight, to see so many, and those so zealous and so unanimous, in this good work." They parted with their mirrors either in repentance for vanity's former abuse, or in zeal—"rather than the workmen should want brass... they would part with their mirrors."

The spiritual lesson: "In the washing of repentance, there is need of the looking-glass of self-examination. The word of God is a glass, in which we may see our own faces; and with it we must compare our own hearts and lives, that, finding out our blemishes, we may wash with particular sorrow."

**The Court (vv. 9-20):** The hangings represented "the state of the Old-Testament church: it was a garden enclosed." Yet being "of curtains only intimated that the confinement of the church in one particular nation was not to be perpetual"—room would be made "for the Gentile world." Henry: "The church here on earth is but the court of God's house... through these courts we are passing to the holy place above."

**The Accounting (vv. 21-31):** Ithamar audits the materials under Moses's authority:
- **Gold**: 29 talents, 730 shekels—all from free-will offerings
- **Silver**: 100 talents, 1,775 shekels—from the half-shekel census tax, used for the tabernacle's foundation sockets
- **Brass**: 70 talents, 2,400 shekels

"See how liberal the people were and how faithful the workmen were, in both which respects their good example ought to be followed."


Chapter 39
----------------------------------------
The priestly garments are completed and all work is presented to Moses for approval.

**The Holy Garments (vv. 1-31):** Called "clothes of service"—Henry notes: "Those that wear robes of honour must look upon them as clothes of service; for from those upon whom honour is put service is expected." Each section concludes "as the Lord commanded Moses" (six times)—"an intimation to all the Lord's ministers to make the word of God their rule in all their ministrations."

The garments were rich and splendid, suited to "the church in its infancy... taught, thus pleased, with the rudiments of this world." But Henry warns against perpetuating such pomp: "Under the gospel... to affect and impose such pompous habits... is to betray the liberty wherewith Christ has made us free."

**Typological Application:** Christ is our great high priest who "put on the clothes of service—he arrayed himself with the gifts and graces of the Spirit... bore them on his shoulders, carried them in his bosom, laid them near his heart, engraved them on the palms of his hands." Believers are spiritual priests whose "clean linen... is the righteousness of saints," and "Holiness to the Lord must be so written upon their foreheads."

**Work Completed and Presented (vv. 32-43):** The workmen finished quickly through unanimity, divine teaching, and zealous hearts: "Resolution and industry, and a cheerful application of mind, will, by the grace of God, bring a great deal of good work to pass."

They brought everything to Moses for inspection—"they showed respect to Moses, who was set over them in the Lord." Though skilled, "they could not be well pleased with their own work, unless they had his approbation. Thus in all the services of religion we should labour to be accepted of the Lord."

**Moses's Blessing (v. 43):** Finding all done according to pattern, Moses blessed them. He "did not find fault where there was none"—"Why should any take a pride in being hard to be pleased?" He both praised and prayed for them. Their reward was not wages but blessing: "Those whom God employs he will bless, and those whom he blesses are blessed indeed."


Chapter 40
----------------------------------------
The tabernacle is erected on New Year's Day and the glory of the Lord fills it—Exodus concludes in triumph.

**Orders to Erect (vv. 1-15):** God commands Moses to set up the tabernacle "on the first day of the first month"—nearly one year after leaving Egypt. Henry: "It is good to begin the year with some good work. Let him that is the first have the first." Moses must place each item in order: ark behind the veil, table, candlestick, golden altar, brazen altar in the court, laver between tent and altar, then the court hangings.

The anointing consecrates all: "Every thing was sanctified when it was put in its proper place, and not till then, for till then it was not fit for the use to which it was to be sanctified. As every thing is beautiful in its season, so is every thing in its place."

**The Work Completed (vv. 16-33):** Moses obeys precisely—"as the Lord commanded Moses" appears seven times in fourteen verses. "Moses himself, as great a man as he was, would not pretend to vary from the institution, neither to add to it nor diminish from it, in the least punctilio."

He immediately puts everything to use: bread on the table, lamps lit, incense burning, offerings on the altar, washing at the laver. "God's gifts are intended for use, and not barely for show... Talents were given to be occupied, not to be buried."

**The Glory Descends (vv. 34-38):** "A cloud covered the tent of the congregation, and the glory of the Lord filled the tabernacle." As God took possession of earth after creating it for man, so now he takes possession of his dwelling among men. "The shechinah, the divine eternal Word, though not yet made flesh, yet, as a prelude to that event, came and dwelt among them."

The cloud served as: (1) visible token of God's presence; (2) concealment—"Verily thou art a God that hidest thyself"; (3) protection—"their best guard"; (4) guide for Israel's journeys.

So dazzling was the glory that "Moses was not able to enter." Henry applies: "What Moses could not do, in that he was weak through the flesh, has been done by our Lord Jesus, whom God caused to draw near and approach, and who, as the forerunner, has for us entered." Christ himself is "the true tabernacle, filled with the glory of God... In him the shechinah took up its rest for ever, for in him dwells all the fulness of the godhead bodily. Blessed be God for Jesus Christ!"
