# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy: Introduction

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Matthew Henry emphasizes Deuteronomy as "the second law, or a second edition of the law" - not requiring amendments since the original was perfect, but containing vital additions for Israel's guidance. He presents a key theological principle: **"What God has spoken once we have need to hear twice, to hear many times."** This repetition serves God's compassionate accommodation to human weakness and the need for reinforcement of divine truth.

Henry draws a significant parallel between Deuteronomy and the gospel, describing the gospel as "a kind of Deuteronomy, a second law, a remedial law, a spiritual law, a law of faith." This connection bridges Old and New Testament revelation, showing continuity in God's redemptive plan.

## Key Practical Applications

The commentary emphasizes three practical ways Deuteronomy was to be honored: kings must personally copy and daily read it, it should be publicly displayed on stones when entering Canaan, and priests must read it publicly every seventh year during the Feast of Tabernacles. These applications demonstrate the importance of **personal engagement, public acknowledgment, and regular communal reinforcement** of God's Word.

Henry advocates for persistent preaching of essential truths, noting that ministers should follow Paul's example: "To write the same things to me indeed is not grievous, but for you it is safe." Repetition in ministry serves congregational safety and spiritual growth.

## Notable Insights

Henry provides crucial historical context: the original generation that received the law had died, making this repetition necessary for **"a new generation"** entering Canaan. Moses functions as a covenant mediator, reading "the articles of agreement" so Israel would "know upon what terms and conditions they were to hold and enjoy that land."

The commentary highlights Moses' intensive final ministry - accomplishing this entire book's content in just two months before his death, demonstrating urgency in gospel work when time is short.

## Cross-References and Biblical Connections

Henry cites multiple supporting passages: Leviticus 1:1 (regarding divine inspiration), Hosea 8:12 (about counting God's law as strange), Isaiah 28:10 ("precept upon precept, line upon line"), and Philippians 3:1 (Paul's approach to repetition).

Significantly, he notes that when Christ answered Satan's temptations with "It is written," **all quotations came from Deuteronomy**, highlighting the book's unique authority and Christ's endorsement of its theological importance.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 1: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

**God's Faithful Providence and Promise**: Henry emphasizes that Moses's farewell sermon demonstrates God's unchanging faithfulness to His covenant promises. The timing—after 40 years in the wilderness and on the threshold of Canaan—shows that "God had borne their manners" and was ready to fulfill His promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Henry notes that God's command to leave Sinai ("You have dwelt long enough in this mount") represents divine timing: "Though God brings his people into trouble and affliction...he knows when they have dwelt long enough in it."

**Divine Government and Human Authority**: A central theme is God's establishment of righteous governance through human magistrates. Henry sees the appointment of judges as evidence of God's care for order and justice, emphasizing that "the judgment is God's"—human authorities serve as God's vicegerents who must "act for him, and therefore must act like him."

**Spiritual Preparation Through Law**: Henry interprets Moses's rehearsal of the law as evidence of divine favor, stating there is "no better evidence and earnest of God's favour than his putting his law in our hearts" (referencing Psalm 147:19-20).

## Key Practical Applications

**Leadership Principles**: Henry draws extensive practical lessons about godly leadership from Moses's example. Moses demonstrates humility by sharing authority ("he was not ambitious of monopolizing the honour"), wisdom in allowing people to choose their own judges, and integrity in seeking others' welfare above his own advancement.

**Judicial Excellence**: The commentary provides detailed guidance for magistrates: be "diligent and patient" (hear all sides carefully), "just and impartial" (show no favoritism), and "resolute and courageous" (not swayed by pressure). Henry emphasizes that judges must remember they represent God and will give account to Him.

**Spiritual Progress**: Drawing parallels between Israel's journey and Christian experience, Henry notes that just as God commanded Israel to "go forward," believers must advance "from the terrors of the spirit of adoption" toward the "heavenly Canaan."

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-5 (Setting and Timing)**: Henry emphasizes that Moses focused on "duty to God" rather than "military affairs" because "their religion would be their best policy."

**Verses 6-8 (Departure from Sinai)**: Mount Sinai represents spiritual bondage and divine terrors, but God's timing is perfect—"he will certainly find a time, the fittest time, to advance them."

**Verses 10-11 (Population Growth)**: Henry celebrates Moses's enlarged prayer for Israel to become "a thousand times more," noting that "we are not straitened in the power and goodness of God, why should we be straitened in our own faith and hope?"

**Verse 17 ("The judgment is God's")**: This becomes a cornerstone principle—human judges "act for him" and must remember they will "certainly call you to account if you do wrong."

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects Deuteronomy 1 to broader biblical themes through several references:

- **Romans 8:15**: Links Israel's progression from Sinai to the Christian's movement from fear to adoption
- **Hebrews 12:18 and Galatians 4:24**: Mount Sinai as representing bondage and terror
- **Acts 6:3,6**: Parallels between Israel choosing judges and apostles appointing deacons
- **Isaiah 50:4**: Connects patient hearing to wisdom ("the ear of the learner is necessary to the tongue of the learned")
- **Psalm 147:19-20**: God giving His law as evidence of divine favor

The commentary presents Deuteronomy 1 as both historical narrative and timeless instruction for godly leadership, righteous judgment, and faithful dependence on God's promises and timing.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy Chapter 2: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry identifies three central theological themes in this chapter. First, **God's sovereignty over all nations** - both in judgment and protection. The commentary emphasizes that God determines territorial boundaries and governs the rise and fall of peoples, including giants who were displaced by smaller nations because "the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong."

Second, **divine timing and preparation**. Henry notes that Israel's 38-year wilderness wandering served dual purposes: "humbling them for sin, teaching them to mortify their lusts" while simultaneously allowing the Canaanites' "measure of iniquity" to fill up. He observes that "it is a work of time to make souls meet for heaven."

Third, **God's faithfulness to His promises**. The commentary highlights how "though God contend long, he will not contend for ever," referencing Habakkuk's principle that "the vision is for an appointed time, and at the end it shall speak, and not lie."

## Key Practical Applications

Henry draws several practical lessons for Christian conduct. Regarding restraint in victory, he warns that "there is need of great caution and a strict government of our own spirits, to keep ourselves from injuring those against whom we have an advantage." When the Edomites would fear Israel's approach, they were still commanded not to exploit this weakness.

On forgiveness and justice, Henry notes that "before God brought Israel to destroy their enemies in Canaan, he taught them to forgive their enemies in Edom." Despite past wrongs, Moses still called the Edomites "brethren," demonstrating that "though our relations fail in their duty to us, we must retain a sense of the relation."

Regarding economic ethics, Henry emphasizes that "religion must never be made a cloak for injustice" - Israel must pay fairly for provisions rather than taking advantage of their military superiority.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

On verse 7's declaration that Israel "lacked nothing," Henry draws two applications: "Thou needest not beg" and "thou must not steal." He encourages believers to "live by faith and not by thy sword," trusting divine providence rather than using "indirect methods for thy supply."

Regarding the historical accounts of displaced giants (verses 10-23), Henry sees encouragement for Israel facing the Anakim, noting that "if the providence of God had done this for the Moabites and Ammonites, much more would his promise do it for Israel his peculiar people."

## Cross-References and Connections

Henry connects this chapter to broader scriptural themes, referencing Numbers 20:21 regarding Edom's earlier refusal of passage, and Genesis accounts of the original inhabitants. He links the principle of territorial sovereignty to Isaiah 5:8, noting that "God's Israel shall be well placed, but must not expect to be placed alone in the midst of the earth."

The commentary also connects to Habakkuk 2:3 regarding divine timing, and references the Genesis flood narrative to explain why giants faced judgment for their "impiety and oppression." Henry concludes that these historical patterns demonstrate "how uncertain things worldly possessions are, and how often they change their owners."

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 3: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry emphasizes **God's faithfulness and power** as the central theme. The conquest of Og demonstrates that "when God pleads his people's cause he can deal with giants as grasshoppers." The victories over Sihon and Og represent the beginning of Israel's triumphs, serving as both encouragement for future battles and "a specimen" for Moses before his death—paralleling how "the Spirit is given to those that believe as the earnest of their inheritance."

The chapter illustrates **divine sovereignty over human strength**. Despite Og being "of the remnant of the giants" with extraordinary physical power, Henry notes that "No man's might can secure him against the Almighty." God's promise to Moses, "Fear him not: for I will deliver him," demonstrates that divine assurance transcends human intimidation.

## Key Practical Applications

**Encouragement from past victories**: Henry emphasizes learning from God's previous faithfulness. Joshua should remember what God accomplished with the two kings, inferring "so shall the Lord do to all the rest of the kingdoms." Christians should similarly draw strength from witnessed divine interventions.

**Community responsibility over self-interest**: The conditional grant to the eastern tribes teaches that "we are not born for ourselves, but are members one of another." Henry applies Philippians 2:4, noting "It ill becomes an Israelite to be selfish... A good man cannot rejoice much in the comforts of his family unless withal he sees peace upon Israel."

**Proper response to denied prayers**: Moses's humble acceptance of God's refusal to let him enter Canaan models faithful submission. His prayer wasn't "passionate and impatient complaints" but humble petition, showing "we should never allow any desires in our hearts which we cannot in faith offer up to God."

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-11** (Og's defeat): Henry details Og's formidable nature—his iron bedstead measuring "four yards and a half long, and two yards broad," suggesting Og was "three yards and a half high." Yet this giant's boldness in attacking Israel showed how "those that are not awakened by the judgments of God upon others... are ripening apace for the like judgments upon themselves."

**Verses 21-22** (Encouraging Joshua): Henry stresses two encouragements for young believers: remembering what God has done and trusting His promises. "We reproach our leader if we follow him trembling."

**Verses 23-29** (Moses's prayer): Moses's plea reveals proper prayer attitude—acknowledging God's greatness while humbly requesting mercy. Henry notes Moses prayed not knowing "but that the threatening was conditional," showing appropriate hope despite apparent finality.

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects this chapter to multiple scriptures: **Psalms 135:11 and 136:19-20** celebrate these same victories; **Jeremiah 3:8** warns about hardened hearts ignoring God's judgments; **Philippians 2:4** supports community-mindedness over selfishness; **Psalm 86:8** echoes Moses's praise of God's incomparable works; and **Exodus 15:11** shows Moses's consistent theology about God's uniqueness.

The commentary demonstrates how Old Testament military victories prefigure spiritual warfare principles, emphasizing that "when we are at rest we should desire to see our brethren at rest too."

## Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 4: Summary

### Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry identifies three primary theological themes in Deuteronomy 4. First, **the absolute uniqueness and supremacy of God**, emphasized through Moses' declaration that "the LORD he is God in heaven above, and upon the earth beneath: there is none else" (v. 39). Henry sees this as foundational to Israel's covenant relationship.

Second, **the prohibition against idolatry** forms a central concern. Henry notes that God deliberately revealed himself at Horeb through voice alone—"You saw no similitude" (v. 12, 15)—to prevent any attempt at creating images. He argues this was "God in infinite wisdom so ordering his manifestation of himself because of the peril of idolatry."

Third, **the permanence and sufficiency of God's law** is stressed through the command to neither add nor diminish from God's word (v. 2). Henry interprets this both practically (not omitting required obedience or adding forbidden acts) and doctrinally (not introducing human inventions in worship).

### Key Practical Applications

Henry emphasizes **diligent attention to Scripture**, noting that "hearken" means not just immediate listening but ongoing attentiveness whenever God's law is read. He stresses the need for **extreme caution in religious observance**: "Those that would be religious must be very cautious, and walk circumspectly."

**Intergenerational teaching** emerges as crucial, with Henry highlighting the command to "teach them thy sons, and thy sons' sons" (v. 9). He also emphasizes **learning from history**, arguing that reviewing God's providences should "quicken and engage" believers to obedience.

### Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

On verse 6, Henry observes that keeping God's statutes demonstrates "wisdom and understanding in the sight of the nations," making Israel's obedience a testimony to surrounding peoples.

Regarding verse 24's description of God as "a consuming fire," Henry connects this to God's jealousy over idolatry, warning that spiritual adultery through false worship will bring divine judgment.

Henry finds particular significance in verses 29-31, seeing God's promise that scattered Israel will find him when seeking "with all thy heart and with all thy soul" as demonstrating divine mercy even in judgment.

### Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects the prohibition against altering God's word (v. 2) to Ecclesiastes 3:14 ("God's work is perfect") and Revelation 22:18-19 (the similar warning concluding Scripture).

He references Hosea 8:6 ("The workman made it, therefore it is not God") as a demonstration against idolatry, and Exodus 19:21 regarding the people's desire to "break through unto the Lord to gaze" at Horeb.

Henry also connects Moses' unique privilege of seeing God's "similitude" (Numbers 12:8) to his being "above the temptation of idolatry," contrasting this with the people's recent exposure to Egyptian idols.

**Overall Assessment**: Henry presents this chapter as Moses' "most lively and excellent discourse," emphasizing how historical review should motivate present obedience and how God's uniqueness demands exclusive worship through his prescribed means alone.

----------------------------------------
# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 5 Summary

## Main Theological Themes

Henry presents Deuteronomy 5 as "the second edition of the ten commandments," emphasizing the **covenant nature** of God's law. He highlights "the wonderful condescension of divine grace in turning the command into a covenant," showing how God binds Himself through promises and threatens consequences to encourage obedience. The **progressive revelation** theme emerges as Henry notes the law given to Israel revealed more of God's mind than previous generations knew - "The light of divine revelation shone gradually."

**Christ as mediator** forms a central typological interpretation: Moses "was a type of Christ, who stands between God and man...a blessed days-man, that has laid his hand upon us both, so that we may both hear from God and speak to him without trembling."

## Key Practical Applications

Henry emphasizes **purposeful hearing**: "When we hear the word of God we must set ourselves to learn it...and what we have learned we must put in practice, for that is the end of hearing and learning; not to fill our heads with notions, or our mouths with talk, but to rectify and direct our affections and conversations."

Regarding the **Sabbath commandment**, he draws practical lessons from Israel's Egyptian bondage: servants should receive sabbath rest "for thou knowest the heart of a servant, and how welcome one day's ease will be after six days' labour."

He stresses **comprehensive obedience**: "God's commands are all of a piece...we must not be partial in the law, but have respect to all God's commandments."

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-5**: Henry emphasizes the covenant was made with the present generation, not just their fathers, showing each generation's direct responsibility to God.

**Sabbath commandment variation**: Unlike Exodus 20's creation rationale, here the Egyptian deliverance provides the reason, which Henry sees as typical of Christian redemption - pointing toward Sunday worship "in remembrance of that glorious work of power and grace."

**Fifth commandment addition**: The phrase "that it may go well with thee" receives special attention, with Henry noting it applies whether one lives long or not - "it shall go well with them, either in this world or in a better."

**Verse 22**: God "added no more" after the Ten Commandments, leading Henry to conclude "therefore we must not add: the law of the Lord is perfect."

## Cross-References and Connections

Henry connects this passage to multiple scriptures: **Acts 7:8** (covenant with Abraham differed from Sinai), **Philippians 3:1** (need for repetition), **Ephesians 6:3** (fifth commandment promise), **James 2:10-11** (breaking one commandment breaks all), and **Revelation 11:19** (ark of the covenant).

He draws parallels between the **Lord's Prayer variations** in Matthew and Luke, arguing for focusing on substance over exact wording. The **terror of Sinai** prefigures both "the gospel of grace" and "the terrors of the judgment-day."

Henry concludes with God's earnest desire expressed in verse 29: "O that there were such a heart in them!" demonstrating that "The God of heaven is truly and earnestly desirous of the welfare and salvation of poor sinners."

# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 6 Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry identifies this chapter as Moses' charge to Israel to "keep up their religion in Canaan." The central theological theme centers on **monotheism and covenant love**. Henry emphasizes that "Jehovah our God is one Jehovah" as the foundational truth that would "effectually arm them against all idolatry." He sees verses 4-5 as "a brief summary of religion, containing the first principles of faith and obedience."

The commentary presents **the relationship between fear and love of God** as complementary rather than contradictory: "the warmer our affection to him the greater will be our veneration for him." Henry notes the remarkable nature of God's law requiring love, observing, "Did ever any prince make a law that his subjects should love him? Yet such is the condescension of the divine grace."

## Key Practical Applications

Henry draws several practical applications for maintaining faith:

**Religious Education**: Parents must "do what they can to engage the affections of their children to him, and so to preserve the entail of religion in their families." He emphasizes that Moses made the law "so very plain and easy that every father might be able to instruct his sons."

**Daily Spiritual Discipline**: Henry advocates for constant engagement with God's word through meditation, pious discourse, and frequent reading. God's words must be "laid up on our heart, that our thoughts may be daily conversant with them."

**Guarding Against Prosperity**: A key warning concerns forgetting God during times of abundance. Henry notes the danger of receiving "cities which thou buildest not, houses which thou filledst not" without maintaining gratitude and dependence on God.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 4-5**: Henry explains that loving God must be "sincere," "strong," "superlative," "intelligent," and "entire." He notes: "He that is our all must have our all, and none but he."

**Verse 6**: Concerning meditation, Henry warns: "we are in danger of losing the things if we neglect the words, by which ordinarily divine light and power are conveyed to the heart."

**Verse 7**: On teaching children, Henry uses the metaphor of whetting: "frequently repeat these things to them, try all ways of instilling them into their minds...as, in whetting a knife, it is turned first on this side, then on that."

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects this passage to the broader biblical narrative through several references:

- **Matthew 28:20** regarding teaching "all that he has commanded, and neither more nor less"
- **Mark 12:32-33** on the unity of God and intelligent love
- **Matthew 23:5** concerning phylacteries and proper religious observance
- **Proverbs 7:1-3** about keeping God's word constantly before us

Henry also draws connections to Christ's ministry, noting how "Christ's ministers are to teach his churches all that he has commanded." He sees the principle that "those that are well taught, if they do what they are taught, shall be well fed too" as applicable beyond Israel to all believers who practice faithful obedience.

The commentary emphasizes that this chapter provides timeless principles for maintaining vital faith across generations through deliberate spiritual practices and unwavering devotion to the one true God.

# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 7: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry presents **God's election and covenant faithfulness** as the central theme. Israel's chosenness stems not from their merit—"You were the fewest of all people" (v. 7)—but from God's sovereign love: "He loved you because he would love you." This demonstrates that "all that God loves he loves freely" and "those that perish perish by their own merits, but all that are saved are saved by prerogative."

The **doctrine of separation from evil** forms another key theme. Henry emphasizes that "those taken into communion with God must have no communication with the unfruitful works of darkness," establishing a principle that transcends the specific historical context.

## Key Practical Applications

Henry draws several enduring applications:

**Marriage and relationships**: He strongly warns against "mixed marriages," noting that "thousands in the world have been undone by irreligious ungodly marriages." He references the New Testament principle of not being "unequally yoked with unbelievers" (2 Cor. 6:14), arguing there's "more ground of fear that the good will be perverted than hope that the bad will be converted."

**Spiritual warfare**: Henry applies the destruction of Canaanites allegorically to personal holiness: "Thus we must deal with our lusts that war against our souls...let us not make covenants with them, nor show them mercy, but mortify and crucify them."

**Destroying idolatrous influences**: Christians must eliminate anything that promotes false worship, comparing this to the believers in Acts who burned their conjuring books.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-2**: Henry notes the commission was specifically limited to seven named nations, emphasizing that "after-ages were not to draw this into precedent" for indiscriminate violence. Under the New Testament dispensation, "human blood has become perhaps more precious than it was."

**Verse 6**: God's choice made Israel "a special people," creating mutual obligation—if God chose them exclusively, shouldn't they choose Him exclusively?

**Verses 7-8**: Henry highlights the paradox of divine election: God chose the smallest, weakest nation to magnify His power and demonstrate pure grace.

**Verses 9-10**: God proves "faithful" to friends but "just to his enemies," showing that "wilful sinners are haters of God" who "cannot hurt him, but certainly ruin themselves."

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry provides extensive scriptural connections:
- **Genesis 6:2**: Mixed marriages causing the flood
- **2 Corinthians 6:14**: New Testament marriage principles  
- **Exodus 23:24; 34:13**: Previous commands to destroy idols
- **2 Chronicles 31:1; 34:3,7**: Historical examples of destroying idolatry
- **Acts 19:19**: Believers burning occult materials
- **Psalm 21:12**: God's arrows prepared against His enemies
- **Proverbs 11:31**: Divine recompense on earth
- **Hosea 14:4**: God's free love

Henry demonstrates how Old Testament principles find fulfillment and application in New Testament teaching, particularly regarding separation from evil influences and God's covenant faithfulness across dispensations.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy Chapter 8: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry emphasizes **God's paternal discipline and providence** as central themes. He interprets the wilderness journey as divine education, describing it as "the school in which they had been for forty years boarded and taught, under tutors and governors." The commentary presents three key theological concepts: God's humbling of Israel to prevent pride, His testing of their faith and obedience, and His faithful provision despite their unworthiness.

**Divine testing and humbling** receives particular attention. Henry explains that God brought Israel into straits "for the mortifying of their pride" and "for the manifesting of their perverseness," showing that God's choice of Israel wasn't based on their merit but His grace alone.

## Key Practical Applications

Henry draws several practical lessons for Christian living:

**Remembrance as spiritual discipline**: He strongly advocates remembering God's past mercies, noting "It is very good for us to remember all the ways both of God's providence and grace, by which he has led us hitherto through this wilderness."

**Trust in divine providence**: Using the manna as an example, Henry teaches that believers shouldn't "perplex themselves with care what they should eat and drink," but trust God's provision even when conventional means fail.

**Proper response to affliction**: He emphasizes that suffering should drive believers toward obedience: "This use we should make of all our afflictions; by them let us be engaged and quickened to our duty."

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verse 3** (man shall not live by bread alone): Henry provides both literal and spiritual interpretations, noting that "the word of God, as it is the revelation of God's will and grace duly received and entertained by faith, is the food of the soul." He connects this to Christ's temptation, showing God's ability to sustain life through extraordinary means.

**Verse 4** (clothing and feet): Henry calls this "a standing miracle," interpreting it as God's care for both practical needs and spiritual lessons about depending on Him rather than material abundance.

**Verse 5** (fatherly chastening): Henry emphasizes the loving nature of divine discipline, explaining that God chastens "not as a man wounds and slays his enemies...but as a man chastens his son whose happiness and welfare he designs."

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry makes extensive scriptural connections throughout his commentary:

- **Matthew 4:3-4**: Christ's temptation parallels Israel's testing with manna
- **Matthew 5:6**: Spiritual hunger parallels Israel's physical hunger before receiving manna  
- **Matthew 6:25**: Christ's teaching about anxiety echoes wilderness lessons about trusting God's provision
- **Luke 22:35**: The disciples' mission without provisions mirrors Israel's dependence on God
- **Psalm 94:12**: Connects divine chastening with teaching and blessing

Henry also references the ceremonial connections, noting that "the feast of tabernacles" commemorated "their passage through the wilderness," just as Passover remembered Egyptian deliverance.

The commentary concludes by viewing Canaan typologically: "The gospel church is the New-Testament Canaan, watered with the Spirit in his gifts and graces," with heaven as the ultimate "good land."

## Summary of Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 9

### Main Theological Themes

Henry emphasizes the **radical unworthiness of human merit** before God, writing this principle "in capital letters at the head of their charter." The central theme is God's grace versus human righteousness - Israel's victory comes "not for your sake" (Ezek. 36:32) but through divine mercy. Henry draws parallels to Christian salvation, noting that "our gaining possession of the heavenly Canaan...must be ascribed to God's grace and not to our own merit: in Christ we have both righteousness and strength."

The commentary highlights **divine sovereignty in judgment and mercy**. God destroys the Canaanites for their wickedness while blessing Israel to fulfill His covenant promises to the patriarchs. Henry observes: "All those whom God rejects are rejected for their own wickedness: but none of those whom he accepts are accepted for their own righteousness."

### Key Practical Applications  

Henry warns against **spiritual pride and self-righteousness**. He notes how "it is hard to bring people from a conceit of their own merit, and yet very necessary that it be done." The commentary emphasizes remembering past failures to prevent pride: "We are apt to forget our provocations...and have need to be often put in mind of them, that we may never entertain any conceit of our own righteousness."

The **necessity of human effort combined with divine dependence** emerges as crucial: "We must do our endeavour in dependence upon God's grace, and we shall have that grace if we do our endeavour."

### Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-3**: Henry contrasts Moses's description of formidable enemies with the earlier evil spies' report - both acknowledge the same dangers, but Moses's account drives people "to God" rather than "from God."

**Verses 4-6**: The threefold emphasis on "not for thy righteousness" demonstrates how difficult it is to root out human pride in spiritual matters.

**Verses 8-21**: The golden calf incident serves as the prime example of Israel's unworthiness. Henry notes the sin was "exceedingly sinful" due to its timing and location - committed while "the mountain was yet burning before their eyes."

### Cross-References

Henry connects the passage to **Romans 3:19-20** regarding universal guilt preventing self-justification, **Romans 11:28** on being "beloved for their fathers' sakes," and **Ephesians 1:9,11** showing how "boasting is for ever excluded." He references **Ezekiel 36:32** as the charter principle and **Numbers 13:28,33** comparing the spies' reports.

The commentary consistently draws parallels between Israel's inheritance of Canaan and Christian salvation, emphasizing that both depend entirely on divine grace rather than human merit.

Chapter 10
----------------------------------------
# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy Chapter 10 Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

**Divine Grace Overcoming Human Sin**: Henry emphasizes how "God's goodness took occasion from their badness to make him the more illustrious." Despite Israel's rebellion with the golden calf, God demonstrated His mercy through reconciliation and covenant renewal. This central theme shows that God's faithfulness transcends human unfaithfulness.

**The Law as Grace**: Henry presents the renewed stone tablets not merely as legal requirements but as tokens of God's favor. He notes that "God's putting his law in our hearts is both our reconciliation to God and the best earnest of our happiness in him." The law prepares hearts through conviction, while grace writes it upon them.

**Christ as Fulfillment**: Henry draws clear typological connections, particularly seeing Moses as "a type of Christ, who, as he ever lives making intercession for us, so he has all power both in heaven and in earth." He also presents Christ as the true ark "in which now our salvation is kept safely."

## Key Practical Applications

**Intergenerational Responsibility**: Henry applies the preservation of the law tablets to modern believers: "Thus we may say to the rising generation, 'God has entrusted us with Bibles, sabbaths, sacraments, &c., as tokens of his presence and favour, and there they be; we lodge them with you'" (referencing 2 Timothy 1:13-14).

**Heart Circumcision**: The call to "circumcise therefore the foreskin of your heart" represents the need for internal spiritual transformation rather than mere external compliance.

**Love for Strangers**: Henry emphasizes practical love, noting that "we must have a tender concern for all that share with us in the human nature" and "do good to all men" according to their necessities and our abilities.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-5 (Renewed Tablets)**: Henry observes that Moses hewed the tablets while God wrote upon them, illustrating how "the law prepares the heart by conviction and humiliation for the grace of God, but it is only that grace that then writes the law in it."

**Verse 12 (What Does God Require?)**: Henry presents this as rhetorical mastery: "Moses brings it in like an orator, with an appeal to his auditors." He emphasizes that God's requirements are "highly just and reasonable in itself and of unspeakable benefit and advantage to you."

**Verses 17-19 (God's Character)**: Henry highlights God as both transcendent ("God of gods, and Lord of lords") and immanent (caring for "the fatherless and widow"), making this the basis for human compassion toward strangers.

## Cross-References and Biblical Connections

Henry connects the chapter to multiple scriptures:
- **Exodus 37:1** regarding Bezaleel's construction of the ark
- **Psalm 116:12** ("What shall I render?") as parallel to Moses' rhetorical question
- **Revelation 14:6-7** showing that fearing God remains gospel duty
- **2 Timothy 1:13-14** for transmitting truth to future generations
- **Psalm 146:9** regarding God's care for strangers

**Conclusion**: Henry frames the entire chapter as demonstrating God's "great kindness to Israel" that creates corresponding "obligations they lay under to fear, and love, and serve God." # Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 11: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

**Covenant Obedience and Divine Blessing**: Henry emphasizes the fundamental connection between loving God and keeping His commandments, noting that "love will work in obedience, and that only is acceptable obedience which flows from a principle of love" (referencing 1 John 5:3). This establishes obedience not as mere legalism but as love's natural expression.

**Divine Providence and Dependence**: A central theme is God's providence, particularly shown through Canaan's dependence on heavenly rain versus Egypt's reliance on the Nile. Henry observes that "the closer dependence we have upon God the more cheerful we should be in our obedience to him." He emphasizes how "mercies bring with them the greatest comfort and sweetness when we see them coming from heaven."

**Historical Remembrance as Foundation**: Henry stresses how God's past deliverances should motivate present obedience, addressing the elders who witnessed Egypt's plagues and wilderness rebellions: "God's mercies to us when we were young we should remember and retain the impressions of when we are old."

## Key Practical Applications

**Religious Education and Family Discipleship**: Henry emphasizes the practical commands for teaching children, writing God's words on doorposts, and constant meditation. This creates a comprehensive approach to spiritual formation within households and communities.

**Trust in Divine Provision**: Rather than teaching military strategy, Moses emphasizes keeping God's commandments as true strength: "let them keep God's commandments, and their religion, while they are true to it, will be their strength, and secure their success."

**Vigilance Against Spiritual Deception**: Henry warns that "all that forsake God...will find themselves wretchedly deceived to their own destruction," emphasizing the need for constant spiritual watchfulness.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 10-12** receive extensive treatment, contrasting Egypt's labor-intensive irrigation with Canaan's rain-dependence. Henry notes this difference "saved them a great deal of labour" while directing them "to look upwards to God."

**Verses 13-15** on seasonal rains represent "all the seasonable blessings which God would bestow upon them, especially spiritual comforts."

**Verses 16-17** on the consequences of idolatry emphasize that apostasy brings both divine wrath and natural consequences: "the heaven would withhold its rain, and then of course the earth would not yield its fruit."

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry weaves numerous biblical connections throughout: **1 John 5:3** (love and obedience), **Isaiah 43:3** (Egypt given as ransom), **Acts 14:17** (rain from heaven), **Hosea 14:5** (God as dew to Israel), **Psalm 104:13-15** (fruits for man's service), and **Zechariah 14:18** (Egypt's lack of rain). These references demonstrate the commentary's integration of biblical theology across testaments.

**Conclusion**: Henry presents Deuteronomy 11 as Moses's urgent final appeal, emphasizing that obedience rooted in love brings blessing, while apostasy inevitably leads to judgment—themes that resonate throughout Scripture's covenant theology.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 12: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry views this chapter as Moses's exposition of the Second Commandment, emphasizing God's jealousy for proper worship. The central theological principle emerges from "those great original truths, That there is a God, and that there is but one God" - leading to the fundamental law that God alone must be worshipped in His appointed way.

The unity of worship location serves as "token of their belief of those two great truths...That there is one God, and one Mediator between God and man" (1 Tim. 2:5). Henry sees this as an Old Testament intimation of Christ as the "one only way of approach to God and communion with him, in and by the Messiah."

## Key Practical Applications

**Spiritual Vigilance**: Henry emphasizes that religion isn't just for times of hardship but must continue "when we come to possess a good land...when we are full of business as well as when we have nothing else to do." Believers must maintain worship throughout prosperity and "all the days, as long as you live upon the earth."

**Complete Separation from Idolatry**: Christians must "abhor that which is evil before there can be a steady adherence to that which is good" (Rom. 12:9). Henry warns against transferring pagan practices into Christian worship, even "under colour of beautifying and improving it."

**Care for Ministry**: The repeated warnings about not forsaking the Levites apply to supporting God's appointed ministers today.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 2-3** (Destroying idolatrous places): Henry notes the absence of idol-temples suggests Moses's tabernacle was "the first habitation that ever was made for religious uses." Pagan worship in groves concealed "works of darkness which could not bear the light."

**Verse 8** ("every man whatsoever is right in his own eyes"): This warns against individualistic worship - people cannot determine their own forms of worship based on personal preference.

**Verses 15-16, 20-25** (Blood prohibition): The restriction that "the blood is the life" maintains the sanctity of life principle even in permitted meat consumption.

## Cross-References and Connections

Henry connects this chapter to Romans 12:9 regarding abhorring evil, and 1 Timothy 2:5 linking the one worship place to one God and one Mediator. He references the kingdom of God being established "upon the ruins of the devil's kingdom" since "there cannot stand together, nor can there be any communion between Christ and Belial."

The emphasis on centralized worship prefigures New Testament unity in Christ, while the careful distinction between sacred and common food consumption establishes principles that echo through biblical dietary laws and their spiritual significance.

## Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 13 Summary

### Main Theological Themes

Matthew Henry identifies **idolatry** as the central peril addressed in Deuteronomy 13. ### Cross-References

Henry connects this passage to **Galatians 1:8** regarding unchangeable gospel truth, **2 Thessalonians 2:9** concerning lying wonders, **Proverbs 1:10** about resisting enticement, **1 Corinthians 5:12-13** on church discipline, and **2 Peter 2:21** regarding apostasy's severity.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 14: Summary

On **verse 1** (cutting): Henry compares God's prohibition to "a parent's charge to his little children...Children, you shall not cut yourselves" - God protects us from self-harm through seemingly restrictive commands.

On **dietary laws**: Henry notes these were clearly temporary since "what they might not eat themselves they might give to a stranger" (v. 21), demonstrating their ceremonial rather than moral nature.

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects Israel's adoption to **Ephesians 1:4** regarding believers being chosen "that they might be so by his grace." The mourning restrictions relate to **1 Thessalonians 4:13** about not grieving "as those that have no hope." He references **1 Timothy 4:4** showing dietary laws are "now antiquated and repealed" since "every creature of God is good."

The commentary emphasizes that Israel's physical distinctiveness foreshadowed the spiritual separation of New Testament believers, with practical holiness flowing from positional holiness as God's chosen people.

## Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 15: Summary

### Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry emphasizes three primary spiritual themes in this chapter. ### Key Practical Applications

On **cheerful giving**, Henry emphasizes that "God loves a cheerful giver"—believers should give "freely" without grudging, viewing charity as honor to God rather than personal loss. For **social justice**, he advocates preventing "extreme poverty" that would bring "reproach of their nation and religion."

**Verses 1-6**: Henry sees debt release as either permanent forgiveness or temporary suspension, favoring permanent release for truly poor Israelites who borrowed for subsistence rather than commerce.

**Verses 7-11**: He highlights the phrase "open thy hand wide" as expressing generous liberality beyond mere token giving. The promise that "poor shall never cease" provides perpetual opportunity for charity.

**Verses 12-18**: Hebrew servant laws demonstrate that "God's Israel were a free people" while requiring masters to provide startup resources—"furnish them liberally"—upon release.

### Cross-References to Other Scripture

### Conclusion

Henry presents Deuteronomy 15 as a comprehensive social welfare system grounded in theological principles. The sabbatical release laws demonstrate God's concern for economic justice while prefiguring gospel grace, requiring believers to trust divine providence over human calculation in charitable giving.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy Chapter 17: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Matthew Henry identifies four primary concerns in this chapter, all centering on **maintaining the purity and authority of God's covenant relationship with Israel**. The overarching theme is that God demands excellence and exclusivity in worship, justice, and leadership.

Henry emphasizes that **idolatry represents "spiritual adultery" that "breaks the marriage bond"** between God and Israel. He notes that while other sins transgress commands, idolatry specifically "transgresses the covenant" itself. The commentary stresses God's demand for perfection in sacrifice as a type of Christ, "who is a Lamb without blemish or spot."

## Key Practical Applications

**Worship Purity**: Henry warns against offering anything less than our best to God, noting that "God is the best of beings, and therefore whatsoever he is served with ought to be the best in its kind."

**Civil Obedience**: The commentary strongly emphasizes submission to legitimate authority, stating it is "for the honour of God and the welfare of a people that the authority of the higher power be supported." Henry argues that rebellion against proper authority stems from "a spirit of contradiction and opposition of God."

**Leadership Accountability**: Regarding kingship, Henry stresses that even kings must submit to divine law, warning that power without divine guidance leads to corruption and oppression.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verse 1 (Sacrificial Purity)**: Henry explains that blemished sacrifices are "an abomination to God" because Old Testament sacrifices were "types of Christ" who is perfectly pure. He references Malachi 1:8, noting how later Israel violated this principle.

**Verses 2-7 (Idolatry Punishment)**: Henry provides four characteristics of idolatry: (1) "what God had not commanded," (2) "wickedness in the sight of God," (3) "a transgression of the covenant," and (4) "abomination in Israel."

**Verses 8-13 (Judicial Appeals)**: Henry describes the appeal system where difficult cases move from local courts to "the supreme power" - judges, high priests, or councils of priests and Levites. He emphasizes that their decisions carry "divine promise" and "divine institution."

**Verses 14-17 (Kingship Limitations)**: The commentary notes that while God permits monarchy, He severely limits royal power to prevent corruption and ensure the king remains subject to divine law.

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry extensively connects this passage to broader biblical themes:

- **1 Peter 1:19** - Links sacrificial purity to Christ's sinlessness
- **Malachi 1:8** - Shows later violation of sacrificial laws
- **Numbers 35:30** - References the two-witness requirement
- **Matthew 18:16** - New Testament application of witness requirements
- **Hebrews 10:28-29** - Uses judicial authority principles to explain punishment for rejecting Christ

The commentary also references Jethro's advice to Moses and connects the authority structure to New Testament teachings about submission to governing authorities.

Henry concludes that these laws demonstrate God's concern for maintaining both the **purity of worship** and the **proper order of society**, with all authority ultimately flowing from and accountable to divine law.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 18: Summary

Henry provides rich commentary on the priests' inheritance, noting that "those that have God for their inheritance, according to the new covenant, should not be greedy of great things in the world." Henry connects these passages to broader scriptural themes. He references Leviticus 7:32-34 regarding priestly portions and links the prohibition against occult practices to Leviticus 18:21. He draws parallels between the Gentile warnings here and Paul's instruction in Ephesians 4:17-20 about not walking "as the Gentiles walked."

# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 19: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Matthew Henry organizes Deuteronomy 19 around three commandments: the sixth ("Thou shalt not kill"), eighth ("Thou shalt not steal"), and ninth ("Thou shalt not bear false witness"). The chapter demonstrates God's careful balance between justice and mercy, establishing systems that protect both the innocent and ensure proper punishment for the guilty.

**Cities of Refuge as Types of Christ**: Henry's most significant theological insight connects the cities of refuge to Christ as our spiritual refuge. He emphasizes that "Christ is not a refuge at a distance" but readily accessible, with "the gospel brings salvation to our door." The detailed provisions for safe passage to these cities become a powerful metaphor for how ministers should guide people to Christ, "ready to remove their prejudices, and help them over their difficulties."

**Divine Justice and Mercy**: The laws reveal God's perfect justice—distinguishing between accidental killing and willful murder—while showing His mercy toward those guilty of unintentional harm. Henry notes that even "sins of ignorance indeed do expose us to the wrath of God, but there is relief provided, if by faith and repentance we make use of it."

## Key Practical Applications

**Property Rights and Honest Dealings**: The landmark law (verse 14) extends beyond literal boundaries to forbid "any fraudulent arts or practices" and "sowing of discord among neighbours." Henry applies this to modern contexts including forged documents and altered property markers.

**Legal Safeguards**: The requirement for multiple witnesses demonstrates God's concern for justice in human courts, preventing false accusations while ensuring proper evidence for convictions.

**Civil Order**: Henry emphasizes that these laws support stable government and social harmony, noting how "the divine law takes care of men's rights and properties, and has made a hedge about them."

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-10 (Cities of Refuge)**: Henry details how "the way must be prepared that led to the city of refuge," describing ancient Jewish practices of maintaining roads and posting signs reading "Miklat, Miklat—Refuge, Refuge." This becomes his central metaphor for gospel ministry.

**Verses 11-13 (No Refuge for Murderers)**: He draws a sharp spiritual parallel: "in Jesus Christ there is no refuge for presumptuous sinners, that go on still in their trespasses." True refuge requires fleeing *from* sin, not seeking shelter *in* sin.

The commentary masterfully demonstrates how Old Testament civil and ceremonial laws reveal eternal spiritual principles, with practical wisdom for both individual conduct and societal justice.

# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 22 Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry presents this chapter as demonstrating God's comprehensive care, noting that while "men's laws commonly do not" concern themselves with small matters, "because God's providence extends itself to the smallest affairs, his precepts do so." The overarching theme is that believers should live "in the fear of the Lord" even in minute details, as these seemingly small statutes are "to be accounted great things."

The chapter emphasizes **moral order and distinction** - preserving boundaries that God established in creation. **Compassionate Living:** The bird's nest law (vv. 6-7) teaches both mercy toward creatures and consideration for the vulnerable. ## Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 23: Summary

### Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Matthew Henry identifies the central purpose of Deuteronomy 23 as preserving both physical and spiritual purity within Israel's covenant community. The chapter addresses **holiness as separation** - excluding those who would compromise the congregation's integrity while maintaining compassion for the genuinely oppressed.

Henry emphasizes that **God's presence demands purity**, noting the theological principle that "the Lord thy God walketh in the midst of thy camp" (v. 14). This divine presence requires both ceremonial cleanliness and moral uprightness. The commentary reveals how external regulations pointed to deeper spiritual truths about **maintaining fellowship with a holy God**.

### Key Practical Applications

**For Military Conduct**: Henry stresses that soldiers must "keep themselves from every wicked thing" during warfare, as "sin takes off the edge of valour; guilt makes men cowards." He advocates for military reforms during wartime, emphasizing that success depends on spiritual preparation.

**For Social Justice**: The protection of fugitive slaves demonstrates practical mercy - Israel must not return escaped servants to abusive masters but allow them to "dwell with thee...where it liketh him best." Henry sees this as teaching compassion toward the oppressed.

**For Personal Integrity**: The laws against usury among fellow Israelites and the strict requirements for keeping vows emphasize **covenant loyalty and trustworthiness** in daily relationships.

### Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-8 (Exclusion Laws)**: Henry explains that exclusion from "the congregation of the Lord" likely meant restrictions on marriage, religious participation, or holding office rather than complete rejection. He notes the graduated restoration for Edomites and Egyptians (third generation) versus permanent exclusion of Ammonites and Moabites, emphasizing that **family relationships deserve special consideration**: "The unkindness of near relations...should with us, for that reason, because of the relation, be first forgiven."

**Verses 9-14 (Camp Purity)**: Henry draws profound spiritual lessons from seemingly mundane hygiene requirements, teaching "modesty and decorum," practical cleanliness, and most importantly, **reverence for divine presence**. These external requirements symbolized the need for "inward purity of soul."

**Verses 19-20 (Usury Laws)**: The distinction between lending practices toward fellow Israelites versus foreigners reflects covenant obligation - economic relationships must embody brotherly love within the community.

### Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry extensively connects these laws to broader biblical themes:

- **Isaiah 56:3-5**: Promises spiritual blessings to eunuchs who remain faithful despite exclusion
- **Nehemiah 13:1-2**: Post-exile application of separation laws
- **Leviticus 15:16**: Ceremonial purity requirements
- **Psalm 66:18**: "If I regard iniquity in my heart, the Lord will not hear me" - linking sin to military defeat
- **1 Samuel 7:3**: Reformation preceding military victory

Henry also references the principle that God judges people "according to their endeavours" (Psalm 28:4), noting that the Moabites' failed attempt to curse Israel through Balaam was still held against them despite God's intervention.

The commentary masterfully weaves together ceremonial law, social justice, and spiritual principles, showing how Old Testament regulations pointed toward the character of God and the nature of true holiness in community life.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 24: Summary

Creditors cannot take millstones (livelihood), must not enter borrowers' homes, and must return poor men's bedclothes nightly. Henry extensively connects these laws to broader biblical principles. He references **Matthew 19:3-7** regarding Christ's teaching on divorce, **Leviticus 13-14** for leprosy regulations, and **Exodus 22:26-27** for pledge laws. # Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 25: Summary

Matthew Henry presents Deuteronomy 25 as a comprehensive guide for maintaining justice, mercy, and divine order in society. Henry demonstrates how these Old Testament laws reveal enduring principles of justice, mercy, and practical righteousness that transcend their immediate historical context.

## Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 26: Summary

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 27: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry presents Deuteronomy 27 as Moses establishing "outward means" to reinforce God's law through memory aids and emotional engagement. The chapter serves two primary purposes: **helping their memories** so they wouldn't "forget the law as a strange thing," and **moving their affections** so they wouldn't treat the law as "a light thing."

The theological centerpiece is the **covenant relationship** between God and Israel. Henry emphasizes that "this day thou hast become the people of the Lord thy God," highlighting how privileges create obligations: "Privileges should be improved as engagements to duty. Should not a people be ruled by their God?"

A crucial theme is **redemption from the law's curse** through sacrifice. Henry notes that the altar on Mount Ebal (where curses were pronounced) signifies "that through Christ we are redeemed from the curse of the law."

## Key Practical Applications

**Authority and Leadership**: Henry stresses that "all that have interest in others, or power over them, should use it for the support and furtherance of religion." He emphasizes that effective religious instruction requires cooperation between "supreme power," "inferior magistrates," "ministers," and "masters of families."

**Public Witness**: The stone monument with plastered law writings demonstrates that "the word of God needs not to be set off by the art of man" but should be written "very plainly, that he who runs may read it."

**Word and Prayer Balance**: The combination of written law and altar sacrifice shows that "word and prayer must go together" - God speaks through His word, while people respond through worship and sacrifice.

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 2-8** (Stone Monument): Henry interprets the simple plastered stones as demonstrating that God's word needs no human embellishment. The inscription of "all the words of this law" serves as entry conditions into Canaan - "write it as the conditions of thy entry."

**Verses 5-7** (Altar Requirements): The unhewn stones prefigure Christ as "a stone cut out of the mountain without hands" - natural, unartificial, yet chosen by God. The altar placement near the written law provides comfort, since "we could not look with any comfort upon the law...if it were not for the great sacrifice."

**Verses 15-26** (Curses): Henry notes these curses target both **public sins** (idolatry) and **secret sins** (family dishonor, sexual immorality, violence), emphasizing that God sees all violations whether public or private.

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects the unhewn altar stones to **1 Kings 18:31-32** (Elijah's altar) and **Daniel 2:34-35** (Christ as the stone cut without hands). He references **Exodus 24:4** regarding the original Sinai covenant ceremony as a parallel solemnity.

The covenant meal references **Genesis 31:46-47** (Laban and Jacob's covenant feast) and **Joshua 24:26** (memorial stones). He also connects the blessing/curse structure to **Galatians 4:31** regarding children of promise.

**Habakkuk 2:2** supports the "very plainly" written requirement, while **Hebrews 11:16** explains God's faithfulness to His covenant promises.

Henry concludes that this solemn ceremony was designed to make "a deep impression upon them" regarding "the great things of God's law" - combining visual, auditory, and participatory elements to engage the whole person in covenant commitment.

# Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 28: Summary

## Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Matthew Henry presents this chapter as "a very large exposition of two words" - blessing and curse. His central theological theme emphasizes the covenant relationship between obedience and divine favor. **Verses 3-6**: He notes the comprehensive nature of blessing covering all circumstances - "Whether their habitation was in town or country...they should be preserved from the dangers and have the comforts of their condition."

**Verse 12**: Rain is called God's "good treasure," emphasizing divine provision in natural processes.

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry connects this chapter to **Leviticus 26**, noting both set forth "life and death, good and evil." He references **Matthew 25:37** regarding blessings overtaking the righteous, **Matthew 6:33** about seeking God's kingdom first, **Romans 16:25** on God's power to establish believers, and **Proverbs 10:4, 22** on divine blessing upon diligent work.

# Matthew Henry Commentary on Deuteronomy 29: Summary

**Universal inclusion**: The covenant encompasses all social levels - from "captains" to "hewers of wood and drawers of water" - demonstrating that "none are too great to come under the bonds of the covenant" and "none are too mean to inherit the blessings."

On **verse 4**, Henry offers a nuanced interpretation: God's not giving them "a heart to perceive" doesn't make Him responsible for their spiritual blindness, but rather highlights their rejection of available grace. "He would have gathered us and we would not."

Henry connects the covenant renewal to **Exodus 24:4**, noting the similar ceremonial solemnity. He references **Luke 19:9** regarding strangers being considered "sons of Abraham" when they join God's covenant, showing early inclusion of Gentiles.

The judgment warnings are linked to **Sodom and Gomorrah**, demonstrating that covenant privilege doesn't guarantee immunity from divine wrath when the covenant is violated. The commentary presents this chapter as both historical covenant renewal and timeless principles for how God relates to His people through grace, requiring faithful response while maintaining His sovereign initiative in salvation.

# Matthew Henry Commentary Summary: Deuteronomy Chapter 30

**Verses 1-10**: Henry details the "exceedingly great and precious promises" conditioned on repentance, including God's compassion, restoration from captivity, heart circumcision, and the transfer of curses to enemies. **Prayer and Action Combined**: Regarding Judah's blessing, Henry observes that "The voice of prayer should always be attended with the hand of endeavour, and then we may expect prosperity."

## Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 2-3**: The "fiery law" represents God's word that "if it be received, it is melting, warming, purifying, and burns up the dross of corruption; if it be rejected, it hardens, sears, torments, and destroys."

**Verse 3**: "All his saints are in thy hand" - Henry beautifully explains that God's hand "is large enough, and strong enough, to hold them all, and we may be sure that none can pluck them out of his hand" (referencing John 10:28).

**Verse 6**: Though Reuben "had lost the honour of his birthright," Moses still blesses him, showing "we should not insult over those that are disgraced, nor desire to perpetuate marks of infamy."

## Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry extensively connects this passage to other biblical texts: He references Habakkuk 3:3-4 and Psalm 18:7-9 for God's glorious appearance, cites Jude 14 regarding the "holy myriads," and connects the law-giving to Acts 7:53 and Hebrews 2:2. He draws parallels between Moses' blessing and Jacob's blessing of his sons (Genesis 49), and links Judah's blessing to messianic prophecies, particularly "to him should the gathering of the people be" (Genesis 49:10), seeing David as "a type of Christ."

This commentary masterfully weaves together Moses' historical blessing with enduring theological truths about God's faithfulness, the value of His word, and His eternal protection of His people.

## Matthew Henry's Commentary on Deuteronomy 34: Summary

### Main Theological Themes and Interpretations

Henry emphasizes **divine sovereignty and faithful stewardship** as central themes. Moses exemplifies perfect submission to God's will, accepting his exclusion from Canaan despite his faithful service. The commentary highlights the **transition of leadership** under divine providence and Moses' role as "the servant of the Lord"—a title Henry notes represents both piety and usefulness in God's service.

The **typological significance** pervades Henry's interpretation. Moses' view of Canaan represents the Old Testament saints' distant vision of Christ's kingdom, while Canaan itself typifies heaven (Hebrews 11:16). Henry writes that Moses had "comfortable prospects of the glory to be revealed" through this divine vision.

### Key Practical Applications

Henry draws several practical lessons:

**Willing submission to death**: Moses climbing Mount Pisgah demonstrates that "those that through grace are well acquainted with another world...need not be afraid to leave this." Christians should view death as "ascension" rather than defeat.

**Trust in divine timing**: Though Moses seemed to die prematurely—his ancestors lived longer and Israel needed his leadership—believers must accept that "God's servants must die that they may rest from their labours, receive their recompense, and make room for others."

**Contentment with God's provision**: Henry encourages believers facing unfulfilled earthly hopes to find comfort knowing "those that shall come after us...will enter that promised land."

### Notable Verse-by-Verse Insights

**Verses 1-4**: Moses ascending alone to Pisgah symbolizes death as upward movement. The supernatural vision of all Canaan required divine assistance—"it is he that gives the spirit of wisdom as well as the spirit of revelation."

**Verse 5**: The phrase "died according to the word of the Lord" (literally "at the mouth of the Lord") prompts Henry to note Jewish tradition of Moses dying "with a kiss from the mouth of God"—indicating a peaceful, willing death.

**Verse 6**: God's burial of Moses "by the ministry of angels" made this funeral "though very private, yet very magnificent." The unknown burial location prevented idolatrous worship of Moses' remains.

**Verse 7**: Moses' undimmed sight and unabated strength at 120 years demonstrates God's sustaining power for His servants until their appointed time.

### Cross-References to Other Scripture

Henry extensively connects this passage to broader biblical themes: Abraham's vision of Christ's day (Hebrews 11:13), believers being "dead to the law" to be "married to another" (Romans 7:4), Christ's transfiguration where Moses appeared (connecting to his unique burial), and the contest over Moses' body between Michael and the devil (Jude 9). He parallels Moses' submission with Christ's prayer in Gethsemane: "Father, thy will be done."

The commentary presents Moses' death as both ending and beginning—closing the Mosaic era while pointing toward Christ's superior covenant, encouraging believers to face death with faith and hope.
