Leviticus - Matthew Henry Commentary Summaries
================================================

Chapter 1: The Burnt Offering
----------------------------------------
Henry establishes that Leviticus opens with sacrificial law because sacrifice is the foundation of approaching God. The burnt offering represents complete consecration - the entire animal was consumed on the altar, symbolizing total devotion to God.

Key observations:
- God speaks from the tabernacle (the mercy-seat), not Sinai - the remedial law of sacrifice comes gently, foreshadowing gospel grace
- Natural religion already inclined humanity toward sacrifice; revealed religion provides proper form and meaning
- The offering must be male and without blemish, typifying Christ's perfection and the sincerity required of believers
- Voluntary offering is essential - what pleases God must come from love, not constraint

The laying of hands on the sacrifice signifies three things: (1) transfer of ownership to God, (2) acknowledgment that the offerer deserves death, and (3) dependence on the sacrifice as a type of Christ's atonement. The blood sprinkled on the altar represents Christ's satisfaction of divine justice.

Henry emphasizes God's accommodation of the poor: bullocks for the wealthy, sheep or goats for the middle class, and turtledoves or pigeons for the poor. The poor man's dove is equally "a sweet savour" to God as the rich man's bullock - God accepts according to what a person has, not what they lack.

The sacrifice being burned entirely teaches that nothing given to God is wasted. The "sweet savour" indicates God's acceptance and rest in the reconciliation these offerings prefigured.

Chapter 2: The Meat (Grain) Offering
----------------------------------------
Henry explains the meat-offering (grain offering) served two purposes: (1) accommodation for the poor who could not afford animals, and (2) acknowledgment of God's provision of daily bread. This offering was like a "quitrent" - a token of dependence on God as owner and benefactor.

Key regulations:
- Fine flour mixed with oil, with frankincense added for burning
- Could be unbaked, baked in oven, on a pan, or in a frying pan
- A portion ("memorial") was burned on the altar; the rest went to the priests
- Leaven forbidden - symbolizing pride, hypocrisy, malice (things that swell and sour)
- Honey forbidden - it turns bitter, and Gentiles used it in idol worship
- Salt required in all offerings - "the salt of the covenant," symbol of friendship and preservation

Henry notes the fire represents fervent devotion, and frankincense represents Christ's intercession that perfumes our services. First-fruits offerings were especially pleasing to God - even "green ears of corn" from the young, if offered with sincere hearts, are acceptable.

Chapter 3: The Peace Offering
----------------------------------------
Henry distinguishes peace-offerings from burnt-offerings: burnt-offerings expressed pure adoration (wholly consumed), while peace-offerings expressed fellowship and gratitude (divided between God, priest, and offerer). "Peace" signifies both reconciliation and prosperity.

Two purposes for peace-offerings:
1. Supplication - backing prayer with sacrifice when seeking mercy
2. Thanksgiving - grateful acknowledgment for mercies received

Key observations:
- Unlike burnt-offerings, either male or female animals were acceptable - "God looks at the heart, not the sex"
- The fat and inwards burned on the altar represent both offering our best affections to God and mortifying corrupt lusts
- "All the fat is the Lord's" - the best must always be devoted to God
- Blood and fat were forbidden as food to preserve honor of what was sacred

Henry emphasizes Christ is our peace-offering: through Him we have communion with God, and the peace-sacrifice prefigured reconciliation accomplished at the cross.

Chapter 4: The Sin Offering
----------------------------------------
Henry introduces the sin-offering as a new development beyond patriarchal sacrifices - "the law being added because of transgressions." This offering specifically addressed sins committed through ignorance, not presumptuous sins (which merited being "cut off").

The chapter distinguishes four categories of offenders, each with different requirements:
1. High priest - must bring a bullock (most costly), blood sprinkled before the veil
2. Whole congregation - elders lay hands on a bullock for national sins
3. Ruler - brings a male kid; blood applied to brazen altar only
4. Common person - brings a female kid or lamb

Key theological points:
- Sin is against "the commandments of the Lord" - human wills cannot define sin
- The church may err; it was never promised to be infallible
- Laying hands on the sacrifice signified: transfer of guilt, confession, and dependence on atonement
- Blood sprinkled seven times represents Christ's perfect satisfaction
- The carcass burned outside the camp foreshadows Christ suffering "outside the gate" (Heb. 13:11-13)

Henry concludes: even sins of ignorance require atonement. We must pray with David to be cleansed from "secret faults."

Chapter 5: The Trespass Offering
----------------------------------------
Henry distinguishes trespass-offerings from sin-offerings: sin-offerings were general, while trespass-offerings addressed specific violations. Three categories of trespass are enumerated:

1. Concealing testimony when adjured as a witness - refusing to speak truth under oath makes one "a partner with the sinner"
2. Touching ceremonial uncleanness and neglecting purification
3. Rash swearing - making oaths that prove unlawful or impossible to keep, creating a dilemma where both keeping and breaking the oath involve sin

Key principles:
- Confession must be particular ("I have sinned in that thing"), not vague generalities
- Both repentance and atonement are necessary; neither alone suffices
- God accommodates the poor: those unable to afford a lamb may bring two doves; those unable to afford doves may bring flour

The flour sin-offering had no oil or frankincense - its unsavoriness reminded the sinner never to "relish his sin again."

For trespasses against holy things (embezzling tithes, eating priests' portions), restitution with 20% interest was required, plus the trespass-offering. Even suspected violations required a sacrifice - "in doubtful cases we should take the safer side."

Chapter 6: Priestly Instructions on Offerings
----------------------------------------
Henry continues trespass-offering law, then transitions to instructions specifically for priests. Trespasses against neighbors are called "trespasses against the Lord" because wronging any person affronts the Law-maker.

Specific trespasses addressed:
- Denying a trust (claiming borrowed items as one's own)
- Defrauding a partner
- Robbery and lying about it
- Keeping found property and denying it under oath

Restitution must precede sacrifice: "Where wrong has been done restitution must be made; till it is made we cannot have the comfort of the forgiveness of the sin."

Priestly duties regarding burnt-offerings:
- The fire must never go out - "the fire from heaven" must be perpetually maintained
- Ashes must be reverently disposed of in a clean place
- Even these menial duties belong to the priests: "God's servants must think nothing below them but sin"

The perpetual fire symbolizes maintaining constant devotion - "though we be not always sacrificing, yet we must keep the fire of holy love always burning."

The priests' consecration meat-offering was wholly burnt (not eaten) because "the sins of the priests must be typically transferred to the altar."

Chapter 7: Laws of Peace-Offerings and Priests' Portions
----------------------------------------
Henry elaborates on peace-offerings, distinguishing three types:
1. Thanksgiving - for mercies received (recovery, deliverance, redemption)
2. Vow - fulfilling promises made in distress
3. Voluntary - accompanying prayers for future mercies

Key regulations:
- Thanksgiving offerings must be eaten the same day; vow offerings within two days
- Anything remaining on the third day must be burned - to prevent putrefaction, encourage generosity, and teach trust in providence
- Unclean persons eating holy food would be "cut off from the people"
- Fat and blood remain absolutely forbidden

The priest's portion (breast and right shoulder) symbolizes "the affections and the actions, which must be devoted to God's honor." Christ, our peace-offering, "feasts all his spiritual priests with the breast and shoulder" - the dearest love and strongest support.

Henry notes the priest who offered received the skin of burnt-offerings as wages: "He that did the work must have the wages... the more diligent we are in religion the more we shall reap of the advantages."

Chapter 8: Consecration of Aaron and His Sons
----------------------------------------
Henry describes the elaborate seven-day ordination ceremony for the priesthood, performed publicly before the congregation to establish the priests' authority and prevent later disputes.

The ceremony's elements:
1. Washing with water - purification from sin ("Christ washes those from their sins in his own blood whom he makes kings and priests")
2. Clothing in holy garments - signifying dignity and righteousness
3. Anointing with oil - the oil poured "plentifully" on Aaron's head typifies Christ receiving the Spirit "without measure"
4. Sin-offering (bullock) - ministers must first be reconciled to God themselves
5. Burnt-offering (ram) - dedication of themselves to God's service
6. Peace-offering (ram of consecration) - blood applied to ear, thumb, and toe, "marrying them to the altar"

Blood on the right ear, thumb, and great toe signified consecration of hearing, actions, and walk to God's service.

The seven-day period required constant attendance "day and night" - teaching that priesthood is "a good warfare" requiring full dedication. Henry applies this to gospel ministers who must be "clothed and anointed by the Spirit" and devoted entirely to service.

Chapter 9: The Priests Begin Ministry
----------------------------------------
Henry describes the "eighth day" - the first day of active ministry after the seven days of consecration. Moses promises that "the glory of the Lord shall appear unto you," motivating their service.

Key observations:
- Aaron offers a calf for his own sin-offering - Jewish writers suggest this reminded him of the golden calf sin
- He must first make atonement for himself, then for the people - "how can we expect to be accepted in prayers for others if we ourselves be not reconciled to God?"
- The regular morning burnt-offering was not omitted despite the special ceremonies - "our accustomed devotions must not be diminished"

After completing the sacrifices, Aaron "lifted up his hand toward the people, and blessed them" - typifying Christ who blessed his disciples at his ascension.

The climax: fire came from the Lord and consumed the sacrifice, signifying divine acceptance. This fire:
- Turned away God's wrath (consuming the sacrifice instead of the people)
- Entered communion with Israel (God "ate" his portion as they ate theirs)
- Kindled the perpetual altar fire that must never go out

The people responded with both joy (shouting) and reverence (falling on their faces) - "that is a sinful fear which drives us from God; a gracious fear makes us bow before him."

Chapter 10: Death of Nadab and Abihu
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this tragic event as a sobering counterpoint to the previous chapter's glory. Nadab and Abihu, Aaron's eldest sons, "offered strange fire before the Lord, which he commanded them not."

Their sins compounded:
1. Acting without orders - presuming to burn incense when not their turn
2. Using common fire instead of fire from the altar
3. Both going in together (only one priest should burn incense at a time)
4. Acting rashly, without reverence
5. Likely intoxicated (suggested by the subsequent prohibition of wine)
6. Presumptuous, not merely ignorant

Fire from the Lord killed them instantly - "the wages of this sin was death." Henry notes they died childless, their priestly garments becoming grave-clothes, symbolizing the eventual abolition of that priesthood.

Moses counsels Aaron: "I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified." Aaron's response: "Aaron held his peace" - a model of submission. Henry commends this: "When God corrects us for sin, it is our duty to be silent, not to quarrel with God."

The priests were forbidden to mourn outwardly - their service must continue. But the congregation was commanded to "bewail the burning which the Lord has kindled."

The chapter concludes with the prohibition against wine during ministry, lest they be unable to "put difference between holy and unholy."

Chapter 11: Clean and Unclean Animals
----------------------------------------
Henry explains the dietary laws as having multiple purposes:

1. Testing obedience - "God would thus try and exercise the obedience of his people in matters of daily occurrence"
2. Health concerns - many forbidden animals were unwholesome
3. Separation from nations - distinctive diet prevented familiar conversation with idolaters
4. Rejection of pagan worship - many unclean animals (swine, owl, eagle) were sacred to heathen deities

Rules for beasts: must both chew cud AND have divided hoof. Henry suggests symbolic meaning - chewing cud represents meditation/devotion, divided hoof represents good works/conversation. "Either without the other will not recommend us to God."

Fish: must have both fins and scales.

Birds: no general rule, but a list of forbidden species - mostly predators, solitary birds, and those used in divination.

Touching carcasses caused uncleanness until evening; earthen vessels that contacted them must be broken.

The conclusion grounds everything in holiness: "Be ye holy, for I am holy." Henry applies this to Christians: "Without holiness no man shall see the Lord." The gospel has repealed these dietary laws (Acts 10), but the moral principle remains: we must "sanctify ourselves and be holy."

Chapter 12: Purification After Childbirth
----------------------------------------
Henry explains that a woman was ceremonially unclean after childbirth - seven days for a male child, fourteen for a female (the reasons for this difference he leaves to "the will of the Law-maker").

This ceremonial uncleanness symbolized "the pollution of sin which we are all conceived and born in" (Psalm 51:5). The exclusion from the sanctuary "signified that our original corruption would have excluded us forever from the enjoyment of God if he had not graciously provided for our purifying."

The purification required:
1. A burnt-offering (lamb, or pigeon if poor) - thanksgiving for safe delivery
2. A sin-offering (same for rich and poor) - either completing ceremonial purification or atoning for impatience during labor

Henry notes that Mary, though her Son "was not conceived in sin as others," still fulfilled this law, bringing only pigeons - "so poor were Christ's parents." This demonstrated Christ being "made under the law, to redeem those under it."

The moral application: women who receive mercy in childbirth should acknowledge God's goodness and "continue in faith, and charity, and holiness, with sobriety."

Chapter 13: Laws for Diagnosing Leprosy
----------------------------------------
Henry explains leprosy as a ceremonial uncleanness rather than merely a disease - a divine plague inflicted by God's hand as judgment for sin (Miriam, Gehazi, Uzziah). The priest's role was judicial, not medical: to convict the leper, not cure him.

Leprosy symbolizes "the moral pollution of men's minds by sin, the leprosy of the soul." Key diagnostic principles:
- If skin-deep only, probably not leprosy; if deeper, unclean - "infirmities that consist with grace do not sink deep into the soul"
- If spreading, dangerous; if at a stay, hopeful - "if sin gets ground, they are going downhill"
- If proud raw flesh appears, certainly leprosy - "self-conceit, confidence in the flesh, and resistance to reproof"
- If eruption covers entire body, actually clean - free confession is better than concealment

The priest must not judge hastily but observe over fourteen days: "Let nothing be done suddenly."

The convicted leper must:
1. Acknowledge his condition - rend clothes, uncover head, cry "Unclean, unclean"
2. Warn others - prevent contagion
3. Dwell alone outside the camp

Henry notes: "The law only shows us our disease; the gospel shows us our help in Christ" (Luke 17:12-13, where lepers cry "Jesus, Master, have mercy on us").

The chapter also addresses leprosy in garments - symbolizing sin's corruption spreading to everything we touch.

Chapter 14: Cleansing of Lepers and Leprous Houses
----------------------------------------
Henry emphasizes that God cured the leprosy; the priest only certified the cure and performed cleansing rites. The priest must "go forth out of the camp to the leper" - a model for ministers reaching the excluded.

The cleansing ceremony employed two birds:
- One killed over running water in an earthen vessel (blood and water mixing, as from Christ's pierced side)
- One dipped in the blood and released alive
- Cedar (strength), scarlet wool (restored color), and hyssop (cleansing) were used

The slain bird typifies "Christ dying for our sins," the living bird "Christ rising again for our justification" - the dipping of the living bird in the slain bird's blood shows "the merit of Christ's death made his resurrection effectual."

The cleansed leper then brought offerings: trespass-offering, sin-offering, burnt-offering. Blood was applied to ear, thumb, and toe (as in priestly consecration), then oil over the blood - "blood seems to have been a token of forgiveness, the oil of healing."

For poor lepers, God accepted lesser offerings - "the poor are as welcome to God's altar as the rich."

Leprosy in houses required examination, and if persistent after scraping and replastering, the entire house must be demolished. This pictures "the leprosy of sin, if obstinate under methods of cure, will at last be the ruin of families and churches."

Chapter 15: Bodily Discharges and Ceremonial Uncleanness
----------------------------------------
Henry treats this chapter with appropriate delicacy, addressing laws about bodily issues that caused ceremonial uncleanness.

For men's issues (often consequences of immorality): the affected person was unclean, everything he touched became unclean, and after healing he must wait seven days then offer sin-offering and burnt-offering.

For women's issues (both regular and abnormal, like the woman healed in Mark 5): similar rules of uncleanness and purification applied.

The purposes of these laws:
1. Teaching privilege - Israel was "purified unto God a peculiar people"
2. Teaching duty - if involuntary pollutions made them unfit for worship, "much more abominable were they if they sinned against the light and law of nature"
3. Preventing death in uncleanness - "it is a dangerous thing to die in our uncleanness"
4. Honoring the tabernacle - maintaining reverence for God's dwelling

Henry applies to Christians: we should bless God we're not under such "carnal ordinances," carefully abstain from sin as defiling to conscience, and understand that "without holiness no man shall see the Lord."

Chapter 16: The Day of Atonement
----------------------------------------
This central chapter of Leviticus describes the annual Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur), which "had as much gospel in it as perhaps any of the appointments of the ceremonial law."

The timing: given after Nadab and Abihu's death - warning against irreverent approach, but also providing the way of safe access.

Key regulations:
- Only the high priest could enter the Most Holy Place, only on this one day
- He must wear plain linen garments, not his glorious vestments (Christ made atonement "not in the robes of his own peculiar glory, but the linen garments of our mortality")
- No one else could be in the tabernacle during atonement (Christ "tread the wine-press alone")

The ceremony:
1. High priest offers bullock for his own sins and his household
2. Two goats presented - lots cast to determine which was sacrificed, which was scapegoat
3. High priest enters Most Holy Place with incense (covering the mercy-seat with smoke) and blood
4. Blood sprinkled on mercy-seat for priests, then for people
5. Scapegoat: sins confessed over it, then sent into the wilderness "to a land not inhabited"

Henry's Christological interpretation:
- The slain goat typifies "Christ dying for our sins"
- The scapegoat typifies "Christ rising again for our justification"
- Christ is both the priest making atonement and the sacrifice by which it is made
- His entering heaven fulfills the high priest entering the Most Holy Place
- The incense represents Christ's intercession

The people must "afflict their souls" - fast and rest from labor. This was "a statute for ever" until fulfilled in Christ, whose "one offering" was sufficient and needed no repetition.

Chapter 17: Centralized Sacrifice and Prohibition of Blood
----------------------------------------
Henry explains this chapter preserves the honor of the Day of Atonement by establishing two prohibitions: (1) all sacrifices must be offered at the tabernacle door, and (2) no blood may be eaten.

On centralized sacrifice:
- Previously, patriarchs could build altars anywhere; this liberty had led to idolatry
- Israel had "sacrificed to demons" (hairy goats) - evil spirits who appeared in that form
- Now all sacrifices must come through the priests at God's altar
- Killing a sacrificial animal elsewhere was treated as murder: "blood shall be imputed to that man"

Reasons for centralization:
1. Preventing idolatry and superstition - priests ensured proper form
2. Securing the honor of God's temple and altar
3. Preserving unity - meeting at one altar promoted brotherly love

On blood prohibition:
- "The life of the flesh is in the blood" - God appointed blood for atonement
- Blood on the altar signified "the life of the sacrifice was given to God instead of the sinner's life"
- Eating blood would dishonor what made atonement sacred

Henry notes the ceremonial law is now superseded: "Christ's blood only" makes atonement, so the dietary restriction has ended. Yet the principle remains: we must revere Christ's blood and not "use it as a common thing" (Heb. 10:29).

Chapter 18: Laws Against Sexual Sin and Idolatry
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this chapter as moral law binding on Christians, being "consonant to the very light and law of nature." The chapter prohibits conformity to Egyptian and Canaanite practices.

The foundation: "I am the Lord your God" - repeated throughout as the basis of authority. The promise: "Which if a man do, he shall live in them" - obedience brings blessing, though now we "live by faith" rather than by merit.

Laws against incest:
- Marriage unites those who were separate; those already "one flesh by nature" cannot become so by institution
- Marriage creates equality; incest would confuse the natural order between parents/children, uncles/nieces
- Brothers and sisters forbidden to prevent "sinful familiarities" in youth and to enlarge friendship through marriage outside the family

Other prohibitions:
- Adultery
- Child sacrifice to Molech - "profaning the name of God" by giving children to demons while offering only cattle to Jehovah
- Sodomy and bestiality - "sins not to be named without the utmost abhorrence"

The warning: these sins defiled the Canaanites so that "the land itself vomiteth out her inhabitants." Israel would face the same fate if they adopted such practices. "Sinful customs are abominable customs" regardless of how common they become.

The remedy: "Keep my ordinance" - close adherence to God's ways preserves from gross sin.

Chapter 19: The Holiness Code
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this chapter a summary of the Ten Commandments, with "I am the Lord" repeated fifteen times. Most precepts here are moral law, permanently binding.

The great command: "Ye shall be holy: for I the Lord your God am holy" (v. 2). This is now "the law of Christ" (1 Pet. 1:15-16).

Key moral duties:
- Honor parents and keep sabbaths (vv. 3, 30)
- Leave gleanings for the poor - "works of piety must be attended with works of charity" (vv. 9-10)
- Do not steal, lie, or swear falsely (vv. 11-12)
- Pay laborers promptly - withholding wages "cries to heaven for vengeance" (v. 13)
- Do not curse the deaf or trip the blind - "the fear of God will restrain us from doing that which will not expose us to men's resentments" (v. 14)
- Judge impartially - neither favoring the poor from pity nor the mighty from fear (v. 15)
- Do not be a talebearer (v. 16)
- Rebuke your neighbor in love rather than harboring hatred (v. 17)
- "Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself" - the summary of the second table (v. 18)

On rebuke: "Friendly reproof is a duty we owe to one another." Not reproving is construed as hating our brother. We must not "suffer sin upon him."

On strangers: "Love him as thyself, for ye were strangers in Egypt." God's people must not despise foreigners but welcome them.

On honest weights: "He that sells is bound to give the full of the commodity" - defrauding under color of justice is worse than open theft.

Chapter 20: Penalties for Sin
----------------------------------------
Henry explains this chapter attaches penalties to previously stated laws, "that those who would not be deterred from sin by the fear of God might be deterred by the fear of punishment."

Capital crimes:
1. Sacrificing children to Molech (vv. 2-5) - stoning; if magistrates fail to act, God will "cut off" both the offender and those who "hide their eyes"
2. Cursing parents (v. 9) - "a direct breach of the fifth commandment as wilful murder is of the sixth"
3. Consulting mediums (vv. 6, 27) - spiritual adultery, giving to the devil honor due to God
4. Adultery (v. 10)
5. Various forms of incest (vv. 11-12, 14, 17)
6. Sodomy and bestiality (vv. 13, 15-16)

Some incestuous unions were punished with childlessness rather than death (vv. 19-21): "Those that keep not within the divine rules of marriage forfeit the blessings of marriage."

The call to holiness (vv. 7-8, 22-26):
- "Sanctify yourselves and be ye holy" - cleanse from pollution, consecrate to God's service
- "I am the Lord which sanctify you" - God's sanctifying grace is reason to pursue personal holiness
- Israel was "separated from other people" - the enclosure, not the common; they must preserve this distinction

The warning: The Canaanites "committed all these things, therefore I abhorred them." If Israel follows their ways, "the land would spue them out" likewise. "Those that sin like others must expect to smart like them."

Chapter 21: Qualifications for Priests
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this chapter as a "commandment for priests" (Mal. 2:1), requiring them to preserve the dignity of their priesthood. "Those whose office it is to instruct must do it by example as well as precept."

Concerning mourning (vv. 1-6):
- Priests could mourn for nearest relatives (parents, children, siblings, wife) but not for others
- Even in mourning, they must not use heathen customs (shaving head, cutting flesh)
- God honors natural affection but limits its expression: "Weeping must not hinder sowing"
- Reason: "They offered the bread of their God" - if servants are ill-behaved, it reflects on the master

Concerning marriage (vv. 7-9):
- Priests must not marry harlots, profane women, or divorced women
- "Ministers' children are profaned if they be unequally yoked with unbelievers"
- Priests' daughters who play the harlot must be burned: "doubly scandalous" in minister's children

Higher standards for the high priest (vv. 10-15):
- Must not mourn at all, even for parents: "His natural affection must be swallowed up in compassion to the ignorant"
- Must not leave the sanctuary for any funeral
- Must marry only a virgin of his own people - typifying Christ's bride, "a chaste virgin"
- "Christ must have our first love, our pure love, our entire love"

On physical blemishes (vv. 16-24):
- Priests with blemishes could eat the holy food but not serve at the altar
- God "made provision for the reputation of his altar"
- Gospel application: "Those are unworthy to be called Christians whose minds are blemished and deformed by any reigning vice"

Chapter 22: Holiness of Offerings
----------------------------------------
Henry continues the theme of priestly holiness, addressing ceremonial purity and quality of sacrifices.

Ceremonial purity for priests (vv. 1-9):
- Priests under uncleanness (leprosy, touching dead, etc.) must not eat holy things
- "That soul shall be cut off from my presence" - severe penalty for profaning sacred things
- "If we affront God in those very performances wherein we pretend to honour him... we shall make up but a bad account"

Who may eat holy things (vv. 10-16):
- Only priests and their households (including purchased servants)
- Daughters married to non-priests forfeit the right, but widows returning home regain it
- David and his men eating showbread shows the law could be "dispensed with in a case of necessity" - "Rituals must give way to morals"
- Application: Ministers "must not admit all, without distinction" to communion

Requirements for sacrifices (vv. 17-33):
- Must be without blemish: blind, lame, bruised, or blemished animals rejected
- Christ "a Lamb without blemish" - Pilate's declaration "I find no fault in this man" pronounced the sacrifice acceptable
- Animals must be at least eight days old - "Christ was sacrificed... in the prime of his time"
- Dam and young must not be killed same day - restraining cruelty
- Thank-offerings must be eaten same day

General principle: "God will be a loser in his glory by no man at last; but sooner or later will recover his right, either in the repentance of sinners or in their ruin."

Chapter 23: The Feasts of the Lord
----------------------------------------
Henry describes the sacred calendar as establishing "holy times" that only God can appoint: "Man may by his appointment make a good day, but it is God's prerogative to make a holy day."

The weekly Sabbath (v. 3):
- "A sabbath of rest, typifying our spiritual rest from sin, and in God"
- Must be observed "in all your dwellings" - family worship, not just public assembly
- "The sabbath of the Lord in our dwellings will be their beauty, strength, and safety"

The Passover and Unleavened Bread (vv. 4-14):
- Commemorates deliverance from Egypt
- The sheaf of firstfruits waved "on the morrow after the sabbath"
- Christ "rose from the dead on the very day that the first-fruits were offered"
- "We must always begin with God, begin our lives with him, begin every day with him"

Pentecost (vv. 15-22):
- Fifty days after Passover, celebrating completed harvest
- Two leavened loaves offered (unlike Passover's unleavened)
- Commemorated giving of the law at Sinai
- "The period and perfection of this feast was the pouring out of the Spirit upon the apostles" (Acts 2)
- Connected to charity: "Leave gleanings for the poor"

The seventh month solemnities:
1. Feast of Trumpets (vv. 23-25): Memorial blowing from sunrise to sunset; "typical of the preaching of the gospel, by which joyful sound souls were to be called in to serve God"
2. Day of Atonement (vv. 26-32): Complete rest, afflicting souls; "the humbling of our souls for sin... is work that requires the whole man"
3. Feast of Tabernacles (vv. 33-44): Dwelling in booths for seven days, remembering wilderness wanderings

On Tabernacles: "The more we are grieved and humbled for sin, the better qualified we are for the comforts of the Holy Ghost." The feast taught them "to sit loose to this world, as those that have here no continuing city."

Chapter 24: The Lamps, Showbread, and Blasphemy
----------------------------------------
Henry addresses practical worship and a narrative of judgment for blasphemy.

The lamps (vv. 1-4):
- Pure olive oil, beaten, provided by the people
- Priests must tend them morning and evening: "the work of ministers of the gospel to hold forth that word of life"
- "Though God has tied us to means, he has not tied himself to them"

The showbread (vv. 5-9):
- Twelve loaves for twelve tribes: "In our Father's house there is bread enough"
- Frankincense burned as memorial when bread was changed weekly
- Renewed every Sabbath: "Christ's ministers should provide new bread for his house every sabbath day"

The blasphemer (vv. 10-23):
- Son of an Egyptian father and Israelite mother (Shelomith of Dan)
- Quarreled and "blasphemed the name of the Lord"
- Held in custody until God's verdict sought - "Judges must deliberate"
- Sentenced to death by stoning outside the camp
- "Magistrates are the guardians of both tables"

Henry notes the irony that Stephen, "the first that died for the gospel, died by the abuse of this law; the martyr and the malefactor suffered the same death: but how vast the difference between them!"

Laws reiterated: Murder punished by death; lex talionis ("eye for eye"); same law for strangers as Israelites.

Chapter 25: Sabbatical Year and Jubilee
----------------------------------------
Henry presents laws showing God's peculiar ownership of the land and his care for Israel's liberty and property.

The Sabbatical Year (vv. 1-7):
- Every seventh year the land must rest: no sowing, no reaping, no pruning
- Spontaneous growth shared freely by all
- Purposes: (1) Showed God was their landlord; (2) Preserved land for posterity; (3) Gave leisure for religion; (4) Taught generosity; (5) Fostered dependence on providence
- "Typified the spiritual rest which all believers enter into through Christ"

The Jubilee (vv. 8-22):
- Every fiftieth year (after seven sabbaths of years)
- Proclaimed by trumpet on Day of Atonement - "when their peace was made with God, then liberty was proclaimed"
- "Proclaim liberty throughout all the land" - restoration of property and freedom
- Property could never be permanently alienated; slaves freed
- Christ "sent his apostles to proclaim it with the trumpet of the everlasting gospel"

Land laws (vv. 23-34):
- "The land is mine; you are strangers and sojourners with me"
- Kinsman-redeemer (goel) could buy back sold land - "typified Christ, who assumed our nature, that he might be our kinsman"
- City houses different from rural land: one year to redeem
- Levite cities protected specially

Poverty relief (vv. 35-55):
- "Thou shalt relieve him" - by sympathy, service, and supply
- No usury on loans to the poor: "Fear thy God"
- Hebrew servants must not be treated as bondservants but as hired workers
- "They are my servants" - God's ownership limits human bondage
- At Jubilee, all Hebrew servants freed with their families
- "Typical of our redemption from the service of sin and Satan"

Chapter 26: Blessings and Curses
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this as "a solemn conclusion" to Leviticus - promises of blessing for obedience, threats of judgment for disobedience.

Key commandments summarized (vv. 1-2):
1. No idols or images
2. Keep sabbaths and reverence the sanctuary
"As nothing tends more to corrupt religion than the use of images... so nothing contributes more to the support of it than keeping the sabbaths"

Blessings for obedience (vv. 3-13):
- Seasonal rain, abundant harvest: "threshing shall reach unto the vintage"
- Peace and safety: "none shall make you afraid"
- Victory in war: "five of you shall chase an hundred"
- Population growth: "I will make you fruitful"
- God's presence: "I will set my tabernacle among you... I will walk among you"
- "I will be your God, and ye shall be my people"

Curses for disobedience (vv. 14-39):
The pattern of escalating judgment: "If less judgments do not do their work, God will send greater"
- First: disease, crop failure, defeat (vv. 14-17)
- Then "seven times more": drought, broken pride (vv. 18-20)
- Seven times more: wild beasts, childlessness (vv. 21-22)
- Seven times more: sword, pestilence, famine (vv. 23-26)
- Seven times more: cannibalism, destroyed sanctuaries, exile (vv. 27-39)

"The land shall enjoy her sabbaths" - the seventy-year captivity compensated for unobserved sabbatical years.

Promise of restoration upon repentance (vv. 40-45):
Requirements: confession (including fathers' sins), humble heart, acceptance of punishment
"Then will I remember my covenant with Jacob, and also my covenant with Isaac, and also my covenant with Abraham"
"None are so ready to repent as God is to forgive upon repentance, through Christ"

Chapter 27: Vows and Dedications
----------------------------------------
Henry presents this appendix on voluntary vows and dedications - "the free-will offerings of their mouth."

Persons vowed to God (vv. 1-8):
- Could dedicate self or family members to tabernacle service
- Since Levites served the tabernacle, vowed persons were redeemed at set prices
- Males worth more than females; prime-aged worth more than young/old
- "The poor shall be valued according to their ability" - God "expects and requires from men according to what they have"

Animals vowed (vv. 9-13):
- Clean animals once vowed were holy and could not be exchanged
- "This teaches caution in making vows and constancy in keeping them"
- Unclean animals could be redeemed at value plus one-fifth

Houses and lands (vv. 14-25):
- Could sanctify house or land; priest set the value
- Redemption required value plus one-fifth
- Inherited land unredeemed by Jubilee became the priests' permanently
- Purchased land returned to original family at Jubilee
- "God hates robbery for burnt-offerings" - cannot give what belongs to another

Firstlings, devoted things, and tithes (vv. 26-34):
- Cannot vow what already belongs to God (firstlings)
- Devoted things (cherem) could not be redeemed - "most holy to the Lord"
- Devoted persons (enemies under divine curse) were put to death
- Tithes: one-tenth of all increase, acknowledging God as owner
- Could be redeemed only at value plus one-fifth

Conclusion (v. 34):
"These are the commandments which the Lord commanded Moses for the children of Israel in mount Sinai."

Henry's final application: "Blessed be God that we are not under the dark shadows of the law, but enjoy the clear light of the gospel... not under the heavy yoke of the law... but under the sweet and easy institutions of the gospel." Yet this freedom should not make us casual: "Let us not therefore think that... a little care, time, and expense, will serve to honour God with. No, but rather have our hearts more enlarged with free-will offerings to his praise."
