BOOK: Genesis - Matthew Henry Commentary (Summarized)
================================================================================

Chapter 1: The Creation
----------------------------------------
Henry establishes that Genesis 1:1 is the foundation of all religion, countering pagan philosophies that claimed the world was eternal or arose by chance. The Hebrew "Elohim" (plural) hints at the Trinity. Key observations:

The six days of creation follow a pattern: Days 1-3 involve separation (light/darkness, waters/firmament, sea/land), while Days 4-6 involve filling these spaces (celestial bodies, sea creatures and birds, land animals and man). Henry notes creation displays God's variety, beauty, exactness, power, order, and mystery.

On man's creation ("Let us make man"), Henry sees the divine counsel of the Trinity. Man bears God's image in knowledge, righteousness, and dominion over creation. The Sabbath pattern is established - God's rest models human rest in worship. Practical application: creation reveals God's power and wisdom; believers should keep heaven in view and earth under their feet.

Chapter 2: The Sabbath and Marriage
----------------------------------------
Henry treats this as an appendix expanding on man's creation. The Sabbath is instituted in Eden before the Fall - making it a creation ordinance, not merely a Mosaic law. God's rest was not from weariness but satisfaction in His completed work.

The garden of Eden represents God's special provision for man. The two trees symbolize life (dependent on God) and knowledge of good and evil (the test of obedience). Adam's naming the animals demonstrates his wisdom and dominion.

Eve's creation from Adam's rib signifies equality in nature yet distinction in role. Henry emphasizes marriage as divine institution: one man, one woman, lifelong union. The phrase "leave and cleave" establishes marriage priority over parental bonds. Their nakedness without shame shows original innocence.

Chapter 3: The Fall
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this "perhaps the saddest story in all the Bible." Satan chose the serpent for its subtlety and specious appearance. His temptation strategy: first create doubt ("Did God really say?"), then denial ("You will not die"), then enticement ("You will be like God").

Eve's error: she engaged in dialogue with the tempter, added to God's command ("neither shall you touch it"), and looked at the forbidden fruit with desire. Adam's sin was worse - he sinned with full knowledge, not being deceived.

Immediate consequences: shame (covering themselves), fear (hiding from God), and blame-shifting. God's interrogation was not for information but for confession. The protoevangelium (first gospel) appears in 3:15 - the seed of the woman will crush the serpent's head, prophesying Christ's ultimate victory over Satan.

The curses are remedial: the serpent cursed absolutely, but Adam and Eve cursed in their circumstances (pain in childbirth, toil in labor) with hope of redemption. Expulsion from Eden prevents access to the tree of life in their fallen state.

Chapter 4: Cain and Abel
----------------------------------------
Henry sees Cain and Abel as representing the two seeds - the godly and the wicked. Eve's naming of Cain ("possession") may reflect mistaken hope that he was the promised seed. Abel ("vanity") suggests lesser expectations.

Their offerings reveal heart conditions: Abel offered the firstlings with faith; Cain offered mere produce without faith. God's acceptance of Abel's sacrifice (likely by fire from heaven) and rejection of Cain's exposed the difference. Henry emphasizes that religious duties without faith are unacceptable to God.

Cain's anger was against God disguised as anger against his brother. God's warning ("sin is crouching at the door") offered Cain opportunity to repent. The first murder arose from religious jealousy. Cain's punishment - to be a fugitive and wanderer - demonstrates that guilt produces restlessness. Yet God's mark of protection shows mercy even on the murderer.

The line of Cain develops worldly culture (cities, music, metallurgy) but godlessness. The chapter ends hopefully with Seth's birth and men beginning to "call upon the name of the LORD" - the first public worship.

Chapter 5: The Genealogy from Adam to Noah
----------------------------------------
Henry views this genealogy as pointing toward Christ, the promised seed. The repeated refrain "and he died" serves as a solemn reminder of sin's wages. Notable observations:

Adam lived 930 years, long enough to tell nine generations about Eden and the Fall. The long lifespans allowed rapid population growth and transmission of knowledge and godliness. Seth was born "in Adam's likeness" - bearing the fallen image, unlike Adam who was made in God's image.

Enoch stands out: "he walked with God, and he was not, for God took him." Henry sees this as an exceptional translation to heaven without death - a testimony that there is life beyond, and a reward for extraordinary piety. Walking with God means consistent fellowship, progressive holiness, and heavenly-mindedness.

Methuselah's name prophetically means "when he dies, it shall come" - and indeed the flood came the year he died. Lamech named Noah ("rest/comfort"), hoping for relief from the curse on the ground. The chapter bridges creation to the flood, showing how sin increased as generations passed.

Chapter 6: The Corruption Before the Flood
----------------------------------------
Henry explains the world's degeneracy through two causes: population increase (more people meant more sin) and mixed marriages between the godly line of Seth ("sons of God") and the ungodly line of Cain ("daughters of men"). This intermarriage for beauty rather than godliness corrupted the church.

God's grief over mankind's wickedness teaches that sin is grievous to Him. The 120-year countdown before the flood represents God's patience, giving time for repentance. Noah "found grace" - an early appearance of the doctrine of grace. He was righteous and blameless "in his generation" - relative but real holiness.

The ark's specifications demonstrate God's precise providence for salvation. Its dimensions (300 x 50 x 30 cubits) provided ample space. Henry sees the ark as a type of Christ - the only refuge from divine judgment. Noah's 120 years of faithful obedience while building the ark, enduring mockery, exemplifies persevering faith.

Chapter 7: The Flood Comes
----------------------------------------
God's invitation to Noah - "Come into the ark" (not "go") - implies God's presence with him. Clean animals by sevens (for sacrifice) and unclean by twos (for preservation) were gathered. Henry notes God brought the animals to Noah; supernatural providence accomplished what human effort could not.

The week's delay before the flood gave final opportunity for repentance and tested Noah's faith. The flood sources were both above (windows of heaven) and below (fountains of the deep), reversing the separation of Day 2 of creation. The 40 days of rain demonstrated thoroughness of judgment.

Henry emphasizes the ark's safety amid the rising waters - floating securely while all outside perished. "The LORD shut him in" shows divine protection. The destruction of all flesh outside the ark parallels final judgment - those not in Christ will perish. Noah's security in the ark represents believers' security in Christ.

Chapter 8: The Flood Recedes
----------------------------------------
"God remembered Noah" - not that He had forgotten, but that He now acted in mercy. The wind that dried the earth parallels the Spirit hovering over waters in creation - a new beginning. The ark rested on Ararat on the 17th day of the 7th month (later Passover week, pointing to resurrection).

Noah's sending of the raven and dove demonstrates wisdom in discerning times. The raven, an unclean bird, found carrion to feed on. The dove returned, finding no rest - like the soul that finds no rest until it returns to Christ. The olive leaf signified new life and peace. When the dove did not return, the earth was ready.

Noah waited for God's command to exit, as he had waited for the command to enter. His first act was building an altar and offering sacrifice - gratitude before personal comfort. God's response to the "pleasing aroma" was the promise never to curse the ground again or destroy all living things by flood, establishing the stability of natural order (seedtime/harvest, summer/winter).

Chapter 9: The Noahic Covenant
----------------------------------------
God renews the creation blessing ("be fruitful and multiply") and establishes the post-flood order. Key provisions:

Animals will now fear man - the original harmony disrupted. Eating meat is now permitted (not before the flood), but not blood, which represents life and is sacred. Capital punishment for murder is instituted - "whoever sheds man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed" - because man bears God's image. This establishes human government's authority.

The rainbow covenant is unconditional and universal - never again will flood waters destroy all life. The rainbow is a sign for God Himself to remember His promise. Henry sees it as pointing to Christ, through whom wrath is restrained.

Noah's drunkenness shows even the righteous can fall. Ham's sin was not merely seeing but disrespecting and mocking his father. Shem and Japheth's respectful covering showed honor. The resulting prophecy: Canaan (Ham's line) cursed to servitude; Shem blessed (the Messiah's line); Japheth enlarged, dwelling in Shem's tents (Gentiles sharing in Israel's spiritual blessings through Christ).

Chapter 10: The Table of Nations
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this the only authentic ancient account of the origin of nations. From Noah's three sons came seventy nations (a complete number). This genealogy was preserved for the sake of identifying Christ's lineage.

Japheth's descendants populated the coastlands and islands - the Gentile nations of Europe and beyond. This includes the British Isles. The promise that Japheth would "dwell in the tents of Shem" prophesies Gentile inclusion in spiritual blessings.

Ham's descendants include Cush (Ethiopia), Mizraim (Egypt), and Canaan. Nimrod stands out as the first empire-builder - "mighty hunter before the LORD" is interpreted negatively as a tyrant hunting men's souls. Babel, his first city, becomes the symbol of human pride against God.

Shem's descendants lead to Abraham and eventually Christ. The genealogies serve Scripture's purpose of tracing the seed of the woman who would crush the serpent's head. Israel came from Shem (hence "Semites"), and through them, the Savior.

Chapter 11: Babel and the Line to Abram
----------------------------------------
The Tower of Babel represents humanity's united rebellion against God's command to spread across the earth. Their motives were pride ("make a name for ourselves") and self-preservation ("lest we be scattered"). Henry notes they had one language - a blessing that became occasion for sin when used for collective defiance.

God's response - confusing their language - was both judgment and mercy: judgment on their pride, mercy in preventing greater evil through united wickedness. The scattering fulfilled God's original purpose for mankind to fill the earth. "Babel" means confusion, foreshadowing Babylon's role as the great enemy of God's people throughout Scripture.

The genealogy from Shem to Abram bridges the post-flood world to patriarchal history. Lifespans gradually decrease, from Shem's 600 years to Terah's 205. This genealogy preserved the messianic line. Terah's departure from Ur with Abram, Lot, and Sarai began the journey to Canaan, though Terah stopped at Haran where he died. Henry sees God's providence preparing Abram for his calling.

Chapter 12: The Call of Abram
----------------------------------------
God's call to Abram represents a major turning point - from general human history to the particular history of redemption. The command was costly: leave country, kindred, and father's house. The promise was sevenfold: great nation, blessing, great name, being a blessing, blessed allies, cursed enemies, and all families blessed through him - pointing to Christ.

Henry emphasizes Abram obeyed "not knowing where he was going" - the essence of faith. He was 75 years old, and Sarai was barren, making the promise of descendants seemingly impossible. Yet he went. Upon arriving in Canaan, he found it inhabited by Canaanites, yet built altars and worshipped - claiming the land by faith.

The famine that drove Abram to Egypt tested his faith. His scheme of calling Sarai his sister (technically a half-truth) revealed fear overcoming faith. God protected Sarai despite Abram's failure, plaguing Pharaoh's house. Henry notes that even great believers have their lapses, but God's purposes are not thwarted by human weakness.

Chapter 13: Abram and Lot Separate
----------------------------------------
Returning from Egypt, Abram was rich but troubled - his and Lot's herdsmen quarreled over pasture. Henry observes that riches, while blessings, bring burdens: care in getting, fear in keeping, temptation in using. The Hebrew word for "rich" means "heavy."

Abram's response demonstrates godly wisdom: he initiated peace, refused to stand on seniority, and generously offered Lot first choice of land. "Let there be no strife between us, for we are brethren" - a model for resolving disputes among believers. The Canaanite presence made their quarrel a poor testimony.

Lot's choice revealed carnal wisdom: he chose by sight (the well-watered plain), not by faith. The Jordan valley looked like Eden but bordered Sodom. Henry warns against choosing by worldly advantage without considering spiritual danger. After Lot departed, God renewed the promise to Abram - expanded now to include land "as far as you can see" and descendants "as the dust of the earth."

Chapter 14: Abram Rescues Lot
----------------------------------------
This chapter records the first war in Scripture and reveals Abram as a warrior. Four eastern kings, led by Chedorlaomer, conquered five kings including Sodom's. When Lot was captured, Abram armed his 318 trained servants, pursued the victors, and by night attack rescued Lot, recovering all people and goods.

Henry sees several lessons: Lot's troubles stemmed from his earlier bad choice; association with Sodom brought Sodom's calamities. Yet Abram, though wronged by Lot's greedy choice, showed no resentment but risked his life for his nephew. Family loyalty and forgiveness are praised.

The mysterious Melchizedek, "king of Salem" and "priest of God Most High," blessed Abram and received tithes from him. Henry sees Melchizedek as a type of Christ: king of righteousness and peace, without recorded genealogy (in priestly terms), uniting kingly and priestly offices. Abram's tithe acknowledged Melchizedek's superiority.

Abram's refusal of Sodom's king's offer ("not a thread or sandal strap") shows integrity: he would not be enriched by the wicked or give them occasion to claim credit for his prosperity.

Chapter 15: The Covenant Confirmed
----------------------------------------
God's word came to Abram: "Fear not, I am your shield and exceeding great reward." After the battle, Abram might fear retaliation or regret refusing Sodom's spoils - God addresses both. Henry emphasizes that God Himself, not His gifts, is the believer's true reward.

Abram's honest complaint - "I remain childless" - shows faith can coexist with perplexity. God responded not with rebuke but revelation: descendants as innumerable as stars. "Abram believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness" - the foundational text for justification by faith, central to Paul's theology.

The covenant ceremony was profound: animals divided, a smoking furnace and flaming torch (representing God) passing between the pieces. This was a self-maledictory oath - God alone passed through, making the covenant unconditional on His part. The 400-year prophecy of Egyptian bondage revealed that fulfillment would be delayed but certain. The boundaries of the promised land were specified, from Egypt's river to the Euphrates.

Chapter 16: Hagar and Ishmael
----------------------------------------
Sarai's scheme to obtain children through Hagar was culturally acceptable but spiritually wrong. Henry notes that temptations from loved ones are especially dangerous. Abram's compliance showed weakness - good men can be led astray by well-meaning but misguided spouses.

Hagar's pregnancy produced pride, and she despised her barren mistress. Sarai's harsh treatment drove Hagar away. The Angel of the LORD (whom Henry identifies as Christ pre-incarnate) found Hagar by a spring and commanded her to return and submit, promising that her son would father a great nation.

Ishmael's prophesied character - "a wild donkey of a man, his hand against everyone" - describes the Arab peoples' fierce independence. The name means "God hears," for God heard affliction. Hagar called God "El Roi" - "the God who sees me." Abram was 86 when Ishmael was born, and would wait 13 more years for Isaac.

Chapter 17: Circumcision Instituted
----------------------------------------
Thirteen years of silence from God ended when Abram was 99. God appeared as "El Shaddai" (God Almighty), commanding: "Walk before me and be blameless." The covenant was formally established with name changes: Abram ("exalted father") became Abraham ("father of multitudes"); Sarai became Sarah ("princess").

Circumcision was instituted as the covenant sign - a mark in the flesh signifying separation unto God and the cutting away of fleshly nature. It was to be performed on the eighth day, including household servants. The uncircumcised male would be "cut off" - excluded from covenant blessings.

Abraham laughed at the promise of a son through Sarah (now 90) - Henry interprets this as wonder, not unbelief. His request that Ishmael might be blessed was answered: Ishmael would father twelve princes and become a great nation. But the covenant would be established through Isaac, to be born in one year. Abraham obeyed immediately, circumcising his entire household that very day.

Chapter 18: Abraham's Intercession for Sodom
----------------------------------------
The LORD appeared to Abraham at Mamre with two angels, appearing as three men. Abraham's lavish hospitality - running to meet them, preparing a feast - earned the commendation: "Abraham shall surely become a great nation." Henry sees this as a picture of Christ's incarnate visitation.

Sarah's laughter at the promise of a son (she was 90) was met with gentle rebuke: "Is anything too hard for the LORD?" Her denial of laughing was a lie, immediately exposed. Yet the promise stood - Isaac would be born "at this time next year."

The LORD revealed His intention to destroy Sodom, saying Abraham should not be kept in ignorance. Abraham's intercession is a model of prayer: reverent ("I am but dust and ashes"), persistent (six requests), and trusting God's justice. He negotiated from fifty righteous down to ten - but even ten could not be found. Henry notes that God is more ready to grant than we to ask; Abraham stopped asking before God stopped granting.

Chapter 19: Sodom Destroyed
----------------------------------------
The two angels arrived at Sodom in the evening; Lot sat in the gate (a place of civic responsibility - showing he had risen in Sodom despite his righteous soul being vexed by their conduct). His hospitality, insisting the visitors stay at his house rather than the street, showed awareness of Sodom's dangers.

The men of Sodom's assault on Lot's house, demanding the visitors for homosexual assault, revealed their depravity. Lot's offer of his daughters (though inexcusable) showed his desperate attempt to protect his guests. The angels struck the attackers with blindness. Henry emphasizes that Sodom's sin brought supernatural judgment.

Lot's warning to his sons-in-law was received as a joke - the world always mocks impending judgment. Even Lot lingered and had to be dragged out by the angels. "Escape to the mountains" was the command, but Lot negotiated for Zoar instead. Lot's wife, looking back (in longing or unbelief), became a pillar of salt - a permanent warning.

The subsequent incest with his daughters (who made Lot drunk) produced Moab and Ammon - future enemies of Israel. Henry sees this as consequence of Lot's compromise: he escaped Sodom's fire but not its corruption.

Chapter 20: Abraham and Abimelech
----------------------------------------
Abraham repeated his earlier sin, again calling Sarah his sister before a foreign king, Abimelech of Gerar. Henry marvels that this occurred so close to Isaac's promised birth, and after Abraham's intercession for Sodom showed his faith. Even mature believers can fall into old sins.

God warned Abimelech in a dream that Sarah was married. Abimelech's protest of innocence - "In the integrity of my heart I did this" - was accepted by God, who had prevented him from touching her. This shows God's common grace restraining sin even among pagans.

Abraham's excuse - technically Sarah was his half-sister - was exposed as inadequate. He had assumed "there is no fear of God in this place" and acted accordingly, but Abimelech showed more integrity than Abraham expected. Abimelech's generous restoration of Sarah, with gifts and permission to dwell anywhere, contrasts with Abraham's deception.

Abraham prayed for Abimelech's household, and God removed the barrenness He had inflicted. Henry observes the irony: Abraham, still waiting for his own child, prayed for others' fertility. God uses imperfect servants, and answered prayer does not validate every action of the one praying.

Chapter 21: Isaac Born, Ishmael Cast Out
----------------------------------------
"The LORD visited Sarah as he had said" - God's promises are certain though delayed. Isaac was born when Abraham was 100 and Sarah 90, at the exact time God specified. Isaac means "laughter" - Sarah's earlier laughter of doubt became laughter of joy. Henry sees Isaac as a type of Christ: long promised, miraculously born, and the seed through whom blessing comes.

At Isaac's weaning feast, Ishmael mocked - the child of flesh persecuting the child of promise. Sarah demanded Hagar and Ishmael's expulsion. Though grievous to Abraham, God confirmed Sarah's demand: "In Isaac shall your seed be called." This became Paul's allegory of law and grace (Galatians 4).

God preserved Hagar and Ishmael in the wilderness, providing water and promising Ishmael would become a great nation. Henry notes God's care extends to the outcast, yet distinguishes between temporal blessings (Ishmael) and covenant blessings (Isaac).

Abraham's treaty with Abimelech at Beersheba ("well of the oath") shows his growing influence in Canaan. He planted a tamarisk tree and called on the Everlasting God - combining cultivation of the land with cultivation of the soul.

Chapter 22: The Sacrifice of Isaac
----------------------------------------
Henry calls this "one of the wonders of the church" - Abraham's supreme test of faith. God's command seemed to contradict His promise: sacrifice the son through whom the promise was to be fulfilled. The command was specific and agonizing: "your son, your only son, whom you love."

Abraham's obedience was immediate ("rose early"), without argument or delay. The three-day journey gave time for reflection, yet he persevered. Isaac's question - "where is the lamb?" - received prophetic answer: "God will provide himself a lamb." Henry sees Isaac (likely a young adult) as willingly submitting, prefiguring Christ's willing sacrifice.

At the moment of sacrifice, the Angel stayed Abraham's hand: "Now I know that you fear God." A ram caught in a thicket became the substitute. Henry emphasizes: Isaac was "offered" in Abraham's heart and intent - the sacrifice was complete though Isaac lived. God swore by Himself to confirm the covenant - His strongest possible oath.

The location, Mount Moriah, later became the temple site. "The LORD will provide" (Jehovah-Jireh) became the place's name - pointing to God's provision of Christ as the true Lamb.

Chapter 23: Sarah's Death and Burial
----------------------------------------
Sarah died at 127 years - the only woman whose age at death Scripture records, highlighting her importance. Abraham mourned genuinely, neither stoically suppressing nor excessively indulging grief. Death parts even the longest and happiest marriages.

Abraham's negotiation with the Hittites for a burial plot shows diplomatic wisdom. He identified as "a stranger and sojourner" - maintaining pilgrim identity even while purchasing property. Ephron offered the cave of Machpelah freely, but Abraham insisted on paying full price (400 shekels) to secure undisputed title.

Henry notes the significance: this was Abraham's only land purchase in Canaan - the promised land remained a matter of faith, not possession. Yet in death, he claimed the land that life had denied him. The cave became the patriarchal tomb - Abraham, Isaac, Rebekah, Leah, and Jacob would all be buried there.

Chapter 24: Isaac's Marriage to Rebekah
----------------------------------------
This longest chapter in Genesis shows God's providence in marriage. Abraham, now old, charged his servant to find Isaac a wife from his relatives, not from Canaanites. Isaac must not return to Mesopotamia - separation from the old country must be maintained.

The servant's prayer at the well demonstrates practical wisdom combined with faith: he asked for a specific sign (offering water to him and his camels) that would reveal character, not just identity. Rebekah's generous response - watering ten camels was substantial labor - confirmed she was the one.

Laban's hospitality and the servant's faithful testimony of Abraham's prosperity set the stage for negotiation. When asked, Rebekah chose to go immediately - her willing consent was essential. Henry emphasizes that marriages should be made with prayer, parental guidance, and personal consent.

Isaac met Rebekah while meditating in the field at evening - a habit of devotion. Their marriage brought him comfort after Sarah's death. The detailed narrative teaches that God guides those who seek Him in life's major decisions.

Chapter 25: Abraham's Death, Esau and Jacob
----------------------------------------
Abraham remarried (Keturah) and had more sons, but Isaac inherited everything - the others received gifts and were sent away. Abraham died at 175, "gathered to his people" - implying reunion with the godly dead. Isaac and Ishmael together buried him at Machpelah.

Ishmael's twelve princes fulfilled God's promise but remained outside the covenant. He died at 137 years.

Isaac's story continues with Rebekah's barrenness - twenty years without children. Isaac prayed, and Rebekah conceived twins who struggled in the womb. God's oracle revealed: "Two nations... the older shall serve the younger" - divine election before birth, before any works.

Esau and Jacob's contrasting characters emerged early: Esau the skillful hunter, outdoors man, Isaac's favorite; Jacob the quiet tent-dweller, Rebekah's favorite. Parental favoritism planted seeds of future conflict.

Esau's sale of his birthright for stew revealed profane priorities - immediate appetite over spiritual inheritance. "Thus Esau despised his birthright" - a warning against trading eternal for temporal. Jacob's opportunism was wrong, but Esau's contempt was worse.

Chapter 26: Isaac and Abimelech
----------------------------------------
Famine drove Isaac toward Egypt, but God commanded him to stay in Canaan, renewing the Abrahamic promises to him. Isaac repeated his father's sin, calling Rebekah his sister before Abimelech. Henry marvels at how good men repeat their predecessors' failures.

Abimelech's discovery (seeing Isaac "laughing" with Rebekah - an intimate scene) led to rebuke but also protection. Unlike Abraham's encounters, there was no taking of Rebekah.

God blessed Isaac's farming extraordinarily - hundredfold return. This provocation of Philistine envy led to conflicts over wells. Isaac's pattern was peaceful: when disputed, he moved on and dug new wells. Finally at Rehoboth ("room") he found space without strife.

God appeared to Isaac at Beersheba, confirming the covenant. Isaac built an altar and called on the LORD - worship preceding settlement. Abimelech's treaty request acknowledged God's evident blessing on Isaac. The chapter ends with the grief Esau's Hittite wives caused - intermarriage with Canaanites brought "bitterness of soul" to Isaac and Rebekah.

Chapter 27: Jacob Obtains the Blessing
----------------------------------------
This chapter records the famous deception. Isaac, old and blind, prepared to bless Esau. Rebekah, having received the oracle of the elder serving the younger, plotted to secure the blessing for Jacob through deceit.

Henry distributes blame: Isaac erred in trying to bless Esau against God's revealed will, motivated by appetite (venison) and partiality. Rebekah sinned in using deception rather than trusting God's providence. Jacob sinned in lying directly to his father.

Yet God overruled human sin to accomplish His purpose. The blessing Jacob received was indeed prophetic: fertility, dominion, cursed enemies, blessed allies. When Esau arrived, Isaac "trembled exceedingly" - recognizing God had overturned his intention.

Esau's bitter cry for blessing received only a subordinate promise: he would live by the sword, serve his brother, but eventually break free. His murderous hatred forced Jacob's flight.

Henry notes the tangled consequences of this family's sins: twenty years of separation, Rebekah never seeing Jacob again, Jacob reaping deception from Laban. God achieves His purposes, but those who use sinful means pay heavy costs.

Chapter 28: Jacob's Dream at Bethel
----------------------------------------
Isaac blessed Jacob formally before departure, charging him to marry within the family. This blessing was now freely given, confirming what had been seized by fraud. Jacob departed as a lonely exile, fleeing his brother's wrath.

At Bethel, using a stone for pillow, Jacob dreamed of a ladder (or stairway) reaching heaven, with angels ascending and descending. God stood above it, declaring Himself "the God of Abraham and Isaac" and renewing the promises: the land, innumerable descendants, blessing to all families, and personal presence and protection.

Henry interprets the ladder as Christ - the only way between heaven and earth (John 1:51). Angels ascending first, then descending, suggests our prayers go up before God's answers come down.

Jacob's response was awe: "How awesome is this place! This is none other than the house of God, and this is the gate of heaven." He renamed the place Bethel ("house of God") and made a vow: if God would protect and provide, the LORD would be his God, the stone would mark a sacred place, and he would tithe.

Esau's response to Jacob's departure was to marry an Ishmaelite, thinking to please his parents - a gesture too late and misdirected.

Chapter 29: Jacob Arrives at Laban's House
----------------------------------------
Providence led Jacob directly to the well where Rachel came with her flock. His emotional meeting with his cousin - rolling away the stone single-handedly, weeping and kissing her - showed the relief of reaching family after lonely flight.

Laban's welcome seemed warm, and Jacob stayed a month. When asked about wages, Jacob proposed seven years' service for Rachel, whom he loved. They "seemed but a few days for the love he had for her."

But the deceiver was deceived. On the wedding night, Laban substituted Leah. Jacob's morning discovery - "Behold, it was Leah!" - echoes his father's discovery of deception. Henry notes the justice: Jacob who exploited his father's blindness was himself exploited by darkness.

Laban's excuse (local custom) was weak, but Jacob was trapped. He worked another seven years for Rachel, whom he married after Leah's bridal week. The stage was set for domestic rivalry.

Leah, unloved but fertile, bore four sons: Reuben ("see, a son" - hoping for Jacob's love), Simeon ("hearing" - God heard her affliction), Levi ("attachment" - hoping Jacob would attach to her), and Judah ("praise" - she would praise the LORD). Through Leah, not Rachel, came the royal and priestly tribes.

Chapter 30: Jacob's Growing Family and Wealth
----------------------------------------
Rachel's barrenness produced envy and impatience: "Give me children, or I die!" Jacob's angry response - "Am I in God's place?" - was warranted, though harsh. Rachel followed Sarah's failed strategy, giving her maid Bilhah to Jacob.

Bilhah bore Dan ("judging") and Naphtali ("wrestling"). Leah responded by giving Zilpah, who bore Gad ("fortune") and Asher ("happy"). The wives competed through their maids - a household of rivalry and jealousy.

The mandrakes episode showed Rachel bargaining with Leah for fertility plants, trading a night with Jacob. The irony: Leah conceived that night (Issachar, "wages"), not Rachel. Leah bore Zebulun ("honor") and finally a daughter, Dinah.

"Then God remembered Rachel" - at last she bore Joseph ("may he add"), anticipating more children. Jacob's twelve sons (eleven here, Benjamin later) would become Israel's twelve tribes.

Jacob then negotiated departure from Laban. His proposal - keeping speckled and spotted animals - seemed to favor Laban. But through selective breeding (and God's blessing), Jacob's flocks increased dramatically. Henry notes that Jacob's methods were questionable, but God's underlying blessing was the true source of prosperity.

Chapter 31: Jacob Flees from Laban
----------------------------------------
Jacob's prosperity bred Laban's sons' resentment. They accused Jacob of taking their father's wealth. Laban's countenance changed - no longer friendly. God's direct command came: "Return to the land of your fathers; I will be with you."

Jacob consulted Rachel and Leah in the field - privately, away from spies. His speech reviewed Laban's unfairness (changing wages ten times) and God's faithfulness (prospering him despite Laban's schemes). Both wives agreed: "Is there any portion or inheritance left to us? He treats us as foreigners."

Jacob departed secretly while Laban was shearing sheep. Rachel stole her father's household idols (teraphim) - perhaps for inheritance claims or lingering paganism. Laban pursued and caught them after seven days, but God warned him in a dream: "Say nothing to Jacob, good or bad."

The confrontation was heated. Jacob defended himself passionately: twenty years of faithful service, bearing losses, enduring heat and cold. Laban's search for the idols failed - Rachel sat on them, claiming women's indisposition.

They made a covenant at Mizpah ("watchtower"): "The LORD watch between you and me when we are absent from each other" - not warm blessing but mutual distrust. They would not cross this boundary to harm each other. Laban departed; Jacob never saw him again.

Chapter 32: Jacob Wrestles with God
----------------------------------------
After Laban, angels met Jacob - "God's camp" (Mahanaim). This divine army reassured him before facing Esau. Jacob sent messengers announcing his return and seeking favor. The report: Esau was coming with 400 men.

Jacob's fear produced practical wisdom and prayer. He divided his company - if one is attacked, the other escapes. His prayer is a model: invoking God's promises, confessing unworthiness ("I am not worthy of the least of all your mercies"), specific petition, and claiming God's word.

He sent elaborate gifts ahead in waves - droves of livestock to soften Esau's heart. Each servant was to say, "These belong to your servant Jacob; they are a gift."

Alone at night at the Jabbok ford, Jacob wrestled with "a man" until daybreak. Henry identifies this as the Angel of the covenant, Christ pre-incarnate. When the man could not prevail, he touched Jacob's hip, dislocating it - leaving permanent weakness. Jacob refused to release him without blessing.

The new name "Israel" ("he struggles with God") marked transformation. Jacob called the place Peniel ("face of God"): "I have seen God face to face, and my life is preserved." He limped away - blessed but broken, stronger through weakness.

Chapter 33: Jacob and Esau Reconciled
----------------------------------------
The dreaded meeting proved peaceful. Esau ran to embrace Jacob - God had changed his heart. Henry quotes Proverbs 16:7: "When a man's ways please the LORD, he makes even his enemies to be at peace with him."

Jacob's repeated bowing (seven times) showed appropriate humility. The brothers wept together. Esau met the women and children graciously. Jacob's insistence that Esau accept the gift ("seeing your face is like seeing the face of God") completed the reconciliation.

Esau offered to travel together; Jacob declined, needing to move slowly with children and nursing animals. Esau offered guards; Jacob declined, trusting God's protection. They parted peacefully - Esau to Seir, Jacob to Succoth, then Shechem.

At Shechem, Jacob bought land and erected an altar: El-Elohe-Israel ("God, the God of Israel") - claiming his new name in worship. Henry notes this was premature settlement; Shechem would bring disaster.

Chapter 34: The Rape of Dinah and Its Aftermath
----------------------------------------
This dark chapter records Dinah's defilement and her brothers' violent revenge. Dinah "went out to see the daughters of the land" - curiosity about worldly society proved dangerous. Shechem, the prince's son, violated her.

Shechem then wanted to marry Dinah - his "soul was drawn to her." Negotiations began: the Shechemites offered any bride-price. Jacob's sons answered deceitfully, demanding all males be circumcised.

The Shechemites agreed - perhaps influenced by commercial advantages. But on the third day, while they were sore, Simeon and Levi attacked, killing all males and taking Dinah. The other brothers plundered the city.

Jacob's rebuke was practical: "You have brought trouble on me, making me stink among the inhabitants." Their answer - "Should he treat our sister like a prostitute?" - was morally indignant but did not justify massacre.

Henry distributes blame widely: Dinah's imprudence, Shechem's violence, Jacob's passivity, and the brothers' treacherous overkill. Using circumcision - the covenant sign - for murder was particularly heinous. Jacob remembered this on his deathbed, cursing Simeon and Levi's anger.

Chapter 35: Return to Bethel; Deaths
----------------------------------------
God commanded Jacob: "Go up to Bethel... make an altar to the God who appeared to you when you fled." This recalled his vow decades earlier. Jacob obeyed, first purifying his household - foreign gods and earrings (apparently idol-associated) were buried under the oak at Shechem.

The journey was protected: "the terror of God fell upon the surrounding cities." At Bethel, Jacob built the altar and called it El-Bethel ("God of Bethel"). Deborah, Rebekah's nurse, died there - a link to Jacob's childhood.

God appeared again, confirming the name Israel and renewing the Abrahamic promises: "Be fruitful and multiply; a nation and company of nations... the land I gave to Abraham and Isaac I will give to you." Jacob set up a pillar, consecrating it with oil.

Departing Bethel, Rachel died giving birth to Benjamin. Her name for him (Ben-Oni, "son of my sorrow") Jacob changed to Benjamin ("son of my right hand"). She was buried on the road to Bethlehem.

Reuben's incest with Bilhah, Jacob's concubine, forfeited his birthright - though Jacob said nothing at the time, he remembered at the blessing. Isaac died at 180, buried by both Jacob and Esau - brothers together in filial duty.

Chapter 36: Esau's Descendants (Edom)
----------------------------------------
This genealogical chapter records Esau's family - the Edomites. Henry notes the contrast: Esau's prosperity came quickly (kings before Israel had kings), but was temporal. Jacob's blessing was delayed but eternal.

Esau married Canaanite women, then an Ishmaelite - departing from family tradition. He moved to Seir because the land could not support both brothers' wealth. His descendants became the Edomites, Israel's persistent enemies.

The chapter lists Esau's sons, grandsons, the chiefs (dukes) of Edom, and the early kings - before Israel's monarchy. The Horites, original inhabitants of Seir, were absorbed into Edom.

Henry sees the record preserved for three purposes: honoring Abraham's broader family, recording fulfillment of promises to Abraham ("father of many nations"), and providing context for later Edomite-Israelite relations.

Chapter 37: Joseph Sold into Slavery
----------------------------------------
The Joseph narrative begins - Genesis's longest continuous story. Joseph at seventeen brought bad reports about his brothers to Jacob. Jacob's open favoritism (the special coat) provoked hatred: "they could not speak peaceably to him."

Joseph's dreams inflamed their hatred further. First dream: their sheaves bowed to his. Second dream: sun, moon, and eleven stars bowed to him. Even Jacob rebuked him, though he "kept the matter in mind." Henry notes dreams can be divine communication, but sharing them unwisely creates trouble.

Jacob sent Joseph to check on his brothers at Shechem. They saw him coming: "Here comes this dreamer. Let us kill him." Reuben intervened, suggesting the pit - planning secret rescue. They stripped Joseph's coat and threw him in.

While eating (!), they saw Ishmaelites passing. Judah suggested selling him - profit over murder. Twenty pieces of silver bought Joseph, who was taken to Egypt. Reuben returned to find Joseph gone, tearing his clothes in distress.

The brothers dipped Joseph's coat in goat's blood. Jacob was devastated: "Without doubt Joseph is torn to pieces." He refused comfort, mourning inconsolably. Meanwhile, Joseph was sold to Potiphar, captain of Pharaoh's guard - providence at work through tragedy.

Chapter 38: Judah and Tamar
----------------------------------------
This chapter interrupts Joseph's story with a disturbing account of Judah's family. Henry wonders that Christ should descend from this line - demonstrating grace, not merit, in salvation.

Judah married a Canaanite woman (against family practice) and had three sons: Er, Onan, and Shelah. Er married Tamar but was so wicked that God killed him. Onan, obligated by levirate custom to provide an heir for his brother, deliberately failed - and God killed him too.

Judah withheld Shelah from Tamar, fearing he would also die. Tamar, widowed and childless, waited in vain. When she heard Judah was traveling (now himself widowed), she disguised as a prostitute and positioned herself on his route.

Judah propositioned her, leaving his staff and seal as pledge for payment. She conceived. When Tamar was found pregnant, Judah demanded her execution for prostitution - until she produced his staff and seal. "She is more righteous than I," Judah admitted, "because I did not give her to my son Shelah."

Tamar bore twins: Perez (through whose line came David and Christ) and Zerah. The story shows God's providence operating through human sin - and Christ's willingness to identify with sinful humanity.

Chapter 39: Joseph in Potiphar's House
----------------------------------------
"The LORD was with Joseph" - the chapter's refrain (v. 2, 3, 21, 23). Sold as a slave, Joseph prospered. Potiphar recognized divine favor and promoted Joseph to overseer of everything.

Potiphar's wife repeatedly attempted seduction. Joseph's refusal was principled: betraying his master's trust would be wrong; more importantly, "How can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" Henry praises this as victory through grace.

She caught his garment; he fled, leaving it behind. She accused him of attempted rape - the scorned woman's revenge. Potiphar imprisoned Joseph, though Henry notes he might have executed him, suggesting doubt about his wife's story.

Even in prison, "the LORD was with Joseph." The warden entrusted all prisoners to Joseph's supervision. From slavery to prison, Joseph's character and God's presence remained constant. His path to prominence ran through suffering.

Chapter 40: The Dreams of the Butler and Baker
----------------------------------------
Providence positioned Joseph for future advancement. Pharaoh's chief butler and chief baker offended the king and were imprisoned - in Joseph's care. One morning, both were troubled by dreams.

"Do not interpretations belong to God?" Joseph asked, before hearing their dreams. This humility - attributing ability to God - characterized Joseph throughout.

The butler dreamed of squeezing grapes into Pharaoh's cup from a vine with three branches. Joseph interpreted: three days until restoration to his position. Joseph added a request: "Remember me when it is well with you; mention me to Pharaoh."

The baker, encouraged, shared his dream: three baskets of bread on his head, birds eating from the top basket. Joseph's interpretation was grim: three days until execution by hanging.

Both interpretations proved accurate. But "the chief butler did not remember Joseph, but forgot him." Two more years would pass before Joseph's release. Henry notes that human hopes disappoint, but God's timing is perfect.

Chapter 41: Joseph Exalted in Egypt
----------------------------------------
Two years later, Pharaoh dreamed: seven fat cows devoured by seven lean cows; seven plump ears of grain swallowed by seven thin ears. Egyptian magicians failed to interpret. Finally, the butler remembered Joseph.

Shaved and dressed appropriately, Joseph stood before Pharaoh. His first words: "It is not in me; God will give Pharaoh an answer of peace." This humility and God-centeredness characterized Joseph's entire career. He interpreted: seven years of plenty followed by seven years of famine. The doubled dream meant certainty and imminent fulfillment.

Joseph's unsolicited advice - appoint a wise administrator, store 20% during plenty - demonstrated administrative brilliance. Pharaoh recognized: "Can we find such a one as this, a man in whom the Spirit of God is?" Joseph, at 30, became second in Egypt, given the signet ring, fine garments, gold chain, and chariot.

He married Asenath, daughter of a priest of On, and had two sons: Manasseh ("making forget" - God helped him forget his troubles) and Ephraim ("fruitful" - God made him fruitful in affliction). During plenty, Joseph stored grain until it was beyond counting. When famine struck, all nations came to Egypt to buy from Joseph.

Chapter 42: Joseph's Brothers Arrive
----------------------------------------
Famine reached Canaan. Jacob sent ten sons to Egypt - but not Benjamin, fearing harm to Rachel's surviving son. Joseph's dreams began fulfillment: his brothers bowed before him, not recognizing the teenager they had sold, now clean-shaven Egyptian viceroy.

Joseph recognized them immediately but concealed himself, speaking harshly through an interpreter. "You are spies!" Their protest - "We are honest men, twelve brothers, the youngest is with our father, one is no more" - gave Joseph information about the family.

Joseph imprisoned them three days, then released all but Simeon, kept as hostage until they returned with Benjamin. Their conscience awoke: "We are truly guilty concerning our brother... therefore this distress has come upon us." They did not know Joseph understood their Hebrew conversation.

Joseph wept privately. Returning home, they found their money in their grain sacks - terror, not relief. Jacob's response was bitter: "Joseph is no more, Simeon is no more, and now you would take Benjamin? All these things are against me." Reuben offered his own sons as pledge; Jacob refused.

Chapter 43: The Brothers Return with Benjamin
----------------------------------------
When the grain ran out, necessity overcame fear. Judah (emerging as leader, not Reuben) pledged himself as surety for Benjamin. Jacob reluctantly consented: "If I am bereaved, I am bereaved." He sent gifts - balm, honey, spices, nuts - and double money to repay what was returned.

Seeing Benjamin, Joseph ordered a feast prepared. The brothers were terrified - summoned to Joseph's house, surely for punishment about the money! But the steward reassured them: "Your God and the God of your father has given you treasure."

Simeon was released. Joseph, seeing Benjamin, was overcome with emotion and withdrew to weep. At the feast, they were seated in birth order (astonishing them) and Benjamin received portions five times larger than the others. Henry sees Joseph testing whether they envied Benjamin as they had envied him.

Chapter 44: The Final Test
----------------------------------------
Joseph devised a final test. His silver cup was hidden in Benjamin's sack. The brothers departed, were overtaken, searched (from eldest to youngest, building suspense), and the cup was found with Benjamin. All returned, tearing their clothes.

Judah spoke: "What can we say? God has found out the iniquity of your servants." He meant not the false theft charge but their ancient sin against Joseph. Joseph demanded Benjamin remain as slave; the others could go free.

Judah's plea is among Scripture's most moving speeches. He recounted the whole story: their father's attachment to Benjamin, how losing him would kill Jacob. He offered himself as substitute: "Let your servant remain instead of the boy as a slave... For how can I go back to my father if the boy is not with me?"

This was the test's climax: would they abandon Benjamin as they had abandoned Joseph? Judah's self-sacrifice proved their repentance genuine.

Chapter 45: Joseph Reveals Himself
----------------------------------------
"Joseph could not restrain himself." He cleared the room and wept so loudly the Egyptians heard. "I am Joseph; is my father still alive?" His brothers were terrified, speechless.

Joseph's theology of providence shines here: "Do not be distressed or angry with yourselves for selling me here, for God sent me before you to preserve life." Three times he emphasized: God sent him - to preserve a remnant, to save lives by great deliverance. "It was not you who sent me here, but God."

He instructed them to bring Jacob quickly - five years of famine remained. Pharaoh generously provided wagons for the move. Joseph gave them provisions and changes of clothes; Benjamin received 300 pieces of silver and five changes of clothes. His parting words: "Do not quarrel on the way."

Jacob's response to the news moved from shock ("his heart became numb") to revival when he saw the wagons. "It is enough; Joseph my son is still alive. I will go and see him before I die."

Chapter 46: Jacob Moves to Egypt
----------------------------------------
At Beersheba, Jacob offered sacrifices. God spoke in night visions: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for I will make you a great nation there. I will go down with you... and I will surely bring you up again."

The genealogy lists 66 persons from Jacob's loins entering Egypt; with Joseph, his sons, and the wives, 70 total. This small group would become the nation that later left Egypt.

Joseph met Jacob in Goshen. "Joseph presented himself to him and fell on his neck and wept on his neck a good while." Jacob's words: "Now let me die, since I have seen your face and know that you are still alive."

Joseph coached his brothers: tell Pharaoh you are shepherds, to secure Goshen - good pastureland, and separate from Egyptians who despised shepherds. This separation would preserve Israel's distinct identity.

Chapter 47: Israel Settles in Goshen
----------------------------------------
Joseph presented five brothers to Pharaoh, who graciously offered them Goshen and employment over royal livestock. Then Jacob blessed Pharaoh. The old pilgrim ("few and evil have been the days of my life") blessing the world's greatest king - spiritual dignity surpassing political power.

Joseph's management of the famine displayed wisdom. As money ran out, he accepted livestock for grain. When livestock was exhausted, people offered themselves and their land. Egypt became Pharaoh's property, the people his tenants paying 20% tax. Only the priests retained their land.

Israel flourished in Goshen - multiplying greatly, seventeen years of peace. At 147 years, Jacob summoned Joseph: "Do not bury me in Egypt." Joseph swore to bury him in Canaan with his fathers. Jacob, satisfied, worshipped at the head of his bed.

Chapter 48: Jacob Blesses Ephraim and Manasseh
----------------------------------------
Joseph brought his sons to dying Jacob. Strengthening himself to sit up, Jacob recounted God's appearance at Luz (Bethel) and the promises received. He formally adopted Ephraim and Manasseh as his own sons, equal to Reuben and Simeon - giving Joseph a double portion.

He mentioned Rachel's death ("she died on the way... when there was still some distance to Ephrath") - still grieving after decades. This explained why Rachel's descendants received special treatment.

Though nearly blind, Jacob blessed the boys. Joseph positioned Manasseh (firstborn) at Jacob's right hand. But Jacob crossed his arms, placing his right hand on Ephraim's head. Joseph objected; Jacob persisted: "I know, my son, I know." Ephraim would become greater.

The blessing: "The angel who has redeemed me from all evil, bless the boys." Jacob's legacy to Joseph included a portion "above your brothers" - the land he had taken from the Amorites.

Chapter 49: Jacob's Final Prophecy
----------------------------------------
Jacob gathered all twelve sons for prophetic blessing, revealing "what shall happen in the last days." Each son's blessing reflected both character and future tribal destiny:

Reuben: firstborn dignity forfeited through instability and incest. Simeon and Levi: scattered for their violent anger at Shechem. Judah: praised by brothers, lion-like, holding the scepter until Shiloh comes (the Messiah), to whom the obedience of peoples belongs. Zebulun: at the seashore, a haven for ships. Issachar: strong donkey, accepting servitude for pleasant land.

Dan: judging his people but also a serpent. Gad: raided but raiding. Asher: rich food, providing royal delicacies. Naphtali: a doe set free, giving beautiful words. Joseph: fruitful vine, attacked but arms made strong by the Mighty One of Jacob - blessings of heaven, deep, breast, and womb.

Benjamin: ravenous wolf, devouring prey.

Jacob charged them again regarding burial in Machpelah with Abraham, Isaac, Sarah, Rebekah, and Leah. He drew up his feet, breathed his last, and was gathered to his people.

Chapter 50: Jacob's Burial and Joseph's Death
----------------------------------------
Joseph wept over his father and ordered embalming - forty days of preparation, seventy days of mourning. Pharaoh granted permission for burial in Canaan. The funeral procession was immense: Egyptian officials, chariots, horsemen. At the threshing floor of Atad, they mourned seven more days.

Jacob was buried in Machpelah as he had commanded. The brothers feared Joseph would now take revenge. They sent message (perhaps fabricated) that Jacob had commanded Joseph to forgive them. Joseph wept at their fear and again spoke providential theology: "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring about this present result, to preserve many people alive."

Joseph promised to provide for them and their children, speaking kindly. He lived 110 years, seeing Ephraim's grandchildren and Manasseh's great-grandchildren. Near death, he charged them: "God will surely visit you and bring you up out of this land to the land he swore to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." He made them swear to carry his bones when they left.

Genesis ends with Joseph embalmed, in a coffin in Egypt - awaiting the exodus, awaiting the promised land. Henry notes: the book that began with creation's light ends with death's darkness, showing what sin has done.
